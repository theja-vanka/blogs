<section id="a-visual-guide-to-gemma-4-12b" class="level1">

<p><img src="/blogs/posts/news/gemma/gemma.png" class="img-fluid"></p>
<p><em>A long-form guide summarizing and expanding on Maarten Grootendorst’s newsletter post, <a href="https://newsletter.maartengrootendorst.com/p/a-visual-guide-to-gemma-4-12b">“A Visual Guide to Gemma 4 12B”</a> (Exploring Language Models, published June 3, 2026).</em></p>
<section id="introduction-filling-the-gap-between-e4b-and-26b-a4b" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="introduction-filling-the-gap-between-e4b-and-26b-a4b" id="introduction-filling-the-gap-between-e4b-and-26b-a4b">Introduction: Filling the Gap Between E4B and 26B A4B</h2>
<p>Google DeepMind’s Gemma 4 family originally shipped with a noticeable size gap: the E4B model on one end, and the much larger 26B A4B Mixture-of-Experts model on the other. Gemma 4 12B was released to sit neatly in that gap, targeting setups with roughly 12 to 16 GB of VRAM — a sweet spot for a lot of local and prosumer hardware.</p>
<p>But DeepMind didn’t just resize an existing architecture and call it a day. Gemma 4 12B ships with a genuinely different design choice: it is <strong>encoder-free</strong>.</p>
<p>That phrase can be confusing at first, since most people already think of modern generative LLMs as “decoder-only” architectures. The nuance is specifically about <em>multimodal</em> input. Gemma 4 12B still understands images and audio — it just no longer relies on separate, dedicated encoder networks to process those inputs before handing them to the language model. Instead, the encoders for vision and audio have been stripped out entirely, and the burden of interpreting raw pixels and raw audio waveforms is now placed directly on the LLM itself.</p>
<p>This is a substantial architectural bet. The obvious benefit is speed: without a separate encoder that first needs to fully process an image or audio clip, the LLM can start consuming and reasoning about multimodal tokens almost immediately, rather than waiting on an upstream network to finish its pass. The less obvious cost is that the LLM now has to learn, through training, how to make sense of much rawer, less pre-digested input than before.</p>
<p>This guide walks through what that trade-off actually looks like under the hood: how multimodal LLMs are conventionally built with encoders and connectors, how Gemma 4’s existing vision and audio encoders work, and exactly what DeepMind replaced them with to make Gemma 4 12B encoder-free.</p>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="part-1-how-llms-normally-handle-non-text-modalities" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="part-1-how-llms-normally-handle-non-text-modalities" id="part-1-how-llms-normally-handle-non-text-modalities">Part 1: How LLMs Normally Handle Non-Text Modalities</h2>
<p>Before getting into what makes Gemma 4 12B special, it helps to review how multimodal LLMs typically work in the first place.</p>
<section id="text-is-native-to-the-llm" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="text-is-native-to-the-llm" id="text-is-native-to-the-llm">Text is Native to the LLM</h3>
<p>When you feed text into a language model, the model itself does all the work. Its token embedding layer splits the text into tokens and converts each one into a vector embedding. Those embeddings then flow into the decoder stack, where self-attention layers build up increasingly contextual, meaningful representations of each token relative to the others. Nothing outside the LLM is involved — text is, by definition, the LLM’s native language.</p>
</section>
<section id="other-modalities-usually-need-outside-help" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="other-modalities-usually-need-outside-help" id="other-modalities-usually-need-outside-help">Other Modalities Usually Need Outside Help</h3>
<p>Images and audio are a different story. In most multimodal LLMs, the model itself doesn’t touch raw pixels or raw waveforms directly. Instead, a separate <strong>encoder</strong> — typically a smaller Transformer model that also relies on attention — is used to pre-process the input into a set of embeddings before the LLM ever sees it.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!WT2m!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6e8f2eae-611f-40a0-97f4-5f862da7ca36_3810x1344.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram illustrating how a separate transformer encoder processes non-text modalities like images or audio into embeddings before they reach the LLM decoder.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p><em>The general pattern: a dedicated encoder processes non-text input using its own attention layers, separate from the LLM’s decoder.</em></p>
</section>
<section id="the-connector-bridging-two-different-embedding-spaces" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-connector-bridging-two-different-embedding-spaces" id="the-connector-bridging-two-different-embedding-spaces">The Connector: Bridging Two Different Embedding Spaces</h3>
<p>There’s a catch, though. The embeddings produced by an image or audio encoder don’t automatically fit into the LLM’s world. They typically have different dimensionality, and they occupy a differently-shaped vector space than the LLM’s own token embeddings — which makes sense, since a <em>Large Language Model</em> is, first and foremost, trained on text.</p>
<p>To reconcile this mismatch, multimodal architectures insert a small <strong>connector</strong> module — usually just a linear projection layer — that maps the encoder’s output into the same dimensional space as the LLM’s token embeddings.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!P0zW!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F791201e6-51e5-4358-b9f0-3c0fb4905341_4128x2610.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram of a connector module, a linear projection layer that maps encoder output embeddings into the same dimensional space as the LLM’s token embeddings.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p><em>The connector’s job: take encoder output and reshape it so it looks, dimensionally, like a token embedding the LLM already knows how to work with.</em></p>
<p>Together, an <strong>encoder + connector</strong> pair is the standard recipe used by many open multimodal models today, including the earlier Gemma 4 variants and models like Qwen 3.5. It works well — but it isn’t free. Two costs stand out:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong>Latency.</strong> The non-text encoders have to fully process their inputs before the LLM can even begin working with them, adding a sequential processing step to every multimodal request.</li>
<li><strong>Parameter overhead.</strong> These encoders aren’t trivial in size — they add a meaningful number of parameters to the overall model, and because they’re usually trained (or at least fine-tuned) somewhat separately from the LLM, they also add complexity whenever you want to fine-tune or scale the model further.</li>
</ol>
<p>This naturally raises the question DeepMind seems to have asked themselves: <em>what if you didn’t need the encoder at all? What if the connector alone were enough?</em></p>
<p>To understand how they answered that question, it’s worth first looking at how the <em>existing</em> Gemma 4 encoders work.</p>
<hr>
</section>
</section>
<section id="part-2-the-modalities-of-gemma-4" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="part-2-the-modalities-of-gemma-4" id="part-2-the-modalities-of-gemma-4">Part 2: The Modalities of Gemma 4</h2>
<p>Gemma 4 supports three modalities, though not uniformly across every model size:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Text input</strong> — supported by all Gemma 4 models.</li>
<li><strong>Image input</strong> — supported by all Gemma 4 models.</li>
<li><strong>Audio input</strong> — supported only by the smaller E2B and E4B models.</li>
</ul>
<p>Each non-text modality historically relied on its own dedicated Transformer-style encoder with attention, producing tokens the LLM could consume.</p>
<section id="the-vision-encoder" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-vision-encoder" id="the-vision-encoder">The Vision Encoder</h3>
<p>Every Gemma 4 model with a vision encoder — E2B, E4B, 26B A4B, and 31B — uses one to process input images, though the encoder’s size scales with the model: E2B and E4B use a vision encoder with roughly <strong>150 million parameters</strong>, while the larger 26B A4B and 31B models use a bigger vision encoder with about <strong>550 million parameters</strong>.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4k8m!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2965d9a1-8eea-41b6-9242-22376ec34080_4408x3928.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram showing the vision encoder architecture used across Gemma 4 models, scaled for model size (150M parameters for E2B/E4B, 550M for 26B A4B/31B).</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>Mechanically, an input image is broken into small patches of 16×16 pixels. After the vision encoder processes these patches, they’re pooled together into a 3×3 grid of patches.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9Jtg!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F645ed06e-253c-4b23-ade0-522500d47823_2904x1672.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram showing 16x16 pixel patches being processed and then pooled together in a 3x3 grid.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>Because pooling groups 3×3 blocks of the original 16×16 patches together, the resulting patch embeddings effectively represent 48×48-pixel regions of the original image. After this pooling step, a small linear projection layer — the connector — transforms these pooled patch embeddings so they match the shape of the LLM’s own token embeddings. The resulting vision tokens are then interleaved directly with the text token embeddings before being handed to the LLM.</p>
</section>
<section id="the-audio-encoder" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-audio-encoder" id="the-audio-encoder">The Audio Encoder</h3>
<p>The smaller Gemma 4 models — E2B and E4B — also ship with an audio encoder to handle spoken or other audio input. Both models share the same audio encoder, which has about <strong>305 million parameters</strong>.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!q0xz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4ff43215-5a63-4c0a-85de-87167c0b9306_5510x4910.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram of the audio encoder architecture shared by Gemma 4 E2B and E4B, with roughly 305 million parameters.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>Just like the vision encoder, once the audio encoder finishes processing an audio clip, its output embeddings are projected into the same dimensional space as the LLM’s token embeddings, and the resulting audio tokens are interleaved with the surrounding text tokens.</p>
<p>While 305 million parameters is relatively modest next to the size of the full LLM, these encoders still add real cost. They increase inference latency because the model has to wait for them to finish, and they complicate fine-tuning: when you fine-tune a Gemma 4 model, you typically only touch the LLM weights, not the encoder weights, which makes it harder to keep the encoders’ capabilities growing in lockstep with the rest of the model.</p>
<p>That tension — real capability, but real cost — is exactly what motivated Gemma 4 12B’s design.</p>
<hr>
</section>
</section>
<section id="part-3-making-gemma-4-12b-encoder-free" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="part-3-making-gemma-4-12b-encoder-free" id="part-3-making-gemma-4-12b-encoder-free">Part 3: Making Gemma 4 12B Encoder-Free</h2>
<p>At its core, Gemma 4 12B’s underlying LLM architecture closely resembles the dense 31B model. It uses the same decoder pattern of interleaved local and global attention layers, structured so that a global attention layer always comes last in the sequence.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!h2lV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F12384610-b895-46de-b5f3-4765cf1c87f6_3000x4488.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram comparing the decoder architecture of Gemma 4 12B to the dense 31B model, showing interleaved local and global attention layers.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>What sets Gemma 4 12B apart isn’t really its size — it’s the fact that both the vision encoder and the audio encoder have been removed entirely and replaced with something far lighter.</p>
<section id="replacing-the-vision-encoder" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="replacing-the-vision-encoder" id="replacing-the-vision-encoder">Replacing the Vision Encoder</h3>
<p>Instead of a full vision Transformer with 15 or 27 attention layers extracting and refining visual features, Gemma 4 12B uses a lightweight <strong>embedding module</strong> containing just a single layer.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2N-X!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffed0644d-ed62-4652-957e-9911c5dfa7d6_3424x3840.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram of the lightweight single-layer embedding module that replaces the full vision encoder in Gemma 4 12B.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>Rather than starting from small 16×16 patches and pooling them up as the encoder-based models do, Gemma 4 12B works directly with 48×48 patches from the outset. This might sound like it should lose detail compared to a “real” vision encoder, but since the embedder has no attention layers, it was never going to produce the same kind of semantically rich embeddings a full encoder would anyway — so skipping the extra pooling step doesn’t cost much in practice.</p>
<p><strong>The positional embedding problem.</strong> One wrinkle with an attention-free embedder is that it needs some way to tell the LLM <em>where</em> each patch sits within the original image. The encoder-based models solve this with 2D-positional RoPE inside the attention layers — but that mechanism doesn’t exist here, since there’s no attention. The LLM’s own positional encoding doesn’t help either, since it’s designed to treat inputs as a flat, one-dimensional sequence of tokens, not a 2D grid.</p>
<p>DeepMind’s solution is to inject spatial position information directly into each vision token’s embedding <em>before</em> it reaches the LLM, using two dedicated matrices — one for x-coordinates, one for y-coordinates.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!nQ5-!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0347cf29-5a49-4984-b3b8-d85412a324d8_1668x1296.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram of the two positional embedding matrices representing x and y coordinates, each sized 1120 by 3840.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>Each of these matrices is sized 1,120 (the maximum number of patches an image can be split into) by 3,840 (matching the model’s hidden dimension). That maximum-patches figure of 1,120 mirrors the token “budget” options already available in the encoder-based Gemma 4 models, which let users choose how finely an image gets split — 70, 140, 280, 560, or 1,120 patches, with more patches giving a more fine-grained view of the image.</p>
<p>For any given patch, the model looks up its x-coordinate’s row in the x-matrix and its y-coordinate’s row in the y-matrix, then simply adds those two vectors together to produce that patch’s positional embedding. For example, a patch at position x=2, y=1 gets the embedding formed by summing row 2 of the x-matrix and row 1 of the y-matrix.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1n69!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa8ef1fbc-57a4-4868-8b7c-845f75b3d063_2400x2896.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram illustrating how a patch’s x and y positional embeddings are looked up and summed together, using the example of a patch at position x=2, y=1.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>After adding this positional information to each vision token embedding, a final LayerNorm is applied for training stability, and the embeddings are then projected into the exact dimensionality Gemma 4 12B expects.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GDnt!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe1f8eeca-54f0-47a0-b55c-f41279df7d3f_4656x3216.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram showing the final LayerNorm step and projection to Gemma 4 12B’s expected dimensionality.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p><strong>Where did 35 million parameters come from?</strong> Given that this whole module replaces a 550-million-parameter vision encoder, its own ~35 million parameters might seem surprisingly large for something described as just “an embedding step.” The answer is almost entirely about raw pixel projection, not learned attention or feed-forward capacity. Each 48×48 patch has 48 × 48 × 3 = 6,912 raw pixel values (three color channels), which need to be projected into Gemma 4 12B’s 3,840-dimensional space. That single projection — sized 6,912 × 3,840, plus a 3,840-dimensional bias term — already accounts for roughly 26 million parameters on its own. In other words, the embedder isn’t secretly hiding 35 million parameters’ worth of attention or reasoning capacity; it’s mostly just the unavoidable cost of mapping a lot of raw pixels into the model’s embedding space.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ABoi!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F47f6586a-6c03-4913-82b1-19df88f6f835_3280x3872.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram showing the pixel-to-dimension projection math: 48x48x3 = 6,912 pixels projected onto 3,840 dimensions, accounting for about 26 million of the embedder’s parameters.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>Because the embedder has no attention mechanism, it processes each patch entirely in isolation — there’s no cross-patch context built at this stage at all. All of that relational reasoning between patches is now the LLM’s job, handled through its own attention layers once the vision tokens arrive.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!RRuZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc1acf330-a0f3-4e60-9ec1-892a918900c5_4656x4864.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Side-by-side comparison diagram showing the large 550-million-parameter vision encoder used in Gemma 4 31B versus the minimal ~35-million-parameter embedding module used in Gemma 4 12B.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>The payoff for this simplification is speed: since vision tokens no longer need to wait on a full Transformer encoder to finish processing, they reach the LLM much faster, letting the model start reasoning about — and generating output from — image input sooner.</p>
</section>
<section id="replacing-the-audio-encoder" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="replacing-the-audio-encoder" id="replacing-the-audio-encoder">Replacing the Audio Encoder</h3>
<p>If replacing the vision encoder required some clever positional-embedding engineering, replacing the audio encoder turned out to be even more straightforward.</p>
<p>Instead of extracting processed audio features the way a real encoder would, Gemma 4 12B works directly with raw audio input — much like it works directly with raw pixels for images. Audio has one convenient property that vision doesn’t: since it’s inherently a sequential (effectively one-dimensional, or “2D” in the sense of amplitude-over-time) signal already, it doesn’t need any extra positional embedding scheme the way image patches do. It can be treated similarly to how the model already treats ordinary text sequences.</p>
<p>Concretely, the raw audio input is split into 40-millisecond windows. Since audio is sampled at 16,000 samples per second (16 kHz), each 40ms window contains 640 raw amplitude values — numbers representing how high or low the sound wave is at each instant.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!X2Fc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F53862529-7a89-45f2-b59c-c83b26b21774_4680x2016.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram illustrating how raw audio is split into 40-millisecond windows, each containing 640 raw amplitude samples from 16kHz audio.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>These 640 raw values per window are then passed straight through a linear projection layer, mapping them into Gemma 4 12B’s expected embedding dimensionality.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!D6Ij!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa5c58dee-0600-49af-b080-6b08fce52d9b_2340x1376.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Diagram of the linear projection layer that maps 640 raw audio amplitude values directly into Gemma 4 12B’s embedding dimensionality.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>That’s genuinely the entire pipeline: split the raw waveform into fixed windows, project each window’s raw samples into the model’s embedding space, and hand the result to the LLM. There’s no dedicated tokenization step and no stack of Transformer decoder layers processing the audio beforehand, unlike in Gemma 4 E4B, which still needs to tokenize audio and run it through several stacked decoder layers before the LLM ever touches it.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5OvL!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffba73d61-baca-4a32-9f48-6116b4bdbfa3_4656x4864.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Side-by-side comparison diagram of audio token creation in Gemma 4 12B (split-and-project only) versus Gemma 4 E4B (tokenize plus stacked decoder processing).</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>

<section id="why-this-matters" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="why-this-matters" id="why-this-matters">Why This Matters</h2>
<p>Gemma 4 12B isn’t just a model sized to fill a gap between E4B and 26B A4B — it represents a genuinely different architectural bet about where multimodal understanding should happen. Rather than delegating vision and audio understanding to specialized, separately-trained encoder networks, Gemma 4 12B pushes nearly all of that responsibility onto the LLM itself, keeping only the bare minimum pre-processing (raw pixel/audio projection, plus spatial positional information for images) outside the main decoder stack.</p>
<p>The tradeoffs are clear on paper: dramatically fewer “encoder” parameters (roughly 35 million vs.&nbsp;550 million for vision, and no dedicated audio encoder at all), lower latency getting multimodal tokens in front of the LLM, and a simpler, more unified fine-tuning story since there’s no separate encoder to keep in sync with the LLM. The cost is that the LLM has to learn, purely through training, to do the interpretive work that a dedicated encoder used to handle — there’s no free lunch, just a different place to pay the bill.</p>
<p>Whether this approach becomes the new default for open multimodal models, or remains a Gemma-specific experiment, likely depends on how it holds up on real-world multimodal benchmarks compared to the encoder-based models — a comparison the original newsletter post doesn’t include, and one commenters on the piece specifically flagged as missing.</p>
