<section id="introduction-and-motivation" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="introduction-and-motivation" id="introduction-and-motivation">1. Introduction and Motivation</h2>
<p>Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) is PyTorch’s mechanism for running selected parts of a model in lower-precision floating point formats (<code>float16</code> or <code>bfloat16</code>) while keeping numerically sensitive operations in <code>float32</code>. The goal is to exploit the throughput advantages of reduced-precision matrix multiplication and convolution units on modern accelerators (Tensor Cores on NVIDIA GPUs, matrix units on AMD MI-series and Google TPUs) without the accuracy degradation that naive full-fp16 training causes.</p>
<p>The value proposition rests on three pillars:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Compute throughput</strong>: Tensor Cores process fp16/bf16 matmuls at 2-8x the FLOP rate of fp32 on comparable hardware generations.</li>
<li><strong>Memory bandwidth and footprint</strong>: Half-precision tensors halve activation and gradient memory, which increases achievable batch size and reduces the time spent moving data through the memory hierarchy — often the actual bottleneck in modern training, not compute.</li>
<li><strong>Numerical safety net</strong>: Unlike manually casting a whole model to <code>.half()</code>, AMP maintains a curated op-level casting policy plus (for fp16) dynamic loss scaling, so accuracy typically matches fp32 baselines within noise.</li>
</ul>
<p>AMP is not free lunch. It introduces a second axis of complexity in the training loop (scaler state, autocast region boundaries, dtype promotion edge cases) and interacts non-trivially with distributed training, custom autograd functions, and certain loss functions. This guide assumes you already know how to write a standard PyTorch training loop and focuses on the mechanics, internals, and failure modes of AMP rather than re-deriving the basics of neural network training.</p>
</section>

<td><code>tf32</code> (TensorFloat-32)</td>
<td>1</td>
<td>8</td>
<td>10</td>
<td>Same as fp32</td>
<td>Between fp16 and fp32</td>
<td>Implicit fp32 matmul acceleration on Ampere+</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p>The critical distinction driving AMP’s design is that <strong>fp16 trades exponent bits for mantissa bits relative to fp32</strong>, so it has <em>less range</em> than fp32 (max ~65504, underflows below ~6e-8 in denormal range). Gradients in deep networks routinely fall below fp16’s representable range, especially for small loss values or deep layers — hence the need for <strong>loss scaling</strong>. <code>bfloat16</code>, by contrast, keeps the same 8-bit exponent as fp32 (same dynamic range) but sacrifices mantissa precision, so it does not suffer the underflow problem and does not require a gradient scaler. This is why bf16 AMP code paths in PyTorch skip <code>GradScaler</code> entirely.</p>
<p><code>tf32</code> is a separate, orthogonal mechanism: on Ampere and later NVIDIA GPUs, fp32 matmuls can be silently accelerated by truncating the mantissa to 10 bits internally, controlled via <code>torch.backends.cuda.matmul.allow_tf32</code> and <code>torch.backends.cudnn.allow_tf32</code>. It is not part of <code>torch.autocast</code>’s casting policy but often used alongside AMP for the residual fp32 ops.</p>
</section>
<section id="how-autocast-works-internally" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="how-autocast-works-internally" id="how-autocast-works-internally">3. How <code>autocast</code> Works Internally</h2>
<p><code>torch.autocast</code> (formerly <code>torch.cuda.amp.autocast</code>, now generalized to <code>torch.amp.autocast(device_type=...)</code>) is a context manager / decorator that intercepts dispatch for a curated list of ops and casts their inputs to a target dtype before execution. It does <strong>not</strong> cast your model’s parameters — weights stay in fp32 (or whatever dtype you created them in); only the <em>activations flowing through eligible ops</em> are cast on the fly, op by op, at dispatch time.</p>
<section id="op-categorization" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="op-categorization" id="op-categorization">3.1 Op categorization</h3>
<p>Internally, PyTorch maintains three categories of ops relevant to autocast:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong>Allowlist (cast to lower precision)</strong>: ops that benefit from Tensor Cores and are numerically robust to reduced precision — <code>matmul</code>, <code>mm</code>, <code>bmm</code>, <code>addmm</code>, <code>conv1d/2d/3d</code>, <code>linear</code>, and similar. These execute in fp16/bf16.</li>
<li><strong>Denylist (kept in fp32)</strong>: numerically sensitive reductions and functions — <code>softmax</code>, <code>log_softmax</code>, <code>cross_entropy</code>, <code>nll_loss</code>, <code>layer_norm</code>, <code>batch_norm</code> (in training mode), <code>pow</code>, <code>exp</code>, <code>mse_loss</code>, and other ops prone to overflow or requiring high dynamic range. These are forced to fp32 regardless of the ambient autocast dtype.</li>
<li><strong>Widest-input / promote rules</strong>: ops like <code>cat</code>, <code>stack</code>, <code>add</code> that combine multiple tensors run in the widest dtype among their inputs, so mixing an fp16 tensor with an fp32 tensor promotes the result to fp32, preventing silent precision loss on concatenation-like ops.</li>
</ol>
<p>This policy is defined in PyTorch’s <code>aten/src/ATen/autocast_mode.cpp</code> and is not user-configurable per op out of the box, though you can force specific regions to a chosen dtype or exit autocast manually (Section 3.3).</p>
</section>

This is the standard pattern for locally opting an operation out of autocast (as shown above) without disabling it for the whole forward pass.</p>

<section id="gradscaler-dynamic-loss-scaling-mechanics" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="gradscaler-dynamic-loss-scaling-mechanics" id="gradscaler-dynamic-loss-scaling-mechanics">4. <code>GradScaler</code>: Dynamic Loss Scaling Mechanics</h2>
<section id="why-scaling-is-necessary" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="why-scaling-is-necessary" id="why-scaling-is-necessary">4.1 Why scaling is necessary</h3>
<p>In fp16, gradients with magnitude below ~6e-8 (or below the representable range once you account for the format’s granularity near zero) flush to zero. Empirically, a large fraction of gradient values in deep networks — especially in later training stages or deep layers — fall into this dead zone. <code>GradScaler</code> multiplies the loss by a scale factor <code>S</code> before backpropagation, so gradients are shifted up into fp16’s representable range by the same multiplicative factor (since gradients are linear in the loss via the chain rule). Before the optimizer step, gradients are divided by <code>S</code> to recover their true magnitude.</p>
</section>
<section id="the-scaling-algorithm" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-scaling-algorithm" id="the-scaling-algorithm">4.2 The scaling algorithm</h3>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb4"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb4-1"><a href="#cb4-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler <span class="op">=</span> torch.amp.GradScaler(device<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>)</span>
<span id="cb4-2"><a href="#cb4-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb4-3"><a href="#cb4-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="cf">for</span> inputs, targets <span class="kw">in</span> dataloader:</span>
<span id="cb4-4"><a href="#cb4-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    optimizer.zero_grad(set_to_none<span class="op">=</span><span class="va">True</span>)</span>
<span id="cb4-5"><a href="#cb4-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="cf">with</span> torch.autocast(device_type<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>, dtype<span class="op">=</span>torch.float16):</span>
<span id="cb4-6"><a href="#cb4-6" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        outputs <span class="op">=</span> model(inputs)</span>
<span id="cb4-7"><a href="#cb4-7" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        loss <span class="op">=</span> loss_fn(outputs, targets)</span>
<span id="cb4-8"><a href="#cb4-8" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb4-9"><a href="#cb4-9" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.scale(loss).backward()      <span class="co"># loss * S, then backward</span></span>
<span id="cb4-10"><a href="#cb4-10" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.step(optimizer)             <span class="co"># unscale grads, check for inf/nan, conditionally step</span></span>
<span id="cb4-11"><a href="#cb4-11" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.update()                    <span class="co"># adjust S for next iteration</span></span></code></pre></div></div>
<p>Under the hood, each call does the following:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong><code>scaler.scale(loss)</code></strong>: returns <code>loss * S</code>, where <code>S</code> starts at <code>init_scale</code> (default <code>2**16</code>).</li>
<li><strong><code>.backward()</code></strong>: backpropagates the scaled loss; every gradient in <code>.grad</code> is implicitly scaled by <code>S</code> due to linearity.</li>
<li><strong><code>scaler.step(optimizer)</code></strong>:
<ul>
<li>Internally calls <code>scaler.unscale_(optimizer)</code> if you haven’t already called it manually (divides all gradients by <code>S</code> in place).</li>
<li>Inspects the unscaled gradients for <code>inf</code>/<code>nan</code> values (a cheap <code>torch.isfinite</code>-style reduction across all parameter grads registered with that optimizer).</li>
<li>If any non-finite values are found, the optimizer step is <strong>skipped entirely</strong> for this iteration (the corrupted update is discarded), and this is reported to <code>update()</code>.</li>
<li>Otherwise, <code>optimizer.step()</code> proceeds normally with the correctly-scaled gradients.</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong><code>scaler.update()</code></strong>:
<ul>
<li>If the last step was skipped (inf/nan detected), <code>S</code> is multiplied by <code>backoff_factor</code> (default <code>0.5</code>) — scale down, since we overflowed.</li>
<li>If <code>growth_interval</code> (default <code>2000</code>) consecutive steps have passed without an overflow, <code>S</code> is multiplied by <code>growth_factor</code> (default <code>2.0</code>) — cautiously scale up to use more of fp16’s range.</li>
</ul></li>
</ol>
<p>This is a <strong>dynamic</strong> scheme: it hunts for the largest scale factor that doesn’t cause overflow, adapting automatically over the course of training without user tuning in the common case.</p>
</section>
<section id="constructor-parameters" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="constructor-parameters" id="constructor-parameters">4.3 Constructor parameters</h3>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb5"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb5-1"><a href="#cb5-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler <span class="op">=</span> torch.amp.GradScaler(</span>
<span id="cb5-2"><a href="#cb5-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    device<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>,</span>
<span id="cb5-3"><a href="#cb5-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    init_scale<span class="op">=</span><span class="fl">2.0</span><span class="op">**</span><span class="dv">16</span>,</span>
<span id="cb5-4"><a href="#cb5-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    growth_factor<span class="op">=</span><span class="fl">2.0</span>,</span>
<span id="cb5-5"><a href="#cb5-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    backoff_factor<span class="op">=</span><span class="fl">0.5</span>,</span>
<span id="cb5-6"><a href="#cb5-6" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    growth_interval<span class="op">=</span><span class="dv">2000</span>,</span>
<span id="cb5-7"><a href="#cb5-7" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    enabled<span class="op">=</span><span class="va">True</span>,</span>
<span id="cb5-8"><a href="#cb5-8" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>)</span></code></pre></div></div>
<ul>
<li><strong><code>init_scale</code></strong>: Start conservatively high; the algorithm will back off quickly if it’s too aggressive, so there’s little cost to starting high, but starting <em>too</em> low wastes early-training gradient signal.</li>
<li><strong><code>growth_interval</code></strong>: Lower values adapt scale faster but risk oscillating around the overflow boundary, wasting steps. 2000 is a reasonable default for most vision/NLP workloads; for very unstable models (some RL, GANs), consider tightening this or reducing <code>growth_factor</code>.</li>
<li><strong><code>enabled=False</code></strong>: Lets you keep an identical code path for bf16 or fp32 runs — <code>scaler.scale(loss)</code> becomes a no-op passthrough, <code>step()</code> calls <code>optimizer.step()</code> directly, and <code>update()</code> does nothing. This is the idiomatic way to write dtype-agnostic training loops (see Section 9.1).</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="gradient-clipping-under-amp" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="gradient-clipping-under-amp" id="gradient-clipping-under-amp">4.4 Gradient clipping under AMP</h3>
<p>A common correctness bug: calling <code>torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_</code> directly on <code>.grad</code> tensors that are still in <em>scaled</em> space clips against the wrong magnitude (scaled by <code>S</code>, not the true gradient norm). You must explicitly unscale first:</p>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb6"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb6-1"><a href="#cb6-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler.scale(loss).backward()</span>
<span id="cb6-2"><a href="#cb6-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler.unscale_(optimizer)                          <span class="co"># bring grads back to true scale</span></span>
<span id="cb6-3"><a href="#cb6-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm<span class="op">=</span><span class="fl">1.0</span>)</span>
<span id="cb6-4"><a href="#cb6-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler.step(optimizer)                               <span class="co"># will NOT re-unscale, since unscale_() already ran</span></span>
<span id="cb6-5"><a href="#cb6-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler.update()</span></code></pre></div></div>
<p><code>GradScaler</code> tracks, per optimizer, whether <code>unscale_()</code> has already been called this iteration and will not double-unscale. Calling <code>unscale_()</code> more than once per <code>step()</code> for the same optimizer without an intervening <code>update()</code> raises a <code>RuntimeError</code>.</p>
</section>
<section id="gradient-accumulation-under-amp" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="gradient-accumulation-under-amp" id="gradient-accumulation-under-amp">4.5 Gradient accumulation under AMP</h3>
<p>When accumulating gradients over multiple micro-batches before stepping, scale and accumulate every micro-batch, but only call <code>step()</code>/<code>update()</code> on the batch boundary:</p>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb7"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb7-1"><a href="#cb7-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler <span class="op">=</span> torch.amp.GradScaler(device<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>)</span>
<span id="cb7-2"><a href="#cb7-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>accum_steps <span class="op">=</span> <span class="dv">4</span></span>
<span id="cb7-3"><a href="#cb7-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb7-4"><a href="#cb7-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="cf">for</span> i, (inputs, targets) <span class="kw">in</span> <span class="bu">enumerate</span>(dataloader):</span>
<span id="cb7-5"><a href="#cb7-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="cf">with</span> torch.autocast(device_type<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>, dtype<span class="op">=</span>torch.float16):</span>
<span id="cb7-6"><a href="#cb7-6" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        outputs <span class="op">=</span> model(inputs)</span>
<span id="cb7-7"><a href="#cb7-7" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        loss <span class="op">=</span> loss_fn(outputs, targets) <span class="op">/</span> accum_steps</span>
<span id="cb7-8"><a href="#cb7-8" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb7-9"><a href="#cb7-9" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.scale(loss).backward()</span>
<span id="cb7-10"><a href="#cb7-10" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb7-11"><a href="#cb7-11" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="cf">if</span> (i <span class="op">+</span> <span class="dv">1</span>) <span class="op">%</span> accum_steps <span class="op">==</span> <span class="dv">0</span>:</span>
<span id="cb7-12"><a href="#cb7-12" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        scaler.step(optimizer)</span>
<span id="cb7-13"><a href="#cb7-13" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        scaler.update()</span>
<span id="cb7-14"><a href="#cb7-14" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        optimizer.zero_grad(set_to_none<span class="op">=</span><span class="va">True</span>)</span></code></pre></div></div>
<p>Because gradients accumulate additively in <code>.grad</code> across the micro-batches and all of them were scaled by the <em>same</em> <code>S</code> (it only changes on <code>update()</code>), the accumulated gradient is consistently scaled and a single <code>unscale_</code>/<code>step</code> at the boundary is correct.</p>
</section>
<section id="multiple-models-optimizers-in-one-scaler" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="multiple-models-optimizers-in-one-scaler" id="multiple-models-optimizers-in-one-scaler">4.6 Multiple models / optimizers in one scaler</h3>
<p>A single <code>GradScaler</code> instance can drive multiple optimizers (e.g., separate generator/discriminator optimizers in a GAN), since it tracks per-optimizer state internally:</p>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb8"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb8-1"><a href="#cb8-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler <span class="op">=</span> torch.amp.GradScaler(device<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>)</span>
<span id="cb8-2"><a href="#cb8-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb8-3"><a href="#cb8-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler.scale(loss_d).backward()</span>
<span id="cb8-4"><a href="#cb8-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler.step(optimizer_d)</span>
<span id="cb8-5"><a href="#cb8-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb8-6"><a href="#cb8-6" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler.scale(loss_g).backward()</span>
<span id="cb8-7"><a href="#cb8-7" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler.step(optimizer_g)</span>
<span id="cb8-8"><a href="#cb8-8" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb8-9"><a href="#cb8-9" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler.update()   <span class="co"># one update call after all steps for this iteration</span></span></code></pre></div></div>
<p>Call <code>update()</code> once per iteration, after all <code>step()</code> calls, not after each one — otherwise the scale factor updates based on partial information and the shared <code>S</code> diverges between optimizers’ actual needs.</p>
</section>
</section>
<section id="custom-autograd-functions-and-op-eligibility" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="custom-autograd-functions-and-op-eligibility" id="custom-autograd-functions-and-op-eligibility">5. Custom Autograd Functions and Op Eligibility</h2>
<section id="the-problem" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-problem" id="the-problem">5.1 The problem</h3>
<p><code>autocast</code>’s dispatch-level interception works by patching registered ATen ops. A <code>torch.autograd.Function</code> subclass with a custom <code>forward</code>/<code>backward</code> is opaque to this mechanism by default — the ops inside it don’t automatically participate in the allowlist/denylist casting policy, and worse, autograd’s replay of forward-pass dtypes during backward doesn’t apply because the function is a single opaque node in the graph.</p>
</section>
<section id="making-custom-functions-autocast-compatible" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="making-custom-functions-autocast-compatible" id="making-custom-functions-autocast-compatible">5.2 Making custom <code>Function</code>s autocast-compatible</h3>
<p>PyTorch provides decorators to explicitly declare a custom function’s casting behavior:</p>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb9"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb9-1"><a href="#cb9-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="im">from</span> torch.amp <span class="im">import</span> custom_fwd, custom_bwd</span>
<span id="cb9-2"><a href="#cb9-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb9-3"><a href="#cb9-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="kw">class</span> MyCustomOp(torch.autograd.Function):</span>
<span id="cb9-4"><a href="#cb9-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="at">@staticmethod</span></span>
<span id="cb9-5"><a href="#cb9-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="at">@custom_fwd</span>(device_type<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>, cast_inputs<span class="op">=</span>torch.float16)</span>
<span id="cb9-6"><a href="#cb9-6" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="kw">def</span> forward(ctx, x, weight):</span>
<span id="cb9-7"><a href="#cb9-7" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        ctx.save_for_backward(x, weight)</span>
<span id="cb9-8"><a href="#cb9-8" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        <span class="cf">return</span> some_low_level_kernel(x, weight)</span>
<span id="cb9-9"><a href="#cb9-9" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb9-10"><a href="#cb9-10" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="at">@staticmethod</span></span>
<span id="cb9-11"><a href="#cb9-11" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="at">@custom_bwd</span>(device_type<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>)</span>
<span id="cb9-12"><a href="#cb9-12" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="kw">def</span> backward(ctx, grad_output):</span>
<span id="cb9-13"><a href="#cb9-13" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        x, weight <span class="op">=</span> ctx.saved_tensors</span>
<span id="cb9-14"><a href="#cb9-14" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        <span class="co"># grad_output arrives in the same dtype forward() computed in</span></span>
<span id="cb9-15"><a href="#cb9-15" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        <span class="cf">return</span> compute_grad_x(grad_output, weight), compute_grad_w(grad_output, x)</span></code></pre></div></div>
<ul>
<li><code>@custom_fwd(cast_inputs=torch.float16)</code> forces all floating-point inputs to <code>forward</code> to be cast to fp16 <em>before</em> the function executes, regardless of ambient autocast state, then disables further autocasting inside the function body (so internal ops run in the requested dtype deterministically).</li>
<li>If you omit <code>cast_inputs</code>, the function runs in whatever dtype its inputs happen to be — you’re opting out of automatic casting but the function will still execute correctly if the caller passes already-cast tensors.</li>
<li><code>@custom_bwd</code> ensures the backward pass replays with the same effectively-disabled-autocast semantics as the forward, keeping dtype behavior symmetric.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="registering-ops-with-autocasts-policy-advanced" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="registering-ops-with-autocasts-policy-advanced" id="registering-ops-with-autocasts-policy-advanced">5.3 Registering ops with autocast’s policy (advanced)</h3>
<p>If you’re authoring a C++/CUDA extension and want a custom op to participate in the same allowlist/promote/denylist machinery as built-in ops (rather than hand-casting in Python), PyTorch exposes <code>torch.library</code>-based registration hooks (<code>m.impl(..., c10::DispatchKey::Autocast...)</code> on the C++ side, or the <code>torch._C._nn</code> registration APIs) to attach an autocast rule to a custom dispatcher key. This is rarely necessary for typical model code — reach for <code>custom_fwd</code>/<code>custom_bwd</code> first — and is primarily relevant if you’re shipping a custom op library that should feel like a first-class autocast citizen for downstream users.</p>
</section>
</section>
<section id="amp-with-distributeddataparallel-ddp" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="amp-with-distributeddataparallel-ddp" id="amp-with-distributeddataparallel-ddp">6. AMP with <code>DistributedDataParallel</code> (DDP)</h2>
<section id="basic-pattern" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="basic-pattern" id="basic-pattern">6.1 Basic pattern</h3>
<p>AMP composes with DDP with essentially no special-casing: each process runs its own local autocast region and its own <code>GradScaler</code>. DDP’s gradient all-reduce happens on the (still-scaled) <code>.grad</code> tensors during <code>backward()</code>, which is safe because all-reduce is a linear (averaging) operation and every rank uses the same scale factor <code>S</code> for a given iteration — the averaged gradient is scaled by the same <code>S</code> as any individual rank’s, so unscaling downstream is still correct.</p>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb10"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb10-1"><a href="#cb10-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>model <span class="op">=</span> MyModel().to(local_rank)</span>
<span id="cb10-2"><a href="#cb10-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>model <span class="op">=</span> torch.nn.parallel.DistributedDataParallel(model, device_ids<span class="op">=</span>[local_rank])</span>
<span id="cb10-3"><a href="#cb10-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler <span class="op">=</span> torch.amp.GradScaler(device<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>)</span>
<span id="cb10-4"><a href="#cb10-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb10-5"><a href="#cb10-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="cf">for</span> inputs, targets <span class="kw">in</span> dataloader:</span>
<span id="cb10-6"><a href="#cb10-6" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    optimizer.zero_grad(set_to_none<span class="op">=</span><span class="va">True</span>)</span>
<span id="cb10-7"><a href="#cb10-7" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="cf">with</span> torch.autocast(device_type<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>, dtype<span class="op">=</span>torch.float16):</span>
<span id="cb10-8"><a href="#cb10-8" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        outputs <span class="op">=</span> model(inputs)</span>
<span id="cb10-9"><a href="#cb10-9" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        loss <span class="op">=</span> loss_fn(outputs, targets)</span>
<span id="cb10-10"><a href="#cb10-10" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb10-11"><a href="#cb10-11" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.scale(loss).backward()   <span class="co"># DDP all-reduces scaled grads during this call</span></span>
<span id="cb10-12"><a href="#cb10-12" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.step(optimizer)</span>
<span id="cb10-13"><a href="#cb10-13" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.update()</span></code></pre></div></div>
</section>
<section id="the-subtle-infnan-divergence-risk" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-subtle-infnan-divergence-risk" id="the-subtle-infnan-divergence-risk">6.2 The subtle inf/nan divergence risk</h3>
<p>Because <code>scaler.step()</code> decides <em>locally, per-rank</em> whether to skip the optimizer step based on that rank’s gradients being finite, and because <code>scaler.update()</code> adjusts <code>S</code> <em>locally</em> based on that rank’s skip/no-skip outcome, there’s a theoretical risk of <strong>scale factor divergence across ranks</strong> if different ranks observe different overflow behavior (e.g., due to non-deterministic ops or rank-specific data causing localized overflow). In practice, since DDP all-reduces gradients <em>before</em> the inf/nan check effectively sees the post-all-reduce values (the check happens on the same all-reduced <code>.grad</code> tensors every rank has after <code>backward()</code> returns), all ranks observe the <em>same</em> averaged gradient tensor and therefore the same finite/non-finite verdict, keeping <code>S</code> synchronized across ranks without extra effort. This only breaks down if you do something unusual like per-rank gradient modification between <code>backward()</code> and <code>step()</code>.</p>
</section>
<section id="find_unused_parameters-and-autocast-caching-interaction" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="find_unused_parameters-and-autocast-caching-interaction" id="find_unused_parameters-and-autocast-caching-interaction">6.3 <code>find_unused_parameters</code> and autocast caching interaction</h3>
<p>If your model has conditional branches (common in mixture-of-experts or dynamic architectures) combined with <code>cache_enabled=True</code> autocast weight caching, be aware that DDP’s bucketing and the autocast cache both add bookkeeping around parameter reuse; if you hit unexpected staleness or DDP “unused parameter” errors under AMP, temporarily disabling <code>cache_enabled</code> is a useful debugging step.</p>
</section>
</section>
<section id="amp-with-fullyshardeddataparallel-fsdp" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="amp-with-fullyshardeddataparallel-fsdp" id="amp-with-fullyshardeddataparallel-fsdp">7. AMP with <code>FullyShardedDataParallel</code> (FSDP)</h2>
<p>FSDP takes a materially different approach to mixed precision than DDP + <code>torch.autocast</code>, and it’s important not to conflate the two.</p>
<section id="fsdps-native-mixedprecision-policy" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="fsdps-native-mixedprecision-policy" id="fsdps-native-mixedprecision-policy">7.1 FSDP’s native <code>MixedPrecision</code> policy</h3>
<p>Rather than (or in addition to) wrapping the forward pass in <code>torch.autocast</code>, FSDP exposes a <code>MixedPrecision</code> config that controls the dtype of parameters, gradient reduction, and buffers directly at the sharding level:</p>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb11"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb11-1"><a href="#cb11-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="im">from</span> torch.distributed.fsdp <span class="im">import</span> FullyShardedDataParallel <span class="im">as</span> FSDP</span>
<span id="cb11-2"><a href="#cb11-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="im">from</span> torch.distributed.fsdp <span class="im">import</span> MixedPrecision</span>
<span id="cb11-3"><a href="#cb11-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb11-4"><a href="#cb11-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>mp_policy <span class="op">=</span> MixedPrecision(</span>
<span id="cb11-5"><a href="#cb11-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    param_dtype<span class="op">=</span>torch.bfloat16,     <span class="co"># dtype params are cast to for forward/backward compute</span></span>
<span id="cb11-6"><a href="#cb11-6" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    reduce_dtype<span class="op">=</span>torch.bfloat16,    <span class="co"># dtype used for gradient all-reduce/reduce-scatter</span></span>
<span id="cb11-7"><a href="#cb11-7" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    buffer_dtype<span class="op">=</span>torch.bfloat16,    <span class="co"># dtype for buffers (e.g., BatchNorm running stats)</span></span>
<span id="cb11-8"><a href="#cb11-8" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>)</span>
<span id="cb11-9"><a href="#cb11-9" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb11-10"><a href="#cb11-10" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>model <span class="op">=</span> FSDP(model, mixed_precision<span class="op">=</span>mp_policy, device_id<span class="op">=</span>local_rank)</span></code></pre></div></div>
<p>This casts the <strong>sharded parameters themselves</strong> to <code>param_dtype</code> for compute, communicates gradients in <code>reduce_dtype</code>, and keeps an fp32 master copy of parameters (by default) for the optimizer step — conceptually similar to what <code>autocast</code> + fp32 master weights achieves manually, but implemented natively in the sharding/communication path rather than at op dispatch time.</p>
</section>
<section id="combining-fsdp-with-torch.autocast" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="combining-fsdp-with-torch.autocast" id="combining-fsdp-with-torch.autocast">7.2 Combining FSDP with <code>torch.autocast</code></h3>
<p>You can still layer <code>torch.autocast</code> on top of FSDP’s <code>MixedPrecision</code> (or instead of it), but the two mechanisms address different things: FSDP’s policy governs what dtype <em>sharded parameters and gradient communication</em> use, while <code>autocast</code> governs what dtype <em>individual ops</em> execute in during the forward pass, independent of sharding. A common pattern for bf16 training on large models is to rely on FSDP’s <code>MixedPrecision</code> alone (bf16 needs no <code>GradScaler</code>), skipping <code>torch.autocast</code> entirely, since FSDP already ensures compute happens in the requested lower-precision dtype for the sharded modules.</p>
<p>For fp16 with FSDP, you still need loss scaling, but the standard <code>torch.amp.GradScaler</code> was originally written for non-sharded parameters. Since PyTorch 1.12+, <code>torch.distributed.fsdp.sharded_grad_scaler.ShardedGradScaler</code> provides an FSDP-aware equivalent that correctly handles the inf/nan check across sharded gradient shards (which live on different ranks and can’t be checked with a naive local reduction the way DDP’s fully-replicated gradients can):</p>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb12"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb12-1"><a href="#cb12-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="im">from</span> torch.distributed.fsdp.sharded_grad_scaler <span class="im">import</span> ShardedGradScaler</span>
<span id="cb12-2"><a href="#cb12-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb12-3"><a href="#cb12-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler <span class="op">=</span> ShardedGradScaler()</span></code></pre></div></div>
<p>Its <code>scale</code>/<code>step</code>/<code>update</code> API mirrors <code>torch.amp.GradScaler</code> but performs the necessary cross-rank communication to determine whether <em>any</em> shard anywhere saw an inf/nan, since with FSDP no single rank holds the complete gradient for a global finiteness check.</p>
</section>
<section id="practical-recommendation" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="practical-recommendation" id="practical-recommendation">7.3 Practical recommendation</h3>
<p>For large-model training (the primary use case FSDP targets), bf16 + FSDP <code>MixedPrecision</code> (no scaler needed) is by far the more common and lower-friction setup than fp16 + <code>ShardedGradScaler</code>, precisely because bf16 sidesteps the scaling machinery’s added complexity in a sharded, multi-rank setting. Reach for fp16 + <code>ShardedGradScaler</code> primarily when targeting hardware where bf16 throughput is not competitive with fp16 (some older Tensor Core generations) or when you need fp16’s extra mantissa precision relative to bf16 for a numerically delicate model.</p>
</section>
</section>
<section id="benchmarking-and-profiling-amp" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="benchmarking-and-profiling-amp" id="benchmarking-and-profiling-amp">8. Benchmarking and Profiling AMP</h2>
<section id="measuring-wall-clock-speedup-correctly" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="measuring-wall-clock-speedup-correctly" id="measuring-wall-clock-speedup-correctly">8.1 Measuring wall-clock speedup correctly</h3>
<p>Naive timing pitfalls to avoid:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>CUDA is asynchronous.</strong> A bare <code>time.time()</code> around a forward/backward call measures kernel <em>launch</em> time, not execution time, unless you call <code>torch.cuda.synchronize()</code> immediately before and after the timed region.</li>
<li><strong>Warm-up matters.</strong> The first several iterations pay for CUDA context initialization, cuDNN algorithm autotuning (especially with <code>torch.backends.cudnn.benchmark=True</code>), and JIT/kernel caching. Discard at least 5-10 warm-up iterations before timing.</li>
<li><strong>Use CUDA events for GPU-side timing</strong>, which avoids CPU-GPU synchronization overhead contaminating the measurement:</li>
</ul>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb13"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb13-1"><a href="#cb13-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="im">import</span> torch</span>
<span id="cb13-2"><a href="#cb13-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb13-3"><a href="#cb13-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="kw">def</span> benchmark(fn, warmup<span class="op">=</span><span class="dv">10</span>, iters<span class="op">=</span><span class="dv">50</span>):</span>
<span id="cb13-4"><a href="#cb13-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="cf">for</span> _ <span class="kw">in</span> <span class="bu">range</span>(warmup):</span>
<span id="cb13-5"><a href="#cb13-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        fn()</span>
<span id="cb13-6"><a href="#cb13-6" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    torch.cuda.synchronize()</span>
<span id="cb13-7"><a href="#cb13-7" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb13-8"><a href="#cb13-8" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    start <span class="op">=</span> torch.cuda.Event(enable_timing<span class="op">=</span><span class="va">True</span>)</span>
<span id="cb13-9"><a href="#cb13-9" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    end <span class="op">=</span> torch.cuda.Event(enable_timing<span class="op">=</span><span class="va">True</span>)</span>
<span id="cb13-10"><a href="#cb13-10" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb13-11"><a href="#cb13-11" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    start.record()</span>
<span id="cb13-12"><a href="#cb13-12" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="cf">for</span> _ <span class="kw">in</span> <span class="bu">range</span>(iters):</span>
<span id="cb13-13"><a href="#cb13-13" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        fn()</span>
<span id="cb13-14"><a href="#cb13-14" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    end.record()</span>
<span id="cb13-15"><a href="#cb13-15" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    torch.cuda.synchronize()</span>
<span id="cb13-16"><a href="#cb13-16" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb13-17"><a href="#cb13-17" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="cf">return</span> start.elapsed_time(end) <span class="op">/</span> iters   <span class="co"># milliseconds per iteration</span></span></code></pre></div></div>
<p>Run this once with autocast enabled and once with it disabled (same model, same batch, same seed) to get a clean speedup ratio.</p>
</section>
<section id="measuring-memory-savings" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="measuring-memory-savings" id="measuring-memory-savings">8.2 Measuring memory savings</h3>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb14"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb14-1"><a href="#cb14-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>torch.cuda.reset_peak_memory_stats()</span>
<span id="cb14-2"><a href="#cb14-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="co"># ... run one or more training iterations ...</span></span>
<span id="cb14-3"><a href="#cb14-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>peak_bytes <span class="op">=</span> torch.cuda.max_memory_allocated()</span>
<span id="cb14-4"><a href="#cb14-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="bu">print</span>(<span class="ss">f"Peak memory: </span><span class="sc">{</span>peak_bytes <span class="op">/</span> <span class="fl">1e9</span><span class="sc">:.2f}</span><span class="ss"> GB"</span>)</span></code></pre></div></div>
<p>Compare <code>max_memory_allocated()</code> between fp32-only and AMP runs at matched batch size to quantify the activation memory savings, or hold memory fixed and increase batch size under AMP until you hit the same peak memory as the fp32 baseline to quantify the <em>effective batch size</em> gain.</p>
</section>
<section id="using-torch.profiler-to-see-the-dtype-breakdown" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="using-torch.profiler-to-see-the-dtype-breakdown" id="using-torch.profiler-to-see-the-dtype-breakdown">8.3 Using <code>torch.profiler</code> to see the dtype breakdown</h3>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb15"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb15-1"><a href="#cb15-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="im">from</span> torch.profiler <span class="im">import</span> profile, ProfilerActivity, record_function</span>

</section>
<section id="what-speedup-to-expect" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="what-speedup-to-expect" id="what-speedup-to-expect">8.4 What speedup to expect</h3>
<p>Realistic expectations, highly hardware- and model-dependent:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Compute-bound models</strong> (large matmul/conv-dominated architectures, large batch sizes) on Tensor Core-equipped GPUs (V100 and later): commonly 1.5-3x throughput improvement.</li>
<li><strong>Memory-bandwidth-bound models</strong> (small batch, many elementwise/normalization ops relative to matmuls): smaller speedup, since the denylist ops (softmax, layer norm, etc.) still run in fp32 and elementwise op throughput isn’t Tensor-Core-accelerated the same way.</li>
<li><strong>Memory capacity gains</strong> are often the more valuable win in practice — the freed-up memory lets you increase batch size or model size, which itself often improves both throughput and, for large-batch-sensitive optimizers, training dynamics.</li>
</ul>
</section>
</section>
<section id="common-pitfalls-and-debugging" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="common-pitfalls-and-debugging" id="common-pitfalls-and-debugging">9. Common Pitfalls and Debugging</h2>
<section id="writing-dtype-agnostic-training-code" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="writing-dtype-agnostic-training-code" id="writing-dtype-agnostic-training-code">9.1 Writing dtype-agnostic training code</h3>
<p>A robust pattern that lets you switch between fp32/fp16/bf16 via configuration without branching your training loop:</p>
<div class="code-copy-outer-scaffold"><div class="sourceCode" id="cb16"><pre class="sourceCode python code-with-copy"><code class="sourceCode python"><span id="cb16-1"><a href="#cb16-1" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>use_amp <span class="op">=</span> <span class="va">True</span></span>
<span id="cb16-2"><a href="#cb16-2" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>amp_dtype <span class="op">=</span> torch.bfloat16   <span class="co"># or torch.float16, or None for fp32</span></span>
<span id="cb16-3"><a href="#cb16-3" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb16-4"><a href="#cb16-4" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>scaler <span class="op">=</span> torch.amp.GradScaler(device<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>, enabled<span class="op">=</span>(use_amp <span class="kw">and</span> amp_dtype <span class="op">==</span> torch.float16))</span>
<span id="cb16-5"><a href="#cb16-5" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb16-6"><a href="#cb16-6" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a><span class="cf">for</span> inputs, targets <span class="kw">in</span> dataloader:</span>
<span id="cb16-7"><a href="#cb16-7" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    optimizer.zero_grad(set_to_none<span class="op">=</span><span class="va">True</span>)</span>
<span id="cb16-8"><a href="#cb16-8" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    <span class="cf">with</span> torch.autocast(device_type<span class="op">=</span><span class="st">"cuda"</span>, dtype<span class="op">=</span>amp_dtype, enabled<span class="op">=</span>use_amp):</span>
<span id="cb16-9"><a href="#cb16-9" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        outputs <span class="op">=</span> model(inputs)</span>
<span id="cb16-10"><a href="#cb16-10" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>        loss <span class="op">=</span> loss_fn(outputs, targets)</span>
<span id="cb16-11"><a href="#cb16-11" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a></span>
<span id="cb16-12"><a href="#cb16-12" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.scale(loss).backward()</span>
<span id="cb16-13"><a href="#cb16-13" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.step(optimizer)</span>
<span id="cb16-14"><a href="#cb16-14" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1"></a>    scaler.update()</span></code></pre></div></div>
<p>Since <code>GradScaler(enabled=False)</code> makes <code>scale()</code>/<code>step()</code>/<code>update()</code> no-ops that fall through to plain <code>backward()</code>/<code>optimizer.step()</code>, this same code path is correct for bf16 (scaler disabled, autocast active) and fp32 (scaler disabled, autocast disabled) without any <code>if</code> branches in the hot loop.</p>
</section>
<section id="loss-becomes-nan-immediately" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="loss-becomes-nan-immediately" id="loss-becomes-nan-immediately">9.2 Loss becomes NaN immediately</h3>
<p>Checklist, roughly in order of likelihood:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong>Scale started too high for this model.</strong> Try a lower <code>init_scale</code> (e.g., <code>2**12</code>) — some architectures (RNNs with long sequences, certain GAN losses) overflow even at moderate scales before the backoff mechanism has a chance to correct.</li>
<li><strong>A custom loss or op outside the denylist is overflowing in fp16.</strong> Force it to fp32 explicitly with a nested <code>autocast(enabled=False)</code> block (Section 3.3), casting inputs with <code>.float()</code> first.</li>
<li><strong>Gradient clipping applied before <code>unscale_()</code></strong> — clipping scaled gradients against an unscaled threshold can mask real explosions or trigger false-positive clipping that destabilizes training. Verify the ordering in Section 4.4.</li>
<li><strong>Data-dependent overflow</strong> — e.g., an input containing <code>inf</code>/<code>nan</code> upstream of the model, unrelated to AMP at all. Sanity-check by running one iteration in full fp32 with <code>torch.autograd.set_detect_anomaly(True)</code>.</li>
<li><strong>Mixing bf16 assumptions into fp16 code</strong> — e.g., initializing <code>GradScaler</code> but ambiently running the model in a <code>bfloat16</code>-dtyped tensor pipeline outside of autocast (bf16 tensors created directly, not via autocast casting), which desyncs the scaler’s assumptions about what dtype is actually in play.</li>
</ol>
</section>
<section id="no-speedup-observed" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="no-speedup-observed" id="no-speedup-observed">9.3 “No speedup observed”</h3>
<ul>
<li>Model is memory-bandwidth-bound rather than compute-bound (Section 8.4) — check with the profiler whether GPU utilization was already near 100% in fp32; if so, AMP has limited room to help.</li>
<li><code>cudnn.benchmark</code> not enabled — set <code>torch.backends.cudnn.benchmark = True</code> for convolutional models with fixed input shapes, so cuDNN can autotune fp16-optimized algorithms.</li>
<li>GPU generation lacks Tensor Cores or has poor fp16 throughput relative to fp32 (pre-Volta NVIDIA GPUs) — AMP provides little to no benefit and mainly serves as a memory-savings tool there.</li>
<li>Small batch size or small model — kernel launch overhead and non-Tensor-Core ops dominate; the fp16 matmul speedup is a small fraction of total step time.</li>
<li>Autocast region doesn’t actually wrap the compute-heavy ops (e.g., wrapping only the loss computation and not the forward pass) — verify region boundaries.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="in-place-operations-and-autocast" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="in-place-operations-and-autocast" id="in-place-operations-and-autocast">9.4 In-place operations and autocast</h3>
<p>In-place ops (<code>add_</code>, <code>mul_</code>, etc.) on tensors that came out of an autocast region can produce dtype-mismatch errors or silently defeat the casting policy, since in-place ops don’t go through the same dispatch-time casting decision in all cases. Prefer out-of-place ops within autocast regions unless you’ve specifically verified the in-place variant is autocast-safe for your PyTorch version.</p>
</section>
<section id="reproducibility" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="reproducibility" id="reproducibility">9.5 Reproducibility</h3>
<p>AMP introduces additional nondeterminism beyond the usual CUDA nondeterminism concerns: dynamic loss scaling means the exact scale factor trajectory (and hence which steps get skipped) can depend on timing-sensitive floating point behavior. For strict reproducibility across runs, log the scaler’s <code>state_dict()</code> (see Section 10) alongside model checkpoints, and be aware that bit-exact reproducibility of AMP training across different GPU architectures is generally not achievable even with fixed seeds, due to differing Tensor Core rounding behavior.</p>
</section>
</section>

<section id="quick-reference-best-practices-checklist" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="quick-reference-best-practices-checklist" id="quick-reference-best-practices-checklist">11. Quick Reference / Best Practices Checklist</h2>
<ul>
<li>Use <code>torch.autocast(device_type=..., dtype=...)</code> (the unified API) rather than the legacy <code>torch.cuda.amp.autocast()</code> in new code; the legacy form still works as a thin wrapper but the unified form is the forward-compatible spelling.</li>
<li>Prefer <code>bfloat16</code> over <code>float16</code> when your hardware supports it well (Ampere+ NVIDIA, recent AMD/TPU) and you want to avoid <code>GradScaler</code> complexity entirely — no scaling, no skipped steps, no scale-factor tuning.</li>
<li>Keep <code>loss.backward()</code> outside the autocast context; it’s unnecessary and potentially confusing inside it.</li>
<li>Always <code>unscale_()</code> before gradient clipping.</li>
<li>Checkpoint <code>scaler.state_dict()</code>.</li>
<li>For DDP, no special handling needed beyond per-rank autocast + scaler; verify scale factors stay synchronized across ranks if you suspect divergence.</li>
<li>For FSDP, prefer native <code>MixedPrecision</code> policy over layering <code>torch.autocast</code> on top; use <code>ShardedGradScaler</code> (not the plain <code>GradScaler</code>) if you need fp16 with sharded parameters.</li>
<li>Wrap numerically fragile custom ops in a nested <code>autocast(enabled=False)</code> block rather than disabling AMP globally.</li>
<li>Decorate custom <code>autograd.Function</code>s with <code>custom_fwd</code>/<code>custom_bwd</code> if they should participate correctly in autocast/backward dtype semantics.</li>
<li>Profile with <code>torch.profiler</code> to confirm ops are actually dispatching to lower-precision kernels before assuming AMP “isn’t working.”</li>
<li>Benchmark with CUDA events and proper warm-up; naive CPU-side timers will misreport AMP speedup due to CUDA’s asynchronous execution model.</li>
</ul>
</section>
