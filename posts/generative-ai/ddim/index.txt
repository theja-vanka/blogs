<section id="denoising-diffusion-implicit-models-ddim-a-verbose-guide" class="level1">

<p><img src="/blogs/posts/generative-ai/ddim/ddim.png" class="img-fluid"></p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p><strong>Paper:</strong> Jiaming Song, Chenlin Meng, Stefano Ermon. <em>Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models.</em> ICLR 2021. <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.02502">arXiv:2010.02502</a></p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>
<section id="tldr" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="tldr" id="tldr">TL;DR</h2>
<p>Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) generate excellent images but require simulating a long Markov chain (often <span class="math inline">\(T=1000\)</span> steps) to produce a single sample, because the generative process is defined as the <em>reverse of a fixed Markovian forward diffusion</em>. This paper observes that the training objective used by DDPMs only depends on the <strong>marginals</strong> <span class="math inline">\(q(x_t \mid x_0)\)</span>, not on the full joint forward process <span class="math inline">\(q(x_{1:T} \mid x_0)\)</span>. That means many different forward processes — including <strong>non-Markovian</strong> ones — share the same marginals and therefore the same (or an equivalent) training objective. The authors construct a family of non-Markovian forward processes indexed by a variance parameter <span class="math inline">\(\sigma\)</span>, derive a corresponding generative process for each, and show that a single DDPM checkpoint can be reused, without retraining, to sample from any member of this family. Setting <span class="math inline">\(\sigma = 0\)</span> yields a fully deterministic generative process — the <strong>Denoising Diffusion Implicit Model (DDIM)</strong> — which can skip steps of the reverse chain, produce comparable or better samples in 10–50<span class="math inline">\(\times\)</span> fewer steps, guarantees that the same initial noise <span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span> maps to visually consistent outputs across trajectories of different lengths, supports meaningful latent-space interpolation, and can be related to an ODE (an Euler discretization of a “probability flow” ODE), enabling near-exact encoding and reconstruction.</p>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="motivation-why-ddpms-are-slow" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="motivation-why-ddpms-are-slow" id="motivation-why-ddpms-are-slow">1. Motivation: Why DDPMs Are Slow</h2>
<p>Deep generative models such as GANs, VAEs, autoregressive models, normalizing flows, and iterative denoising models (DDPMs, score-based models / NCSNs) all try to approximate a data distribution <span class="math inline">\(q(x_0)\)</span> so that it is easy to sample from. GANs currently produce very sharp samples with a single forward pass through a generator network, but they are notoriously difficult to train (mode collapse, unstable optimization) and require careful architecture and hyperparameter choices.</p>
<p>DDPMs and Noise Conditional Score Networks (NCSNs) sidestep adversarial training entirely. They train a denoising autoencoder at many different noise levels and then produce a sample by <strong>iteratively denoising</strong> pure noise, using either Langevin-dynamics-like updates (NCSN) or the reverse of an explicit forward diffusion process (DDPM). This gets rid of adversarial instability, but the price is steep: because the generative Markov chain is the reverse of a forward chain with <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span> steps (often <span class="math inline">\(T=1000\)</span>), producing a single sample requires <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span> sequential network evaluations. The paper quotes concrete numbers: roughly 20 hours to sample 50,000 images of size <span class="math inline">\(32\times32\)</span> from a DDPM on an Nvidia 2080 Ti, versus under a minute for a GAN — and this gap balloons to roughly 1,000 hours for <span class="math inline">\(256\times256\)</span> images. This makes DDPMs impractical wherever latency matters.</p>
<p>The central question the paper asks is: <strong>can we keep the same trained network and the same training objective, but design a much faster generative process?</strong></p>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="background-the-ddpm-formalism" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="background-the-ddpm-formalism" id="background-the-ddpm-formalism">2. Background: The DDPM Formalism</h2>
<p>DDPMs are latent-variable models</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[p_{\theta}(x_0) = \int p_{\theta}(x_{0:T})\, dx_{1:T}, \qquad p_{\theta}(x_{0:T}) := p_{\theta}(x_T) \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_{\theta}^{(t)}(x_{t-1}\mid x_t) \tag{1}\]</span></p>
<p>where <span class="math inline">\(x_1, \dots, x_T\)</span> live in the same space <span class="math inline">\(\mathcal{X}\)</span> as <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span>. Training maximizes a variational lower bound on the log-likelihood:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\max_{\theta}\ \mathbb{E}_{q(x_0)}[\log p_\theta(x_0)] \;\le\; \max_\theta\ \mathbb{E}_{q(x_0,\dots,x_T)}\big[\log p_\theta(x_{0:T}) - \log q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)\big] \tag{2}\]</span></p>
<p>Unlike a VAE, the inference distribution <span class="math inline">\(q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)\)</span> here is <strong>fixed</strong>, not learned. Ho et al.&nbsp;(2020) chose a Markov chain of Gaussian transitions parameterized by a decreasing sequence <span class="math inline">\(\alpha_{1:T} \in (0,1]^T\)</span>:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0) := \prod_{t=1}^T q(x_t \mid x_{t-1}), \quad q(x_t\mid x_{t-1}) := \mathcal{N}\!\Big(\sqrt{\tfrac{\alpha_t}{\alpha_{t-1}}}\, x_{t-1},\ \big(1 - \tfrac{\alpha_t}{\alpha_{t-1}}\big) I\Big) \tag{3}\]</span></p>
<p>This is called the <strong>forward process</strong> because it progressively injects noise as <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> increases from <span class="math inline">\(0\)</span> to <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span>; the model <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta(x_{0:T})\)</span> is the <strong>generative process</strong> because it progressively removes noise going from <span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span> back to <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> (this is exactly the picture in Figure 1, left panel, discussed below).</p>
<p>A crucial property of this construction is that the forward process has a simple closed form for jumping directly from <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> to any <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span>, without needing to simulate the intermediate steps:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[q(x_t \mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}\big(x_t;\ \sqrt{\alpha_t}\, x_0,\ (1-\alpha_t) I\big) \tag{"special property"}\]</span></p>
<p>so we can write <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span> as a noised version of <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span>:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[x_t = \sqrt{\alpha_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\,\epsilon, \qquad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \tag{4}\]</span></p>
<p>Choosing <span class="math inline">\(\alpha_T \approx 0\)</span> makes <span class="math inline">\(q(x_T\mid x_0)\)</span> converge to a standard Gaussian for every <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span>, motivating the prior <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta(x_T) := \mathcal{N}(0,I)\)</span>. If every conditional is Gaussian with a trainable mean and fixed variance, the ELBO in Eq. (2) simplifies to a weighted sum of denoising losses:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[L_\gamma(\epsilon_\theta) := \sum_{t=1}^{T} \gamma_t\, \mathbb{E}_{x_0 \sim q(x_0),\, \epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)}\Big[\big\|\epsilon_\theta^{(t)}(\sqrt{\alpha_t}x_0 + \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\,\epsilon_t) - \epsilon_t\big\|_2^2\Big] \tag{5}\]</span></p>
<p>Here <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta = \{\epsilon_\theta^{(t)}\}_{t=1}^T\)</span> is a (typically shared-parameter, time-conditioned) network that tries to predict the noise <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_t\)</span> that was added to produce <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span>. Ho et al.&nbsp;found that setting all weights <span class="math inline">\(\gamma_t = 1\)</span> (rather than the theoretically-derived weights) produced the best samples in practice — this <span class="math inline">\(L_1\)</span> objective is what the field commonly calls “the DDPM loss,” and it is the same objective used by score-matching-based NCSNs.</p>
<p>The key limitation: <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span> is large (e.g., 1000) because a large <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span> keeps each individual reverse transition close to Gaussian, which is required for the Gaussian parameterization of <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta^{(t)}\)</span> to be a good approximation. But all <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span> steps must be run <em>sequentially</em> to draw one sample — there’s no way to parallelize across <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>.</p>

</section>
<section id="the-key-idea-non-markovian-forward-processes-with-the-same-marginals" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-key-idea-non-markovian-forward-processes-with-the-same-marginals" id="the-key-idea-non-markovian-forward-processes-with-the-same-marginals">3. The Key Idea: Non-Markovian Forward Processes with the Same Marginals</h2>
<p>Look again at Eq. (5): the objective <span class="math inline">\(L_\gamma\)</span> only involves the <strong>marginal</strong> distributions <span class="math inline">\(q(x_t\mid x_0)\)</span> for each <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> — it never references the <em>joint</em> <span class="math inline">\(q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)\)</span> directly. This is the crux of the paper. Since infinitely many joint distributions can share the same set of marginals, the authors ask whether a <strong>non-Markovian</strong> joint could be constructed that (a) has the same marginals <span class="math inline">\(q(x_t\mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\alpha_t}x_0, (1-\alpha_t)I)\)</span> for every <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>, and (b) yields a generative process that is faster to sample from.</p>
<section id="a-family-of-non-markovian-inference-processes" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="a-family-of-non-markovian-inference-processes" id="a-family-of-non-markovian-inference-processes">3.1 A family of non-Markovian inference processes</h3>
<p>Consider a family <span class="math inline">\(\mathcal{Q}\)</span> of inference distributions indexed by a vector <span class="math inline">\(\sigma \in \mathbb{R}^T_{\ge 0}\)</span>:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[q_\sigma(x_{1:T}\mid x_0) := q_\sigma(x_T\mid x_0) \prod_{t=2}^{T} q_\sigma(x_{t-1}\mid x_t, x_0) \tag{6}\]</span></p>
<p>with <span class="math inline">\(q_\sigma(x_T\mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\alpha_T}x_0, (1-\alpha_T)I)\)</span> and, for <span class="math inline">\(t&gt;1\)</span>,</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[q_\sigma(x_{t-1}\mid x_t, x_0) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\alpha_{t-1}-\sigma_t^2}\cdot \frac{x_t - \sqrt{\alpha_t}\,x_0}{\sqrt{1-\alpha_t}},\ \sigma_t^2 I\right) \tag{7}\]</span></p>
<p>The mean is chosen precisely so that, by induction (proved as <strong>Lemma 1</strong> in the paper’s Appendix B and reproduced below), <span class="math inline">\(q_\sigma(x_t\mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\alpha_t}x_0, (1-\alpha_t)I)\)</span> holds for <em>every</em> <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> — i.e., the marginals match the DDPM ones exactly, no matter what <span class="math inline">\(\sigma\)</span> is. Applying Bayes’ rule gives the corresponding “forward” conditional</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[q_\sigma(x_t\mid x_{t-1}, x_0) = \frac{q_\sigma(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\,q_\sigma(x_t\mid x_0)}{q_\sigma(x_{t-1}\mid x_0)} \tag{8}\]</span></p>
<p>which is also Gaussian, but now <strong>depends on both <span class="math inline">\(x_{t-1}\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span></strong> rather than only on <span class="math inline">\(x_{t-1}\)</span> — this is what makes the process non-Markovian. The magnitude of <span class="math inline">\(\sigma\)</span> controls how stochastic this process is: as <span class="math inline">\(\sigma \to 0\)</span>, observing <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> and any single <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span> pins down <span class="math inline">\(x_{t-1}\)</span> almost exactly (it becomes near-deterministic).</p>
<p><strong>Proof sketch of Lemma 1</strong> (marginal matching by induction): Assume <span class="math inline">\(q_\sigma(x_t\mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\alpha_t}x_0, (1-\alpha_t)I)\)</span> holds for some <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>. Using the Gaussian marginalization identity <span class="math inline">\(q_\sigma(x_{t-1}\mid x_0) = \int q_\sigma(x_t\mid x_0)\, q_\sigma(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\, dx_t\)</span> and the standard formula for marginalizing a linear-Gaussian model (Bishop, <em>PRML</em>, Eq. 2.115), the resulting mean is</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\mu_{t-1} = \sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\alpha_{t-1}-\sigma_t^2}\cdot \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t}x_0 - \sqrt{\alpha_t}x_0}{\sqrt{1-\alpha_t}} = \sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}\, x_0\]</span></p>
<p>(the correction term vanishes because the two <span class="math inline">\(\sqrt{\alpha_t}x_0\)</span> terms cancel), and the resulting covariance is</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\Sigma_{t-1} = \sigma_t^2 I + \frac{1-\alpha_{t-1}-\sigma_t^2}{1-\alpha_t}(1-\alpha_t) I = (1-\alpha_{t-1}) I.\]</span></p>
<p>So <span class="math inline">\(q_\sigma(x_{t-1}\mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}x_0, (1-\alpha_{t-1})I)\)</span>, completing the induction (base case <span class="math inline">\(t=T\)</span> holds by construction). <span class="math inline">\(\blacksquare\)</span></p>
</section>
<section id="the-matching-generative-process" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-matching-generative-process" id="the-matching-generative-process">3.2 The matching generative process</h3>
<p>Given a noisy <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span>, the natural strategy is: first predict what <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> probably was, then plug that prediction into <span class="math inline">\(q_\sigma(x_{t-1}\mid x_t, x_0)\)</span> to sample <span class="math inline">\(x_{t-1}\)</span>. Concretely, since <span class="math inline">\(x_t = \sqrt{\alpha_t}x_0 + \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\epsilon_t\)</span> (Eq. 4), a network <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta^{(t)}(x_t)\)</span> trained to predict the noise <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_t\)</span> gives us a <strong>denoised prediction of <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span></strong> for free:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[f_\theta^{(t)}(x_t) := \big(x_t - \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\cdot \epsilon_\theta^{(t)}(x_t)\big) / \sqrt{\alpha_t} \tag{9}\]</span></p>
<p>The generative process is then defined, with prior <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta(x_T) = \mathcal{N}(0,I)\)</span>, as</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[p_\theta^{(t)}(x_{t-1}\mid x_t) = \begin{cases}\mathcal{N}\big(f_\theta^{(1)}(x_1),\ \sigma_1^2 I\big) &amp; t=1\\[4pt] q_\sigma\big(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,\, f_\theta^{(t)}(x_t)\big) &amp; t&gt;1\end{cases} \tag{10}\]</span></p>
<p>i.e., Eq. (7) with the true <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> replaced by the model’s prediction <span class="math inline">\(f_\theta^{(t)}(x_t)\)</span>. Training uses the variational objective</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[J_\sigma(\epsilon_\theta) := \mathbb{E}_{q_\sigma(x_{0:T})}\big[\log q_\sigma(x_{1:T}\mid x_0) - \log p_\theta(x_{0:T})\big] \tag{11}\]</span></p>
<p>At first glance it looks like a <em>different model</em> must be trained for every choice of <span class="math inline">\(\sigma\)</span>, since each <span class="math inline">\(\sigma\)</span> defines a different objective <span class="math inline">\(J_\sigma\)</span>. The paper’s central theoretical result rules this out:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p><strong>Theorem 1.</strong> For all <span class="math inline">\(\sigma &gt; 0\)</span>, there exist <span class="math inline">\(\gamma \in \mathbb{R}^T_{&gt;0}\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(C \in \mathbb{R}\)</span> such that <span class="math inline">\(J_\sigma = L_\gamma + C\)</span>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Proof (sketch).</strong> Expand <span class="math inline">\(J_\sigma\)</span> and complete the square: each summand for <span class="math inline">\(t&gt;1\)</span> becomes a KL divergence between two Gaussians with the same variance <span class="math inline">\(\sigma_t^2\)</span>, which collapses to a squared-error term,</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\mathbb{E}\big[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\sigma(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\,\|\,p_\theta^{(t)}(x_{t-1}\mid x_t))\big] = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\|x_0 - f_\theta^{(t)}(x_t)\|_2^2}{2\sigma_t^2}\right]\]</span></p>
<p>Substituting the definitions of <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(f_\theta^{(t)}\)</span> from Eq. (4) and (9), the <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span>-dependent terms cancel and this reduces exactly to a rescaled noise-prediction error,</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[= \mathbb{E}_{x_0,\epsilon}\left[\frac{\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta^{(t)}(x_t)\|_2^2}{2 d\, \sigma_t^2 \alpha_t}\right]\]</span></p>
<p>where <span class="math inline">\(d = \dim(x_0)\)</span>. The <span class="math inline">\(t=1\)</span> term reduces analogously. Hence, setting <span class="math inline">\(\gamma_t = 1/(2d\sigma_t^2\alpha_t)\)</span> for every <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> gives <span class="math inline">\(J_\sigma(\epsilon_\theta) \equiv L_\gamma(\epsilon_\theta)\)</span> for all <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta\)</span>, i.e., <span class="math inline">\(J_\sigma = L_\gamma + C\)</span>. <span class="math inline">\(\blacksquare\)</span></p>
<p>The payoff of Theorem 1 is enormous: since <span class="math inline">\(L_\gamma\)</span>’s optimum does not depend on the weighting <span class="math inline">\(\gamma\)</span> whenever parameters are <em>not shared across timesteps</em> (the global optimum is reached by independently minimizing each term of the sum), the <span class="math inline">\(L_1\)</span>-trained DDPM network is <em>already</em> the optimal solution for <strong>every</strong> <span class="math inline">\(J_\sigma\)</span>. <strong>A single pretrained DDPM checkpoint can serve as the noise predictor for any member of this entire family of generative processes — no retraining required.</strong> The only thing that changes across family members is how you use the trained network at sampling time.</p>

</section>
</section>
<section id="sampling-from-generalized-generative-processes" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sampling-from-generalized-generative-processes" id="sampling-from-generalized-generative-processes">4. Sampling from Generalized Generative Processes</h2>
<section id="denoising-diffusion-implicit-models-ddim" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="denoising-diffusion-implicit-models-ddim" id="denoising-diffusion-implicit-models-ddim">4.1 Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM)</h3>
<p>From Eq. (10), one can sample <span class="math inline">\(x_{t-1}\)</span> from <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span> via:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}\underbrace{\left(\frac{x_t - \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\,\epsilon_\theta^{(t)}(x_t)}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\right)}_{\text{"predicted }x_0\text{"}} + \underbrace{\sqrt{1-\alpha_{t-1}-\sigma_t^2}\cdot \epsilon_\theta^{(t)}(x_t)}_{\text{"direction pointing to }x_t\text{"}} + \underbrace{\sigma_t \epsilon_t}_{\text{random noise}} \tag{12}\]</span></p>
<p>with <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_t\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\)</span> independent of <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span>, and <span class="math inline">\(\alpha_0 := 1\)</span>. Different choices of <span class="math inline">\(\sigma\)</span> give different generative processes using the <strong>same</strong> <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta\)</span>:</p>
<ul>
<li>If <span class="math inline">\(\sigma_t = \sqrt{(1-\alpha_{t-1})/(1-\alpha_t)}\sqrt{1-\alpha_t/\alpha_{t-1}}\)</span> for all <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>, the forward process becomes Markovian again and the generative process reduces exactly to the original <strong>DDPM</strong>.</li>
<li>If <span class="math inline">\(\sigma_t = 0\)</span> for all <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>, the forward process becomes deterministic given <span class="math inline">\(x_{t-1}\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> (except at <span class="math inline">\(t=1\)</span>), and the coefficient on the random noise term in Eq. (12) vanishes. The resulting model is an <strong>implicit probabilistic model</strong> — samples are a deterministic function of the initial latent <span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span>. This is the <strong>Denoising Diffusion Implicit Model (DDIM)</strong>, pronounced /ˈdɪm/, so named because it is an implicit model trained with the DDPM objective, even though its “forward process” is no longer a diffusion in the classical sense.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="accelerated-generation-processes-sampling-with-fewer-steps" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="accelerated-generation-processes-sampling-with-fewer-steps" id="accelerated-generation-processes-sampling-with-fewer-steps">4.2 Accelerated Generation Processes (Sampling with Fewer Steps)</h3>
<p>Because the denoising loss <span class="math inline">\(L_1\)</span> never depended on the forward process having exactly <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span> steps — only on the marginals <span class="math inline">\(q_\sigma(x_t\mid x_0)\)</span> being fixed — we can define the forward process over just a <strong>subsequence</strong> <span class="math inline">\(\{x_{\tau_1}, \dots, x_{\tau_S}\}\)</span>, where <span class="math inline">\(\tau\)</span> is an increasing subsequence of <span class="math inline">\([1,\dots,T]\)</span> of length <span class="math inline">\(S \ll T\)</span>, while requiring <span class="math inline">\(q(x_{\tau_i}\mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\alpha_{\tau_i}}x_0, (1-\alpha_{\tau_i})I)\)</span> to still match the marginals. The generative process then only needs to walk backwards along <span class="math inline">\(\tau\)</span> — this reversed subsequence is called the <strong>sampling trajectory</strong>. Since sampling cost is linear in the number of steps taken, using <span class="math inline">\(S \ll T\)</span> steps gives a proportional speedup.</p>
<p>Formally (Appendix C), the accelerated inference process factorizes as</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[q_{\sigma,\tau}(x_{1:T}\mid x_0) = q_{\sigma,\tau}(x_{\tau_S}\mid x_0)\prod_{i=1}^{S} q_{\sigma,\tau}(x_{\tau_{i-1}}\mid x_{\tau_i}, x_0) \prod_{t\in\bar\tau} q_{\sigma,\tau}(x_t\mid x_0) \tag{52}\]</span></p>
<p>where <span class="math inline">\(\bar\tau := \{1,\dots,T\}\setminus\tau\)</span>. Intuitively, <span class="math inline">\(\{x_{\tau_i}\}\)</span> together with <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> form a chain (used to actually produce samples), while the remaining <span class="math inline">\(\{x_t\}_{t\in\bar\tau}\)</span> together with <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> form a star graph (only needed to keep the variational bound well-defined — see Figure 2 below). A similar argument to Theorem 1’s proof shows this accelerated objective is <em>also</em> equivalent (up to reweighting) to <span class="math inline">\(L_\gamma\)</span>, so — again — <strong>no retraining is required</strong>; only the sampling procedure changes.</p>
<p>Two practical choices for picking the subsequence <span class="math inline">\(\tau\)</span> (Appendix D.2) are used in the experiments: <strong>linear</strong> (<span class="math inline">\(\tau_i = \lfloor ci\rfloor\)</span>) and <strong>quadratic</strong> (<span class="math inline">\(\tau_i=\lfloor ci^2\rfloor\)</span>) spacing, with <span class="math inline">\(c\)</span> chosen so <span class="math inline">\(\tau_S \approx T\)</span>. The paper uses quadratic spacing for CIFAR-10 and linear spacing for the other datasets, each found empirically to give marginally better FID than the alternative.</p>
</section>
<section id="connection-to-neural-odes" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="connection-to-neural-odes" id="connection-to-neural-odes">4.3 Connection to Neural ODEs</h3>
<p>Rewriting the DDIM update (Eq. 12, with <span class="math inline">\(\sigma_t=0\)</span>) in terms of a small step <span class="math inline">\(\Delta t\)</span> reveals a resemblance to an Euler discretization of an ODE:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\frac{x_{t-\Delta t}}{\sqrt{\alpha_{t-\Delta t}}} = \frac{x_t}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} + \left(\sqrt{\frac{1-\alpha_{t-\Delta t}}{\alpha_{t-\Delta t}}} - \sqrt{\frac{1-\alpha_t}{\alpha_t}}\right)\epsilon_\theta^{(t)}(x_t) \tag{13}\]</span></p>
<p>Reparameterizing with <span class="math inline">\(\bar x := x/\sqrt{\alpha}\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(\sigma(t) := \sqrt{(1-\alpha(t))/\alpha(t)}\)</span> (an increasing, continuous function with <span class="math inline">\(\sigma(0)=0\)</span>), taking <span class="math inline">\(\Delta t \to 0\)</span> turns Eq. (13) into the ODE</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[d\bar x(t) = \epsilon_\theta^{(t)}\!\left(\frac{\bar x(t)}{\sqrt{\sigma^2(t)+1}}\right) d\sigma(t) \tag{14}\]</span></p>
<p>with initial condition <span class="math inline">\(\bar x(T) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma(T)^2 I)\)</span> for large <span class="math inline">\(\sigma(T)\)</span>. Because this is an ODE (not an SDE), it can, with a fine enough discretization, be simulated <strong>forward in time</strong> (<span class="math inline">\(t=0\to T\)</span>, encoding <span class="math inline">\(x_0\to x_T\)</span>) just as easily as backward (<span class="math inline">\(t=T\to0\)</span>, generating <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> from <span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span>). This is a property DDPMs fundamentally lack, since their reverse process is stochastic.</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p><strong>Proposition 1.</strong> The ODE in Eq. (14), evaluated at the optimal <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta^{(t)}\)</span>, is equivalent to the probability-flow ODE corresponding to the “Variance-Exploding” (VE) SDE of Song et al.&nbsp;(2020) — a continuous-time generalization of NCSN-style score-based models.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Proof idea.</strong> Reparameterize with <span class="math inline">\(\bar x(t) = \bar x(0) + \sigma(t)\epsilon\)</span>, matching the VE-SDE convention. The DDIM-derived ODE (Eq. 45 in the appendix) is <span class="math inline">\(\frac{d\bar x}{dt} = \frac{d\sigma}{dt}\,\epsilon_\theta^{(t)}\big(\bar x/\sqrt{\sigma^2+1}\big)\)</span>. Independently, the probability-flow ODE for a VE-SDE is <span class="math inline">\(d\bar x = -\tfrac12 g(t)^2 \nabla_{\bar x}\log p_t(\bar x)\, dt\)</span> with <span class="math inline">\(g(t)^2 = d\sigma^2(t)/dt\)</span>. Using the fact that the optimal noise predictor and the optimal score function are related by <span class="math inline">\(\nabla_{\bar x}\log p_t(\bar x) = -\epsilon_\theta^{(t)}(\bar x/\sqrt{\sigma^2+1})/\sigma(t)\)</span> (a standard denoising-score-matching identity), substituting into the probability-flow ODE and simplifying recovers exactly <span class="math inline">\(\frac{d\bar x}{dt} = \frac{d\sigma}{dt}\epsilon_\theta^{(t)}(\cdot)\)</span> — identical to the DDIM ODE. <span class="math inline">\(\blacksquare\)</span></p>
<p>Although the two ODEs are mathematically identical, their <strong>discretizations differ</strong>: DDIM’s Euler steps are taken with respect to <span class="math inline">\(\sigma(t)\)</span>, while the probability-flow ODE’s Euler steps (Eq. 15 in the paper) are taken with respect to <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> directly. These coincide when <span class="math inline">\(\alpha_t\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(\alpha_{t-\Delta t}\)</span> are close, but diverge for large steps — which is precisely the regime accelerated sampling operates in, explaining why the two integration schemes behave differently at low step counts in practice.</p>

</section>
</section>
<section id="algorithms" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="algorithms" id="algorithms">5. Algorithms</h2>
<p>The paper does not present its methods as boxed pseudocode, but the two procedures below (training, and generalized sampling) directly formalize Eq. (5) and Eq. (12)/(52), and match how DDIM is implemented in practice.</p>
<p><strong>Algorithm 1 — Training (unchanged from DDPM)</strong></p>
<pre><code>Input: data distribution q(x0), noise schedule alpha_1..alpha_T, network eps_theta
repeat
    sample x0 ~ q(x0)
    sample t ~ Uniform({1, ..., T})
    sample epsilon ~ N(0, I)
    x_t = sqrt(alpha_t) * x0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_t) * epsilon
    take gradient step on
        grad_theta || epsilon - eps_theta(x_t, t) ||^2
until converged</code></pre>
<p><strong>Algorithm 2 — Generalized (DDIM/DDPM) Sampling</strong></p>
<pre><code>Input: trained eps_theta, schedule alpha_1..alpha_T, sub-sequence tau = [tau_1 &lt; ... &lt; tau_S = T],
       stochasticity coefficients sigma_{tau_i} (sigma = 0 gives DDIM; a specific sigma_t gives DDPM)
x_{tau_S} ~ N(0, I)
for i = S, S-1, ..., 1:
    predict noise:      eps_hat = eps_theta(x_{tau_i}, tau_i)
    predict x0:          x0_hat = (x_{tau_i} - sqrt(1 - alpha_{tau_i}) * eps_hat) / sqrt(alpha_{tau_i})
    direction to x_t:     dir    = sqrt(1 - alpha_{tau_{i-1}} - sigma_{tau_i}^2) * eps_hat
    sample noise:         z ~ N(0, I)  if i &gt; 1 else z = 0
    x_{tau_{i-1}} = sqrt(alpha_{tau_{i-1}}) * x0_hat + dir + sigma_{tau_i} * z
return x_{tau_0}  # = x0</code></pre>
<p>Setting <span class="math inline">\(\sigma \equiv 0\)</span> for every step removes the stochastic term entirely, giving deterministic DDIM sampling; setting <span class="math inline">\(\dim(\tau) = T\)</span> (i.e.&nbsp;<span class="math inline">\(\tau = [1,\dots,T]\)</span>) with the DDPM-matching <span class="math inline">\(\sigma_t\)</span> recovers the original full-length DDPM sampler. Everything in between — fewer steps, partial stochasticity via <span class="math inline">\(\eta\)</span> (Section 6 below) — is available immediately, from the same checkpoint.</p>

</section>
<section id="experiments" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="experiments" id="experiments">6. Experiments</h2>
<p>All experiments reuse a <strong>single trained model per dataset</strong> (<span class="math inline">\(T=1000\)</span>, trained with the <span class="math inline">\(L_1\)</span> objective from Eq. 5, exactly as in Ho et al.&nbsp;2020) — datasets are CIFAR-10 (<span class="math inline">\(32\times32\)</span>), CelebA (<span class="math inline">\(64\times64\)</span>), LSUN Bedroom, and LSUN Church (both <span class="math inline">\(256\times256\)</span>). Only the <em>sampling procedure</em> changes across experiments, controlled by two knobs: the sub-sequence <span class="math inline">\(\tau\)</span> (how many/which steps to use) and a stochasticity interpolation parameter <span class="math inline">\(\eta\)</span>, defined so that</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\sigma_{\tau_i}(\eta) = \eta\sqrt{\frac{1-\alpha_{\tau_{i-1}}}{1-\alpha_{\tau_i}}}\sqrt{1-\frac{\alpha_{\tau_i}}{\alpha_{\tau_{i-1}}}} \tag{16}\]</span></p>
<p><span class="math inline">\(\eta=0\)</span> recovers <strong>DDIM</strong> (fully deterministic); <span class="math inline">\(\eta=1\)</span> recovers the original <strong>DDPM</strong>. The paper also benchmarks <span class="math inline">\(\hat\sigma\)</span>, a larger-variance DDPM variant used by Ho et al.&nbsp;(2020) specifically for their CIFAR-10 results.</p>

<p>Takeaways: as <span class="math inline">\(\dim(\tau)\)</span> grows, quality improves for every method (expected trade-off between compute and quality). DDIM (<span class="math inline">\(\eta=0\)</span>) is consistently the best or tied-best at every step count, and its advantage is largest exactly where it matters most — few steps. DDPM-style stochastic sampling (<span class="math inline">\(\eta=1\)</span>, <span class="math inline">\(\hat\sigma\)</span>) degrades sharply as <span class="math inline">\(\dim(\tau)\)</span> shrinks; <span class="math inline">\(\hat\sigma\)</span> in particular becomes extremely poor at low step counts because FID is highly sensitive to the added noisy perturbations it introduces. Wall-clock time scales linearly with the number of sampling steps (Figure 4 in the paper), so DDIM’s ability to hit “1000-step quality” using only 20–100 steps translates directly into a <span class="math inline">\(10\times\)</span>–<span class="math inline">\(50\times\)</span> real speedup; e.g., the 100-step DDPM on CelebA is roughly matched in FID by the 20-step DDIM.</p>

<section id="sample-consistency" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sample-consistency" id="sample-consistency">6.2 Sample Consistency</h3>
<p>Because DDIM’s generative process is a deterministic function of <span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span>, fixing <span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span> and varying the sampling trajectory <span class="math inline">\(\tau\)</span> (i.e., varying how many/which intermediate steps are taken) produces images that share the same high-level content, with only fine details varying. Even a 20-step trajectory already captures most of the high-level structure that a 1000-step trajectory would produce. This strongly suggests that <strong><span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span> alone functions as a semantically meaningful latent code</strong> — an unusual property for a diffusion-style model, and one that DDPM’s stochastic sampling cannot offer (identical <span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span> under DDPM yields highly diverse outputs).</p>
</section>
<section id="latent-space-interpolation" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="latent-space-interpolation" id="latent-space-interpolation">6.3 Latent-Space Interpolation</h3>
<p>Since high-level content is encoded in <span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span>, the authors test whether interpolating in the <em>latent</em> space, as is common for GANs, produces meaningful interpolation in image space. Two random <span class="math inline">\(x_T^{(0)}, x_T^{(1)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)\)</span> are combined with <strong>spherical linear interpolation (slerp)</strong>, which is the natural way to interpolate points that are meant to lie roughly on a Gaussian’s typical “shell”:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[x_T^{(\alpha)} = \frac{\sin((1-\alpha)\theta)}{\sin\theta}\, x_T^{(0)} + \frac{\sin(\alpha\theta)}{\sin\theta}\, x_T^{(1)}, \qquad \theta = \arccos\!\left(\frac{x_T^{(0)\top}x_T^{(1)}}{\|x_T^{(0)}\|\,\|x_T^{(1)}\|}\right) \tag{67}\]</span></p>
<p>Each interpolated <span class="math inline">\(x_T^{(\alpha)}\)</span> is then decoded with DDIM. The resulting sequences show smooth, semantically coherent transitions (pose, identity, background attributes blending gradually) — a property DDPM cannot replicate directly, since re-using the same <span class="math inline">\(x_T\)</span> under DDPM’s stochastic decoder does not pin down a consistent <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span>.</p>
</section>

<section id="related-work" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="related-work" id="related-work">7. Related Work</h2>
<p>DDIM sits within a long line of work on learning generative models as Markov-chain transition operators (Sohl-Dickstein et al.&nbsp;2015; Bengio et al.&nbsp;2014; Salimans et al.&nbsp;2014; and others). DDPMs (Ho et al.&nbsp;2020) and NCSNs (Song &amp; Ermon 2019, 2020) are the two most prominent recent members of this family, and — despite differing motivations (variational lower bound vs.&nbsp;score matching) — turn out to be closely related: both train a denoising objective across many noise scales, and both draw samples with a Langevin-dynamics-like iterative procedure, which is why both require many steps to reach good quality (Langevin dynamics is a discretization of a gradient flow, and gradient flows are inherently slow to converge in high dimension).</p>
<p>DDIM breaks from this mold precisely because it is derived as a purely variational construction, decoupled from any requirement to resemble Langevin dynamics. Because its generative process is a deterministic map from a fixed latent, it inherits properties usually associated with <strong>implicit generative models</strong> (Mohamed &amp; Lakshminarayanan 2016) such as GANs and invertible normalizing flows — most notably, meaningful latent-space interpolation. Its “continuous-depth” sampling behavior (same high-level content regardless of how many intermediate steps are simulated) is also reminiscent of Neural ODEs and continuous-depth networks (Chen et al.&nbsp;2018; Grathwohl et al.&nbsp;2018).</p>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="discussion-limitations-and-future-directions" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="discussion-limitations-and-future-directions" id="discussion-limitations-and-future-directions">8. Discussion, Limitations, and Future Directions</h2>
<p>The paper closes by noting that the non-Markovian construction opens the door to forward processes that are not Gaussian at all — something the original diffusion framework could not do, since Gaussian is the unique stable distribution with finite variance. As a proof of concept, Appendix A works out an analogous <strong>non-Markovian construction for discrete/categorical data</strong> (see below). The authors also flag the ODE connection as an especially promising direction: numerical methods that reduce discretization error beyond simple Euler steps — e.g., multistep methods like Adams–Bashforth — could plausibly improve sample quality at a fixed, small step budget, since DDIM sampling is mathematically just Euler integration of an ODE. This idea would later be highly influential in the diffusion literature (fast ODE/SDE solvers, DPM-Solver, and related accelerated samplers, are direct intellectual descendants of this observation, though they postdate this paper).</p>
<section id="appendix-a-the-discretecategorical-case-briefly" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="appendix-a-the-discretecategorical-case-briefly" id="appendix-a-the-discretecategorical-case-briefly">Appendix A: The discrete/categorical case (briefly)</h3>
<p>For a one-hot categorical <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> with <span class="math inline">\(K\)</span> classes, define <span class="math inline">\(q(x_t\mid x_0) = \mathrm{Cat}(\alpha_t x_0 + (1-\alpha_t)\mathbf{1}_K)\)</span> (a mixture that gradually forgets the true class in favor of the uniform distribution <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{1}_K/K\)</span>). An analogous non-Markovian <span class="math inline">\(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\)</span> is defined as a three-way mixture over “keep <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span>”, “reveal <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span>”, and “go fully uniform,” with mixture weights chosen so the marginals match. The KL divergence between this and the model’s own reverse conditional <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\)</span> reduces to (an upper bound of) a standard multi-class classification loss, mirroring the Gaussian case’s reduction to squared error. The authors leave a full empirical study of this discrete variant to future work, but note it suggests DDIM-style acceleration is not intrinsically tied to continuous, Gaussian diffusions.</p>

<section id="key-takeaways" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="key-takeaways" id="key-takeaways">10. Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>The central theoretical contribution is deceptively simple but far-reaching: the DDPM training objective is a property of marginals, not of any particular Markov chain, so a whole family of non-Markovian generative processes — parameterized by a stochasticity knob <span class="math inline">\(\sigma\)</span> (or, in the experiments, <span class="math inline">\(\eta\)</span>) and a sampling-trajectory length <span class="math inline">\(S\)</span> — can all be driven by exactly the same trained noise-prediction network. Setting stochasticity to zero yields DDIM, a deterministic implicit model that (a) samples 10–50<span class="math inline">\(\times\)</span> faster than DDPM at comparable or better quality, (b) produces trajectory-length-consistent outputs from a fixed latent, (c) supports meaningful latent interpolation via slerp, and (d) is exactly the Euler discretization of a probability-flow ODE, enabling near-lossless encode/decode. These properties collectively foreshadow much of the later fast-sampler and ODE/SDE-solver literature for diffusion models.</p>
