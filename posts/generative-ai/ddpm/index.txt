<section id="denoising-diffusion-probabilistic-models" class="level1">

<p><img src="/blogs/posts/generative-ai/ddpm/ddpm.png" class="img-fluid"></p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p><strong>Paper:</strong> Jonathan Ho, Ajay Jain, Pieter Abbeel. <em>Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models.</em> NeurIPS 2020. <strong>arXiv:</strong> <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.11239">2006.11239</a> · <strong>Code:</strong> <a href="https://github.com/hojonathanho/diffusion">hojonathanho/diffusion</a></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This guide walks through the full technical content of the paper: the probabilistic setup, every derivation needed to arrive at the training objective, the training and sampling algorithms, the architecture, the experiments, and the paper’s interpretation of diffusion models as progressive lossy decoders.</p>
<section id="why-this-paper-matters" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="why-this-paper-matters" id="why-this-paper-matters">Why this paper matters</h2>
<p>Before DDPM, “diffusion probabilistic models” (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) were a theoretically elegant but practically unproven idea: define a Markov chain that slowly destroys structure in data by adding noise, then learn to reverse it. GANs, VAEs, flows, and autoregressive models were all producing sharp samples; diffusion models were not known to be competitive.</p>
<p>Ho, Jain, and Abbeel’s contribution was to show that with the right parameterization, diffusion models generate CIFAR-10 samples with an Inception Score of 9.46 and an FID of 3.17 — beating essentially all published generative models on that benchmark at the time — and produce LSUN/CelebA-HQ 256×256 samples competitive with ProgressiveGAN. Just as importantly, they show that a particular parameterization choice makes the training objective equivalent to <strong>denoising score matching across multiple noise scales</strong>, unifying diffusion models with the score-based generative modeling line of work (Song &amp; Ermon, 2019).</p>
<p>The price paid for this sample quality: DDPMs are not competitive on log-likelihood compared to autoregressive/flow models, and sampling requires a full sequential pass through hundreds or thousands of denoising steps (this second issue is what later work like DDIM and distillation methods addresses).</p>
</section>
<section id="setup-latent-variable-models-with-a-fixed-encoder" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="setup-latent-variable-models-with-a-fixed-encoder" id="setup-latent-variable-models-with-a-fixed-encoder">Setup: latent variable models with a fixed encoder</h2>
<p>A diffusion model is a latent-variable model</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0) := \int p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{0:T})\, d\mathbf{x}_{1:T}\]</span></p>
<p>where <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_T\)</span> are latents with the <em>same dimensionality</em> as the data <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0 \sim q(\mathbf{x}_0)\)</span>. This is unlike a VAE, where the latent is lower-dimensional and the encoder is learned. Here:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <strong>approximate posterior</strong> <span class="math inline">\(q(\mathbf{x}_{1:T} \mid \mathbf{x}_0)\)</span> — called the <strong>forward</strong> or <strong>diffusion process</strong> — is <em>fixed</em> (not learned) and is itself a Markov chain that gradually adds Gaussian noise according to a variance schedule <span class="math inline">\(\beta_1, \dots, \beta_T\)</span>.</li>
<li>The <strong>reverse process</strong> <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{0:T})\)</span> is a learned Markov chain that starts at <span class="math inline">\(p(\mathbf{x}_T) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_T; \mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\)</span> and denoises step by step back to data.</li>
</ul>
<p>Because both processes are Markov chains of conditional Gaussians, the whole model reduces to learning the mean (and optionally variance) of a Gaussian at every step — “a particularly simple neural network parameterization,” in the paper’s words.</p>
<section id="the-forward-diffusion-process" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-forward-diffusion-process" id="the-forward-diffusion-process">The forward (diffusion) process</h3>
<p><span class="math display">\[q(\mathbf{x}_{1:T} \mid \mathbf{x}_0) := \prod_{t=1}^{T} q(\mathbf{x}_t \mid \mathbf{x}_{t-1}), \qquad q(\mathbf{x}_t \mid \mathbf{x}_{t-1}) := \mathcal{N}\big(\mathbf{x}_t; \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\,\mathbf{x}_{t-1},\, \beta_t \mathbf{I}\big)\]</span></p>
<p>At each step, a small amount of Gaussian noise is added and the signal is slightly rescaled down. <span class="math inline">\(\beta_1, \dots, \beta_T\)</span> is a fixed (not learned) variance schedule; in this paper it is a linear schedule from <span class="math inline">\(\beta_1 = 10^{-4}\)</span> to <span class="math inline">\(\beta_T = 0.02\)</span> with <span class="math inline">\(T = 1000\)</span>.</p>
<p><strong>Closed-form marginal.</strong> Define <span class="math inline">\(\alpha_t := 1 - \beta_t\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(\bar\alpha_t := \prod_{s=1}^{t} \alpha_s\)</span>. Using the reparameterization trick and the fact that a sum of independent Gaussians is Gaussian, the forward process admits sampling <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_t\)</span> at <em>any</em> timestep directly from <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0\)</span> in closed form, without stepping through the chain:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[q(\mathbf{x}_t \mid \mathbf{x}_0) = \mathcal{N}\big(\mathbf{x}_t;\, \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0,\, (1-\bar\alpha_t)\mathbf{I}\big)\]</span></p>
<p>Equivalently, with <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\)</span>:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon\]</span></p>
<p>This identity is the workhorse of the entire paper — it lets training sample a random timestep <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> and jump straight to the noised image <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_t\)</span>, rather than simulating <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> sequential noising steps. As <span class="math inline">\(t \to T\)</span>, <span class="math inline">\(\bar\alpha_t \to 0\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_T\)</span> becomes (approximately) isotropic Gaussian noise, matching the prior <span class="math inline">\(p(\mathbf{x}_T)\)</span>.</p>
</section>
<section id="the-reverse-process" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-reverse-process" id="the-reverse-process">The reverse process</h3>
<p><span class="math display">\[p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{0:T}) := p(\mathbf{x}_T) \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid \mathbf{x}_t), \qquad p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t) := \mathcal{N}\big(\mathbf{x}_{t-1};\, \boldsymbol\mu_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t),\, \boldsymbol\Sigma_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\big)\]</span></p>
<p>A single neural network (conditioned on the timestep <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>) predicts the mean <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\mu_\theta\)</span> (and, in the simplest variant of the paper, the variance is fixed rather than learned — see <a href="#sec-training-sampling" class="quarto-xref">Section&nbsp;1.4</a>). Sampling means starting from pure noise <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\)</span> and iteratively drawing <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_{t-1} \sim p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t)\)</span> down to <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0\)</span>.</p>
<p>The diagram below is the paper’s Figure 2: the directed graphical model showing both processes on the same chain of variables. The <strong>forward</strong> process <span class="math inline">\(q\)</span> moves left-to-right (adding noise, fixed, no learned parameters); the <strong>reverse</strong> process <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta\)</span> moves right-to-left (removing noise, learned).</p>

<section id="deriving-the-training-objective" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="deriving-the-training-objective" id="deriving-the-training-objective">Deriving the training objective</h2>
<section id="the-variational-bound" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-variational-bound" id="the-variational-bound">The variational bound</h3>
<p>Since <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0)\)</span> requires an intractable integral, training proceeds by minimizing a variational bound on negative log-likelihood, exactly as in a VAE, using <span class="math inline">\(q\)</span> as the fixed “encoder”:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\mathbb{E}[-\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0)] \le \mathbb{E}_q\left[-\log \frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{0:T})}{q(\mathbf{x}_{1:T}\mid\mathbf{x}_0)}\right] =: L\]</span></p>
<p>Expanding this using the Markov structure of both chains and regrouping terms via Bayes’ rule (the standard DDPM/Sohl-Dickstein derivation) yields a sum of KL divergences between Gaussians, which are analytically tractable:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[L = \underbrace{D_{KL}\big(q(\mathbf{x}_T\mid\mathbf{x}_0) \,\|\, p(\mathbf{x}_T)\big)}_{L_T} + \sum_{t=2}^{T} \underbrace{D_{KL}\big(q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0) \,\|\, p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t)\big)}_{L_{t-1}} \;\underbrace{- \log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0\mid\mathbf{x}_1)}_{L_0}\]</span></p>
<ul>
<li><span class="math inline">\(L_T\)</span> has no learnable parameters (<span class="math inline">\(q\)</span> is fixed and <span class="math inline">\(\beta\)</span>’s are fixed constants), so it is a constant during training and can be ignored, provided <span class="math inline">\(\bar\alpha_T \approx 0\)</span> so that <span class="math inline">\(q(\mathbf{x}_T\mid\mathbf{x}_0) \approx \mathcal{N}(0,I) = p(\mathbf{x}_T)\)</span>.</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(L_{t-1}\)</span> for <span class="math inline">\(t = 2, \dots, T\)</span> are the terms that actually drive learning: each one matches the learned reverse step to the <strong>true forward posterior</strong> <span class="math inline">\(q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0)\)</span> — i.e.&nbsp;what the noising step “really” did, given that we know the clean image <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0\)</span>.</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(L_0\)</span> is a separate discrete decoder term (see below) handling the final step from continuous <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_1\)</span> back to discrete pixel values.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="the-tractable-forward-posterior" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-tractable-forward-posterior" id="the-tractable-forward-posterior">The tractable forward posterior</h3>
<p>Because <span class="math inline">\(q\)</span> is Gaussian and Markov, conditioning additionally on <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0\)</span> (using Bayes’ rule) gives another Gaussian in closed form:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0) = \mathcal{N}\big(\mathbf{x}_{t-1};\, \tilde{\boldsymbol\mu}_t(\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0),\, \tilde\beta_t \mathbf{I}\big)\]</span></p>
<p>with</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\tilde{\boldsymbol\mu}_t(\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0) = \frac{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\,\beta_t}{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\,(1-\bar\alpha_{t-1})}{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_t, \qquad \tilde\beta_t = \frac{1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}}{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\beta_t\]</span></p>
<p>This <span class="math inline">\(q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0)\)</span> is exactly what <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t)\)</span> is trained to approximate — except the network only has access to <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_t\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>, not the ground-truth <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0\)</span>.</p>
</section>
<section id="reparameterizing-the-mean-predicting-noise-instead-of-the-mean-or-the-image" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="reparameterizing-the-mean-predicting-noise-instead-of-the-mean-or-the-image" id="reparameterizing-the-mean-predicting-noise-instead-of-the-mean-or-the-image">Reparameterizing the mean: predicting noise instead of the mean or the image</h3>
<p>Since both <span class="math inline">\(q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0)\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t)\)</span> are Gaussians, their KL divergence reduces to a squared difference of means (scaled by variance):</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[L_{t-1} = \mathbb{E}_q\left[\frac{1}{2\sigma_t^2}\left\| \tilde{\boldsymbol\mu}_t(\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0) - \boldsymbol\mu_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t,t) \right\|^2\right] + C\]</span></p>
<p>The paper’s key move: instead of directly predicting the mean, substitute the forward-process identity <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}\big(\mathbf{x}_t - \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon\big)\)</span> into <span class="math inline">\(\tilde{\boldsymbol\mu}_t\)</span>. This shows that <span class="math inline">\(\tilde{\boldsymbol\mu}_t\)</span> can be written purely as a function of <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_t\)</span> and the noise <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\epsilon\)</span> that produced it:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\tilde{\boldsymbol\mu}_t(\mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{x}_0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\left(\mathbf{x}_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon\right)\]</span></p>
<p>So instead of asking the network to predict the mean directly, we parameterize it to <strong>predict the noise</strong> <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\epsilon\)</span> that was added:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\boldsymbol\mu_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\left(\mathbf{x}_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\right)\]</span></p>
<p>Plugging this back in, the loss term collapses to a simple noise-prediction MSE:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[L_{t-1} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}_0,\boldsymbol\epsilon}\left[\frac{\beta_t^2}{2\sigma_t^2 \alpha_t (1-\bar\alpha_t)} \left\| \boldsymbol\epsilon - \boldsymbol\epsilon_\theta\big(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon,\; t\big) \right\|^2 \right]\]</span></p>
<p>Intuitively: <strong>the network is a denoiser.</strong> Given a noisy image <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_t\)</span> and the timestep <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>, it predicts the noise vector that was mixed in, and the reverse step subtracts a scaled version of that prediction.</p>
<p>For the variance <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\Sigma_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t,t) = \sigma_t^2\mathbf{I}\)</span>, the paper fixes it (untrained) to either <span class="math inline">\(\sigma_t^2 = \beta_t\)</span> (matching the case where <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)\)</span>) or <span class="math inline">\(\sigma_t^2 = \tilde\beta_t\)</span> (matching a deterministic <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0\)</span>); both gave similar results experimentally.</p>
</section>
<section id="the-final-decoder-term-l_0" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-final-decoder-term-l_0" id="the-final-decoder-term-l_0">The final decoder term <span class="math inline">\(L_0\)</span></h3>
<p>Images are stored as integers in <span class="math inline">\(\{0, \dots, 255\}\)</span>, linearly scaled to <span class="math inline">\([-1, 1]\)</span>. To go from the continuous Gaussian model <span class="math inline">\(p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0\mid\mathbf{x}_1)\)</span> back to a distribution over discrete pixel values, the paper derives an independent discrete decoder for each pixel/channel: it integrates the Gaussian density <span class="math inline">\(\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_0; \boldsymbol\mu_\theta(\mathbf{x}_1,1), \sigma_1^2\mathbf{I})\)</span> over the small interval around each discrete pixel value, ensuring the model’s log-likelihood is a fair comparison against other likelihood-based models operating on discrete data (similar in spirit to PixelCNN++’s discretized logistic mixture decoder).</p>
</section>
<section id="the-simplified-training-objective-l_textsimple" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-simplified-training-objective-l_textsimple" id="the-simplified-training-objective-l_textsimple">The simplified training objective, <span class="math inline">\(L_\text{simple}\)</span></h3>
<p>The weighting term <span class="math inline">\(\frac{\beta_t^2}{2\sigma_t^2\alpha_t(1-\bar\alpha_t)}\)</span> in front of the MSE above down-weights the loss for small <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> (where noise is barely perceptible) relative to large <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>. The paper finds that <strong>dropping this weighting</strong> — training on the plain, unweighted noise-prediction MSE — produces a more effective training target that emphasizes harder, higher-noise denoising tasks and empirically improves sample quality (at the cost of the resulting objective no longer being a valid bound on log-likelihood):</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[L_\text{simple}(\theta) := \mathbb{E}_{t \sim \mathcal U(1,T),\, \mathbf{x}_0,\, \boldsymbol\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)}\Big[\big\| \boldsymbol\epsilon - \boldsymbol\epsilon_\theta(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon,\; t) \big\|^2\Big]\]</span></p>
<p>This is the loss actually used to train the best models in the paper. It is remarkably close to the denoising score-matching objectives used in score-based generative models (see below), which is the connection the paper highlights as one of its main contributions.</p>
</section>
</section>
<section id="sec-training-sampling" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-training-sampling" id="sec-training-sampling">Training and sampling algorithms</h2>
<p><strong>Algorithm 1 — Training</strong></p>
<pre class="text"><code>repeat
    x_0 ~ q(x_0)                                   # sample a real image
    t ~ Uniform({1, ..., T})                       # sample a random timestep
    epsilon ~ N(0, I)                              # sample noise
    take a gradient descent step on
        grad_theta || epsilon - epsilon_theta( sqrt(abar_t) x_0 + sqrt(1-abar_t) epsilon,  t ) ||^2
until converged</code></pre>
<p><strong>Algorithm 2 — Sampling</strong></p>
<pre class="text"><code>x_T ~ N(0, I)
for t = T, ..., 1:
    z ~ N(0, I)   if t &gt; 1,   else z = 0
    x_{t-1} = (1/sqrt(alpha_t)) ( x_t - (beta_t/sqrt(1-abar_t)) epsilon_theta(x_t, t) )  +  sigma_t z
return x_0</code></pre>
<p>Training is a single forward pass per step (efficient — no need to unroll the chain), but sampling is inherently sequential: generating one image requires <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span> (up to 1000) full network evaluations, each depending on the previous. This asymmetry — cheap training, expensive sampling — is the central practical drawback of DDPMs and the main target of later speedups (e.g.&nbsp;DDIM’s non-Markovian samplers, distillation, and fewer-step schedules).</p>

<section id="connection-to-denoising-score-matching-and-langevin-dynamics" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="connection-to-denoising-score-matching-and-langevin-dynamics" id="connection-to-denoising-score-matching-and-langevin-dynamics">Connection to denoising score matching and Langevin dynamics</h2>
<p>A separate line of work (score matching, Song &amp; Ermon 2019) trains a network <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{s}_\theta(\mathbf{x}, \sigma)\)</span> to estimate the <strong>score</strong> <span class="math inline">\(\nabla_\mathbf{x} \log q(\mathbf{x})\)</span> of data perturbed by noise level <span class="math inline">\(\sigma\)</span>, then generates samples via <strong>annealed Langevin dynamics</strong>: iteratively nudging a sample in the direction of increasing log-density, with noise levels annealed from large to small.</p>
<p>The paper observes that the noise-prediction network <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\epsilon_\theta\)</span> trained with <span class="math inline">\(L_\text{simple}\)</span> is, up to a constant scaling factor, estimating the score of the noised data distribution:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[\mathbf{s}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) \approx -\frac{\boldsymbol\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t,t)}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\]</span></p>
<p>and the reverse sampling update in Algorithm 2 is structurally identical to a step of Langevin dynamics using this score estimate, with the <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span> diffusion noise levels playing the role of the annealed noise schedule. This means DDPM training is, in effect, simultaneously training a denoising autoencoder at every noise scale <span class="math inline">\(t = 1,\dots,T\)</span> and generating samples via annealed Langevin-like updates — two previously separate ideas (diffusion models and score matching) turn out to be two views of the same objective. The paper considers this equivalence one of its primary contributions, not just an implementation detail, because the best empirical results come precisely from the parameterization that makes this connection exact.</p>
</section>
<section id="model-architecture" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="model-architecture" id="model-architecture">Model architecture</h2>
<p>The reverse-process network <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\)</span> is a single U-Net shared across all timesteps, conditioned on <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Backbone:</strong> a U-Net in the style of PixelCNN++’s Wide ResNet, adapted with <strong>group normalization</strong> in place of weight normalization.</li>
<li><strong>Multi-resolution structure:</strong> downsampling then upsampling convolutional stages (encoder–decoder with skip connections), each resolution level built from residual blocks.</li>
<li><strong>Self-attention:</strong> attention blocks are inserted at the 16×16 feature-map resolution (between convolutional residual blocks), letting the model capture long-range spatial dependence in addition to local convolutional structure.</li>
<li><strong>Timestep conditioning:</strong> the timestep <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> is embedded using sinusoidal position embeddings (the same construction used for positional encoding in Transformers) and injected into every residual block, so a single network can specialize its denoising behavior to the current noise level.</li>
<li><strong>Weight sharing across resolutions:</strong> because <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_t\)</span> always has the same dimensionality as <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0\)</span>, the same network architecture handles every timestep — there is no need for <span class="math inline">\(T\)</span> separate networks.</li>
</ul>

<section id="reverse-process-parameterization-and-objective-ablation" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="reverse-process-parameterization-and-objective-ablation" id="reverse-process-parameterization-and-objective-ablation">Reverse-process parameterization and objective ablation</h3>
<p>The paper systematically ablates:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li>What the network predicts — the raw mean <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\mu_\theta\)</span>, the clean image <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0\)</span>, or the noise <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\epsilon\)</span>.</li>
<li>Whether <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\Sigma_\theta\)</span> is fixed or learned.</li>
<li>Whether the loss is the properly weighted variational bound <span class="math inline">\(L_\text{vlb}\)</span> or the unweighted <span class="math inline">\(L_\text{simple}\)</span>.</li>
</ol>
<p>The clear winner for sample quality is <strong><span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\epsilon\)</span>-prediction with the unweighted <span class="math inline">\(L_\text{simple}\)</span> loss and fixed variance</strong> — this is the configuration used for the headline CIFAR-10/LSUN/CelebA-HQ results. Predicting <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_0\)</span> directly (rather than <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\epsilon\)</span>) trains far less stably and produces noticeably worse samples, especially early in training. Training with the fully weighted <span class="math inline">\(L_\text{vlb}\)</span> gives better log-likelihoods (as expected, since it is the actual variational bound) but worse perceptual sample quality than <span class="math inline">\(L_\text{simple}\)</span> — a quality/likelihood trade-off that the paper flags as an open question.</p>
</section>
<section id="likelihood-lossless-codelength" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="likelihood-lossless-codelength" id="likelihood-lossless-codelength">Likelihood / lossless codelength</h3>
<p>Despite excellent samples, DDPM’s negative log-likelihood on CIFAR-10 (≤ 3.75 bits/dim under the best variant) is not competitive with dedicated likelihood-based models such as autoregressive PixelCNN-family models or normalizing flows, which achieve markedly lower bits/dim. The paper investigates <em>why</em> using a <strong>rate–distortion analysis</strong>: it decomposes the lossless codelength into contributions from each reverse step and plots how quickly image <em>distortion</em> (reconstruction quality) improves as more <em>rate</em> (bits) is spent decoding. This shows that the overwhelming majority of the model’s bits are spent describing imperceptible, high-frequency image details, while the perceptually important large-scale structure of the image is captured using only a small fraction of the total codelength. This explains the apparent paradox: a model can produce excellent-looking samples while still not achieving a good likelihood score, because likelihood cares equally about every bit, including bits humans cannot perceive.</p>
</section>
<section id="progressive-generation-as-generalized-autoregressive-decoding" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="progressive-generation-as-generalized-autoregressive-decoding" id="progressive-generation-as-generalized-autoregressive-decoding">Progressive generation as generalized autoregressive decoding</h3>
<p>Because sampling proceeds through <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_T \to \mathbf{x}_{T-1} \to \dots \to \mathbf{x}_0\)</span>, one can inspect intermediate reconstructions <span class="math inline">\(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0\)</span> predicted at each step along the way. The paper shows that this progressive decoding reveals large-scale image structure (rough shapes, layout) emerging first, with fine detail filled in only at the final steps — analogous to autoregressive image models decoding pixel-by-pixel, except that DDPM’s implicit “ordering” of information is over <em>spatial frequency / scale</em> rather than a fixed raster-scan pixel order, which the authors argue is a strict generalization of what autoregressive decoding can express.</p>
</section>
<section id="interpolation" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="interpolation" id="interpolation">Interpolation</h3>
<p>The paper also demonstrates latent-space interpolation: encode two real images to a shared intermediate noise level <span class="math inline">\(\mathbf{x}_t\)</span> (via the forward process), linearly interpolate the two noised latents, and run the reverse process back down to <span class="math inline">\(t=0\)</span>. This produces smooth, semantically plausible interpolations between the two source images, showing that intermediate diffusion latents encode meaningful structure rather than being pure noise.</p>

<section id="limitations-and-legacy" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="limitations-and-legacy" id="limitations-and-legacy">Limitations and legacy</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sampling cost.</strong> <span class="math inline">\(T=1000\)</span> sequential network evaluations per sample is orders of magnitude slower than a single GAN forward pass. This motivated a large body of follow-up work on faster samplers (DDIM, samplers based on SDE/ODE solvers, distillation).</li>
<li><strong>Likelihood vs.&nbsp;sample quality trade-off.</strong> The best-sounding objective for samples (<span class="math inline">\(L_\text{simple}\)</span>) is not the best for likelihood (<span class="math inline">\(L_\text{vlb}\)</span>), and the paper leaves reconciling this as an open problem — later work (e.g.&nbsp;“Improved DDPM,” Nichol &amp; Dhariwal 2021) directly addresses learned variances and hybrid objectives to close this gap.</li>
<li><strong>Fixed, hand-designed variance schedule.</strong> <span class="math inline">\(\beta_t\)</span> is fixed and linear; later work learns or otherwise improves the noise schedule.</li>
<li><strong>Legacy.</strong> This paper, together with concurrent score-based generative modeling work, is the direct ancestor of the modern text-to-image diffusion pipeline (GLIDE, DALL·E 2, Imagen, Stable Diffusion), all of which reuse the same forward/reverse Gaussian diffusion formalism and <span class="math inline">\(\boldsymbol\epsilon\)</span>-prediction training objective derived here, typically combined with classifier(-free) guidance and latent-space diffusion for efficiency.</li>
</ul>
</section>
