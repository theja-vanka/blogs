<section id="visual-instruction-tuning-llava" class="level1">

<p><img src="/blogs/posts/generative-ai/llava/llava.png" class="img-fluid"></p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p><strong>Paper:</strong> Visual Instruction Tuning <strong>Authors:</strong> Haotian Liu¹, Chunyuan Li², Qingyang Wu³, Yong Jae Lee¹ ¹University of Wisconsin–Madison, ²Microsoft Research, ³Columbia University <strong>Venue:</strong> NeurIPS 2023 (Oral) <strong>arXiv:</strong> <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.08485">2304.08485</a> (v1: 17 Apr 2023, v2: 11 Dec 2023) <strong>PDF:</strong> <a href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/2304.08485" class="uri">https://arxiv.org/pdf/2304.08485</a> <strong>Project page:</strong> <a href="https://llava-vl.github.io" class="uri">https://llava-vl.github.io</a> <strong>License:</strong> CC BY 4.0</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This guide is a comprehensive, section-by-section walkthrough of the paper that introduced <strong>LLaVA (Large Language and Vision Assistant)</strong> — the first system to apply <em>instruction tuning</em>, previously an NLP-only technique, to the joint language-image domain. It reorganizes the paper’s content for study purposes, reproduces its key tables and equations, and adds diagrams (as Mermaid flowcharts) to visualize the architecture and pipelines that the paper describes only in prose and a single static figure.</p>
<hr>
<section id="abstract" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="abstract" id="abstract">Abstract</h2>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>Instruction tuning large language models (LLMs) using machine-generated instruction-following data has been shown to improve zero-shot capabilities on new tasks, but the idea is less explored in the multimodal field. We present the first attempt to use language-only GPT-4 to generate multimodal language-image instruction-following data. By instruction tuning on such generated data, we introduce LLaVA: Large Language and Vision Assistant, an end-to-end trained large multimodal model that connects a vision encoder and an LLM for general-purpose visual and language understanding. To facilitate future research on visual instruction following, we construct two evaluation benchmarks with diverse and challenging application-oriented tasks. Our experiments show that LLaVA demonstrates impressive multimodal chat abilities, sometimes exhibiting the behaviors of multimodal GPT-4 on unseen images/instructions, and yields a 85.1% relative score compared with GPT-4 on a synthetic multimodal instruction-following dataset. When fine-tuned on Science QA, the synergy of LLaVA and GPT-4 achieves a new state-of-the-art accuracy of 92.53%. We make GPT-4 generated visual instruction tuning data, our model, and code publicly available.</p>
</blockquote>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="tldr-key-contributions" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="tldr-key-contributions" id="tldr-key-contributions">TL;DR — Key Contributions</h2>
<p>The paper makes four concrete contributions:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong>Multimodal instruction-following data.</strong> A pipeline that reformats existing image-caption pairs into instruction-following conversations by prompting text-only GPT-4/ChatGPT with symbolic (textual) representations of images — captions and bounding boxes — rather than the images themselves.</li>
<li><strong>A large multimodal model (LMM): LLaVA.</strong> A simple architecture connecting a frozen CLIP ViT-L/14 vision encoder to the Vicuna LLM via a single trainable linear projection layer, trained end-to-end on the generated data.</li>
<li><strong>Two new evaluation benchmarks: LLaVA-Bench (COCO) and LLaVA-Bench (In-the-Wild).</strong> Designed to stress-test multimodal instruction-following and chat ability, scored automatically using GPT-4 as a judge.</li>
<li><strong>Open-source release</strong> of the 158K-sample instruction dataset, model weights, training code, and a chat demo.</li>
</ol>
<p>The headline numbers: <strong>85.1%</strong> relative score vs.&nbsp;text-only GPT-4 on the synthetic instruction-following benchmark, and a new state-of-the-art <strong>92.53%</strong> accuracy on ScienceQA when LLaVA’s predictions are ensembled with GPT-4 acting as a judge.</p>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="introduction" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="introduction" id="introduction">1. Introduction</h2>
<p>Humans understand the world through multiple channels — vision and language chief among them — because each channel captures concepts the other cannot as efficiently. AI research has long pursued a general-purpose assistant that can follow multimodal vision-and-language instructions to complete real-world tasks.</p>
<p>The paper frames prior progress along two separate tracks that, until this work, had not been fully merged:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Vision-only foundation models.</strong> A wave of language-augmented vision models (CLIP, GLIP, BLIP, DALL-E 2, Latent Diffusion, Imagen, GLIGEN, etc.) achieved strong open-world performance on classification, detection, segmentation, captioning, and generation. However, each task is typically solved by a <em>separate</em> model with a <em>fixed interface</em> — language is used only to describe content, not to flexibly instruct the system, so interactivity and adaptability to user intent are limited.</li>
<li><strong>Instruction-tuned LLMs.</strong> ChatGPT and GPT-4 demonstrated that language can serve as a <em>universal interface</em>: task instructions expressed in natural language can steer a single end-to-end model to solve arbitrary tasks. Open-source efforts — LLaMA, Alpaca, Vicuna, GPT-4-LLM — replicated this instruction-following ability using machine-generated instruction data, but remained strictly <strong>text-only</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p>LLaVA’s core idea is to <strong>extend instruction tuning into the language-image multimodal space</strong> — the first attempt of its kind — to move toward a general-purpose visual assistant. The four contributions listed in the TL;DR above are given as the paper’s explicit list of contributions.</p>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="related-work" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="related-work" id="related-work">2. Related Work</h2>
<p>The paper positions itself against two bodies of work:</p>
<p><strong>Multimodal instruction-following agents.</strong> Existing systems fall into two categories:</p>
<ul>
<li><em>(i) End-to-end trained, task-specific models</em> — e.g., vision-and-language navigation agents, Habitat embodied agents, and InstructPix2Pix (which edits images given a natural-language instruction). Each is built and trained for one narrow task.</li>
<li><em>(ii) Tool-orchestration systems</em> that coordinate multiple existing models via an LLM or a framework like LangChain — e.g., Visual ChatGPT, X-GPT, MM-REACT, VisProg, ViperGPT. These “glue” pretrained vision tools together using an LLM as a controller/orchestrator, rather than training one unified model end-to-end.</li>
</ul>
<p>LLaVA instead pursues an <strong>end-to-end trained, single multimodal model</strong> that generalizes across multiple tasks — distinct from both categories.</p>
<p><strong>Instruction tuning.</strong> In NLP, instruction tuning (InstructGPT/ChatGPT, FLAN-T5, FLAN-PaLM, OPT-IML) reliably improves zero-/few-shot generalization of LLMs by fine-tuning on instruction-formatted data. The paper argues it is natural to import this idea into vision-language modeling. It notes prior multimodal models — Flamingo (described as “the GPT-3 moment” for multimodal, due to strong zero-shot/in-context learning), BLIP-2, FROMAGe, KOSMOS-1, PaLM-E (for embodied AI), OpenFlamingo, and LLaMA-Adapter — as precedents that connect vision and language models, but observes that <strong>none of them were explicitly tuned on vision-language instruction-following data</strong>, so their multimodal task performance lags behind their language-only performance. LLaVA is presented as filling exactly this gap.</p>
<p>The paper also draws a sharp distinction between <em>visual instruction tuning</em> (this paper — improving a model’s ability to follow instructions) and <em>visual prompt tuning</em> (a parameter-efficient adaptation technique, unrelated in goal).</p>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="gpt-assisted-visual-instruction-data-generation" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="gpt-assisted-visual-instruction-data-generation" id="gpt-assisted-visual-instruction-data-generation">3. GPT-Assisted Visual Instruction Data Generation</h2>
<p>This is arguably the paper’s most novel methodological contribution: a recipe for manufacturing multimodal instruction data <strong>without any new human annotation</strong>, by exploiting a <em>text-only</em> GPT-4/ChatGPT as a “teacher.”</p>
<section id="the-core-problem" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-core-problem" id="the-core-problem">The core problem</h3>
<p>Public image-text pair datasets (CC, LAION) are abundant, but <em>instruction-following</em> multimodal data is scarce because it is expensive and hard to define via crowdsourcing.</p>
</section>
<section id="naive-baseline-and-why-its-insufficient" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="naive-baseline-and-why-its-insufficient" id="naive-baseline-and-why-its-insufficient">Naive baseline (and why it’s insufficient)</h3>
<p>Given an image <strong>X</strong>ᵥ and its caption <strong>X</strong>c, one could trivially synthesize an instruction sample by generating a question <strong>X</strong>q (“describe this image”) and pairing it with the caption as the answer:</p>
<pre><code>Human: Xq Xv &lt;STOP&gt;
Assistant: Xc &lt;STOP&gt;</code></pre>
<p>This is cheap but <strong>lacks diversity and in-depth reasoning</strong> in both instructions and responses — every sample looks the same.</p>
</section>
<section id="the-gpt-4-as-teacher-solution" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-gpt-4-as-teacher-solution" id="the-gpt-4-as-teacher-solution">The GPT-4-as-teacher solution</h3>
<p>Since GPT-4/ChatGPT are <em>text-only</em> (in this paper’s setting), the image itself cannot be shown to them. Instead, the image is encoded into two <strong>symbolic text representations</strong>:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong>Captions</strong> — multiple independent captions describing the scene from different perspectives.</li>
<li><strong>Bounding boxes</strong> — object class + normalized spatial coordinates for every detected object (e.g., <code>person: [0.681, 0.242, 0.774, 0.694]</code>).</li>
</ol>
<p>Feeding these symbolic representations (plus a handful of hand-written seed examples used as in-context-learning demonstrations) into GPT-4 lets it “imagine” the image and generate rich, varied instruction-response data — while never seeing the pixels. <section id="visual-instruction-tuning" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="visual-instruction-tuning" id="visual-instruction-tuning">4. Visual Instruction Tuning</h2>
<section id="architecture" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="architecture" id="architecture">4.1 Architecture</h3>
<p><strong>Goal:</strong> combine the instruction-following strength of a pretrained LLM with the visual representation power of a pretrained vision encoder, as simply as possible.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Language model f_φ(·):</strong> <a href="https://github.com/lm-sys/FastChat">Vicuna</a> — chosen because, among publicly available checkpoints (Alpaca, Vicuna, GPT-4-LLM), it had the best language instruction-following quality at the time.</li>
<li><strong>Vision encoder g(·):</strong> the pretrained <strong>CLIP ViT-L/14</strong> visual encoder, which converts an input image <strong>X</strong>ᵥ into visual features <strong>Z</strong>ᵥ = g(<strong>X</strong>ᵥ). Both the <em>grid features before and after</em> the last Transformer layer were tried experimentally.</li>
<li><strong>Connector:</strong> a single <strong>trainable linear projection matrix W</strong> maps the visual feature space into the <em>same dimensionality as the LLM’s word-embedding space</em>, producing a sequence of visual tokens <strong>H</strong>ᵥ:</li>
</ul>
<p><span class="math display">\[
{\bf H}_{v} = {\bf W} \cdot {\bf Z}_{v}, \quad \text{with} \quad {\bf Z}_{v} = g({\bf X}_{v}) \tag{1}
\]</span></p>
<p>The resulting <strong>H</strong>ᵥ tokens are treated exactly like language embedding tokens and injected directly into the LLM’s input sequence alongside the tokenized instruction.</p>
<p>The paper is explicit that this projection is <strong>deliberately simple</strong> (“lightweight”), chosen to allow fast iteration on the data side of the research; it explicitly calls out <strong>gated cross-attention</strong> (as in Flamingo) and the <strong>Q-Former</strong> (as in BLIP-2) as more sophisticated alternatives left to future work. (This simple-linear-projection choice is exactly what the paper’s own 2023 follow-up, “Improved Baselines with Visual Instruction Tuning” / LLaVA-1.5, revisits with an MLP projector.)</p>

<section id="training" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="training" id="training">4.2 Training</h3>
<p>For each image <strong>X</strong>ᵥ, the paper generates <strong>multi-turn conversation data</strong> — a sequence of T question/answer turn pairs (X¹q, X¹a, …, X^T_q, X^T_a). All turns are concatenated into one long training sequence, and the <em>instruction</em> at turn <em>t</em> is defined as:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
{\bf X}^t_{\text{instruct}} =
\begin{cases}
\text{Randomly choose } [{\bf X}^1_q, {\bf X}_v] \text{ or } [{\bf X}_v, {\bf X}^1_q], &amp; t = 1 \\
{\bf X}^t_q, &amp; t &gt; 1
\end{cases} \tag{2}
\]</span></p>
<p>In words: on the <em>first</em> turn, the image token can appear either before or after the first question (randomized), while on all subsequent turns only the new question text is added — the image is only “introduced” once.</p>
<p>This produces the <strong>unified training sequence format</strong> (reproduced from Table 2):</p>
<pre><code>X_system-message &lt;STOP&gt;
Human: X¹_instruct &lt;STOP&gt;  Assistant: X¹_a &lt;STOP&gt;
Human: X²_instruct &lt;STOP&gt;  Assistant: X²_a &lt;STOP&gt;  ⋯</code></pre>
<p>The system message and <code>&lt;STOP&gt;</code> token follow the <strong>Vicuna-v0</strong> conversation template, with <code>&lt;STOP&gt;</code> = <code>###</code>. Critically, <strong>only the green (assistant-answer) tokens contribute to the training loss</strong> — the model is trained autoregressively to predict the assistant’s answers (and where to stop), not to reproduce the human’s questions.</p>
<p>Formally, for a sequence of length L, the model maximizes the likelihood of the target answer tokens conditioned on the image and all preceding instruction/answer tokens:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
p({\bf X}_a \mid {\bf X}_v, {\bf X}_{\text{instruct}}) = \prod_{i=1}^{L} p_\theta(x_i \mid {\bf X}_v, {\bf X}_{\text{instruct},&lt;i}, {\bf X}_{a,&lt;i}) \tag{3}
\]</span></p>
<p>where θ are the trainable parameters and the subscript <code>&lt;i</code> denotes all tokens before position <em>i</em> in prior turns.</p>

<h4 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="two-stage-instruction-tuning-procedure">Two-stage instruction-tuning procedure</h4>
<div class="cell" data-eval="true" data-layout-align="center">
<div class="cell-output-display">

<div>

<ul>
<li><strong>Stage 1: Pre-training for Feature Alignment.</strong>
<ul>
<li>CC3M is filtered down to <strong>595K</strong> image-text pairs (CC-595K) — see <a href="#appendix-e-data">Appendix E</a> for the filtering method.</li>
<li>Each pair becomes a single-turn “briefly describe this image” instruction sample using the naive expansion method from Section 3.</li>
<li><strong>Both the vision encoder and the LLM are frozen.</strong> Only the projection matrix <strong>W</strong> is trained, maximizing the likelihood in Eq. (3) with θ = W.</li>
<li>Intuition: this trains <strong>W</strong> to act as a <em>compatible visual tokenizer</em> for the already-frozen, already-capable LLM — teaching the projector to speak the LLM’s “language,” not teaching the LLM anything new.</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong>Stage 2: Fine-tuning End-to-End.</strong>
<ul>
<li>The vision encoder <strong>remains frozen</strong>; now <strong>both W and the LLM weights φ</strong> are updated (θ = {W, φ}).</li>
<li>Two concrete use cases are trained separately:
<ul>
<li><strong>Multimodal Chatbot:</strong> fine-tuned on the full 158K instruction dataset (conversation data is multi-turn; detailed description and complex reasoning are single-turn; all three are uniformly sampled during training).</li>
<li><strong>Science QA:</strong> fine-tuned on the ScienceQA benchmark, framed as single-turn — the question + context is the instruction, and the reasoning + final answer is the target response.</li>
</ul></li>
</ul></li>
</ul>

<section id="quantitative-evaluation-methodology" class="level4">
<h4 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="quantitative-evaluation-methodology">Quantitative evaluation methodology</h4>
<p>Because no established multimodal instruction-following benchmark existed, the paper designs its own <strong>GPT-4-as-judge protocol</strong>:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li>Build triplets of (image, ground-truth textual description, question).</li>
<li>Generate a <strong>candidate answer</strong> from the model under test (e.g., LLaVA), using the actual image.</li>
<li>Generate a <strong>reference answer</strong> from <em>text-only</em> GPT-4, using only the ground-truth textual description (an approximate “theoretical upper bound,” since GPT-4 never sees the image either).</li>
<li>Feed the question, the textual description (as a stand-in for “what the judge can see”), and both answers to a <em>judge</em> (text-only GPT-4), which scores each response 1–10 on helpfulness, relevance, accuracy, and level of detail, with a justification.</li>
<li>Report the candidate’s score <strong>as a percentage relative to the reference GPT-4 score</strong> — this is the “relative score” metric quoted throughout the paper (e.g., the headline 85.1%).</li>
</ol>

<section id="limitations-table-6" class="level4">
<h4 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="limitations-table-6">Limitations (Table 6)</h4>
<p>Two hard failure cases are highlighted, both requiring capabilities LLaVA lacks:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>ICHIRAN Ramen photo:</strong> correctly naming the restaurant requires broad world/multilingual knowledge and possibly internet retrieval; correctly listing the side dishes requires fine-grained recognition.</li>
<li><strong>Filled fridge photo:</strong> identifying the yogurt brand requires reading small high-resolution text; more strikingly, <strong>LLaVA incorrectly answers “yes” when asked if strawberry-flavored yogurt is present</strong>, even though the fridge only contains plain yogurt <em>and separately</em> strawberries. The paper interprets this as LLaVA sometimes perceiving the image as a <strong>“bag of patches”</strong> — i.e., recognizing the objects present but failing to bind attributes to the correct object (a known weakness of the simple linear-projection + patch-token architecture).</li>
</ul>

<section id="scienceqa" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="scienceqa" id="scienceqa">5.2 ScienceQA</h3>
<p><a href="https://scienceqa.github.io/">ScienceQA</a> is a large-scale multimodal multiple-choice benchmark: <strong>21K questions</strong>, 3 subjects, 26 topics, 127 categories, 379 skills, with train/val/test splits of <strong>12,726 / 4,241 / 4,241</strong>. Each question is paired with a lecture/explanation and (usually) either a natural-language or image context.</p>

<p>Interpretations offered in the paper:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Feature layer:</strong> the layer <em>before</em> CLIP’s final Transformer layer works better — hypothesized to retain more localized/detailed properties, whereas the very last layer focuses more on global/abstract image properties.</li>
<li><strong>Answer-vs-reasoning order:</strong> answer-first converges to a slightly higher final accuracy (89.77%), while reasoning-first (chain-of-thought style) converges <em>faster</em> (reaching the same number in half the epochs) but plateaus at the same ceiling — i.e., CoT-style ordering helps convergence speed, not final quality.</li>
<li><strong>Pre-training matters a lot:</strong> skipping Stage 1 costs <strong>5.11 accuracy points</strong> — strong evidence that the feature-alignment pre-training stage is doing real work, not just serving as a formality.</li>
<li><strong>Model scale matters:</strong> the 13B model beats the 7B model by ~1 point, consistent with general LLM scaling trends.</li>
</ul>

<section id="conclusion" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="conclusion" id="conclusion">6. Conclusion</h2>
<p>The paper’s own summary: it demonstrates the effectiveness of <em>visual instruction tuning</em>, presents an automatic pipeline for generating language-image instruction data, and trains LLaVA — achieving new SoTA on ScienceQA when fine-tuned there, and strong visual-chat ability when fine-tuned on multimodal chat data. It also contributes the first benchmark suite explicitly targeting multimodal instruction-following capability. The authors describe this as an <em>initial step</em>, focused on real-life tasks, and point readers to a follow-up paper — “Improved Baselines with Visual Instruction Tuning” (LLaVA-1.5) — for stronger quantitative results on academic benchmarks.</p>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="appendix-a.-broader-impact" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="appendix-a.-broader-impact" id="appendix-a.-broader-impact">Appendix A. Broader Impact</h2>
<p>The authors discuss risks and mitigations specific to releasing a general-purpose visual assistant:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Malicious input:</strong> mitigated via (1) the OpenAI Filter API on text instructions, and (2) an NSFW filter on uploaded images.</li>
<li><strong>Hallucination:</strong> like text-only LLMs, LLaVA can generate outputs not grounded in the actual input — a particular concern in high-stakes domains like medicine.</li>
<li><strong>Biases:</strong> inherited from both the vision encoder (CLIP) and the language decoder (LLaMA/Vicuna).</li>
<li><strong>Energy consumption:</strong> not a major concern at LLaVA’s scale (small pretraining set), but flagged as a future concern if scaled up (e.g., to a 65B-parameter LLaMA backbone).</li>
<li><strong>Evaluation complexities:</strong> their GPT-4-based evaluation is consistent within their study, but its generality/robustness across other settings is untested; aspects like hallucination degree and fine-grained visual understanding are not yet well covered by their benchmarks.</li>
</ul>
<p>The authors conclude that the research-community benefits of releasing LLaVA outweigh these risks, since open release enables collaborative improvement and mitigation.</p>
</section>

<section id="appendix-e.-data" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="appendix-e.-data" id="appendix-e.-data">Appendix E. Data</h2>
<p><strong>CC3M filtering methodology (→ CC-595K):</strong> noun phrases are extracted from every CC3M caption via spaCy, and their frequencies counted across the whole dataset.</p>
<ul>
<li>Noun phrases occurring <strong>fewer than 3 times</strong> are dropped (too rare / idiosyncratic to be useful concept coverage).</li>
<li>For noun phrases occurring <strong>more than 100 times</strong>, only a <strong>random subset of 100</strong> captions containing that phrase is kept (to avoid massive redundancy for common concepts).</li>
<li>Captions are added starting from the <em>least-frequent remaining</em> noun phrases, building up the final pool.</li>
<li>This filtering reduces the noun-phrase vocabulary from <strong>108,182 unique noun phrases (full CC3M)</strong> down to <strong>31,423 unique noun phrases (filtered)</strong>, while shrinking the pair count to <strong>~595K</strong> — the paper’s own summary is that the filtered set trades off total pair volume for <strong>better balance of concept coverage</strong> (avoiding the raw dataset’s long tail of near-duplicate high-frequency captions).</li>
</ul>
<p><em>(Figure 7 in the paper is a log-log plot of noun-phrase frequency before/after filtering; not independently linked here — see the <a href="https://arxiv.org/html/2304.08485v2#A5">paper’s appendix online</a>.)</em></p>

<section id="appendix-f.-prompts" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="appendix-f.-prompts" id="appendix-f.-prompts">Appendix F. Prompts</h2>
<p>The paper documents its exact GPT-4 prompting template (Table 13). The <strong>system prompt</strong> used to generate the “conversation”-type data reads (reproduced verbatim):</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p><em>“You are an AI visual assistant, and you are seeing a single image. What you see are provided with five sentences, describing the same image you are looking at. Answer all questions as you are seeing the image.”</em></p>
<p><em>“Design a conversation between you and a person asking about this photo. The answers should be in a tone that a visual AI assistant is seeing the image and answering the question. Ask diverse questions and give corresponding answers.”</em></p>
<p><em>“Include questions asking about the visual content of the image, including the object types, counting the objects, object actions, object locations, relative positions between objects, etc. Only include questions that have definite answers: (1) one can see the content in the image that the question asks about and can answer confidently; (2) one can determine confidently from the image that it is not in the image. Do not ask any question that cannot be answered confidently.”</em></p>
<p><em>“Also include complex questions that are relevant to the content in the image, for example, asking about background knowledge of the objects in the image, asking to discuss about events happening in the image, etc. Again, do not ask about uncertain details. Provide detailed answers when answering complex questions. For example, give detailed examples or reasoning steps to make the content more convincing and well-organized. You can include multiple paragraphs if necessary.”</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This system message is followed programmatically by a handful of <strong>few-shot in-context examples</strong> (Tables 15 and 16 in the paper give two full worked examples — a fire-hydrant-in-snow photo, and a skier photo — each showing 3–4 turns of Q&amp;A the model should imitate in style and depth), and finally the <em>actual</em> query (captions + boxes for the target image) is appended as the final user turn. The paper notes that prompts for the other two data types (detailed description, complex reasoning) follow the same pattern but are omitted from the main text for space; readers are pointed to the released codebase.</p>

<section id="key-takeaways" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="key-takeaways" id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>The central methodological insight of this paper is that <strong>you can bootstrap high-quality multimodal instruction data purely from a text-only LLM</strong>, by describing images symbolically (captions + boxes) rather than requiring a multimodal teacher model — this sidesteps the chicken-and-egg problem of needing a good multimodal model to generate training data for a good multimodal model. Architecturally, the paper shows that a <strong>deliberately minimal design</strong> (frozen CLIP + frozen/then-unfrozen Vicuna + a single linear layer) is sufficient to reach near-SoTA results when the <em>training data</em> is good, which is the paper’s implicit thesis: <strong>data quality and instruction diversity, not architectural complexity, were the main bottleneck</strong> for multimodal instruction-following at the time. The two-stage training recipe (align first, then fine-tune end-to-end) and the “GPT-4 as judge” ensembling trick are both reused in numerous follow-up works, including the paper’s own sequel, LLaVA-1.5.</p>
