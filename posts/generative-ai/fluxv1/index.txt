<section id="abstract" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="abstract" id="abstract">Abstract</h2>
<p>FLUX.1 is a diffusion-based text-to-image generation model developed by Black Forest Labs, designed to achieve faithful text-image alignment while maintaining high image quality and diversity. FLUX is considered state-of-the-art in text-to-image generation, outperforming popular models such as Midjourney, DALL·E 3, Stable Diffusion 3 (SD3), and SDXL. Although publicly available as open source, the authors have not released official technical documentation detailing the model’s architecture or training setup. This report summarizes an extensive reverse-engineering effort aimed at demystifying FLUX’s architecture directly from its source code, to support its adoption as a backbone for future research and development.</p>
</section>
</section>
<section id="background" class="level1">
<h1>Background</h1>
<p>To understand FLUX’s design choices, it helps to walk through the lineage of models and methods that shaped it: generative modeling broadly, diffusion models specifically, Stable Diffusion’s evolution, Rectified Flow as a training paradigm, and the Transformer architecture that FLUX adopts as its full backbone.</p>
<section id="ml-based-image-generation" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="ml-based-image-generation" id="ml-based-image-generation">ML-Based Image Generation</h2>
<p>Image generation, in the machine-learning sense, means learning to sample novel images from a distribution that mimics some source dataset. A common strategy is to learn a mapping from the complex data distribution (natural images) to something simple and easy to sample from, typically an isotropic Gaussian — and then invert that mapping at generation time.</p>
<p>Several major families implement this idea:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)</strong> — learn an encoder/decoder pair with a regularized latent space.</li>
<li><strong>Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)</strong> — pit a generator against a discriminator in an adversarial game.</li>
<li><strong>Normalizing Flows (NFs)</strong> — learn an invertible, exact-likelihood transformation between data and noise.</li>
<li><strong>Diffusion Models (DMs)</strong> — learn to reverse a gradual noising process.</li>
</ul>
<p>Among these families, text-to-image models — DALL·E, Stable Diffusion, and FLUX among them — have become the dominant paradigm for producing high-quality images from natural-language prompts.</p>
</section>
<section id="diffusion-models" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="diffusion-models" id="diffusion-models">Diffusion Models</h2>
<p>Diffusion Models (DMs) are today’s state-of-the-art approach for text-conditioned image generation. Training corrupts clean images with Gaussian noise according to a timestep-dependent schedule, and the network learns to undo that corruption. Early diffusion models used U-Net backbones — convolutional layers augmented with attention for image–text alignment — but newer models such as SD3 and FLUX.1 have moved to fully Transformer-based denoisers, trading convolutional inductive bias for scalability and better long-range context modeling.</p>
<p>In the most common formulation, the network is trained to predict the (normalized) noise <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon\)</span> that was added to a clean sample, using a simple reconstruction loss:</p>
<p><span id="eq-eps-loss"><span class="math display">\[
\mathcal{L}_{\epsilon} = \mathbb{E}_{x_t, \epsilon, t}\Big[\|\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) - \epsilon\|^2\Big]
\tag{1}\]</span></span></p>
<p>where the noisy sample at timestep <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> is</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
x_t = \sqrt{\alpha_t}\, x + \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\, \epsilon,\qquad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)
\]</span></p>
<p>and <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> denotes the clean image. At inference time, the model starts from pure Gaussian noise and iteratively denoises it into a realistic image (<a href="#eq-eps-loss" class="quarto-xref">Equation&nbsp;1</a>). Compared to GANs’ adversarial objective, this reconstruction-based training is more stable and scales better to high resolutions, while also enabling fine-grained control for tasks like super-resolution, inpainting, and text-conditioned synthesis.</p>
</section>
<section id="stable-diffusion" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="stable-diffusion" id="stable-diffusion">Stable Diffusion</h2>
<p>Stable Diffusion is a family of <strong>Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs)</strong>: rather than diffusing directly in pixel space, images are first compressed into a lower-dimensional latent space by a pretrained autoencoder, the diffusion process runs in that latent space, and a decoder maps the final latent back to pixels. This dramatically reduces compute cost while preserving visual fidelity.</p>
<p>The lineage, as covered in the paper:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>SD 1.x (1.0–1.5):</strong> frozen CLIP text encoder + U-Net latent denoiser, trained on LAION-2B subsets. Open-source release drove rapid adoption and fine-tuning.</li>
<li><strong>SD 2.1:</strong> switched to OpenCLIP, trained at higher resolution (768×768), improving structure and fidelity.</li>
<li><strong>SDXL:</strong> two-stage base + refiner architecture, better handling of complex prompts, closer to commercial-grade realism.</li>
<li><strong>SD-Turbo:</strong> near real-time generation via Adversarial Diffusion Distillation (ADD) — a student model distilled from a teacher (SD2.1 or SDXL) to require far fewer steps.</li>
<li><strong>SD3:</strong> adopts a diffusion-transformer backbone and multimodal training, integrating text and image understanding jointly — a direct architectural precursor to FLUX (see <a href="#sec-transformer" class="quarto-xref">Section&nbsp;3.3</a>).</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="sec-rectified-flows" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-rectified-flows" id="sec-rectified-flows">Rectified Flows</h2>
<p>Rectified Flow (RF) is the training paradigm FLUX actually uses instead of standard DM noise-prediction. It’s a training scheme, not an architectural change, but it matters here because it shapes what the FLUX transformer is trained to predict. RF builds on the Flow Matching framework and replaces stochastic denoising with a deterministic vector field.</p>
<p>Standard diffusion models train <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta\)</span> to predict the noise added to a sample (<a href="#eq-eps-loss" class="quarto-xref">Equation&nbsp;1</a>). Rectified Flow instead trains a model <span class="math inline">\(v_\theta\)</span> to predict the <strong>velocity</strong> — a vector pointing from a noise sample toward a data sample along a straight interpolation path:</p>
<p><span id="eq-rf-loss"><span class="math display">\[
\mathcal{L}_v = \mathbb{E}_{x_0, x_1, t}\Big[\|v_\theta(x_t, t) - (x_1 - x_0)\|^2\Big]
\tag{2}\]</span></span></p>
<p>where</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
x_t = (1-t)\,x_0 + t\,x_1,\qquad x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I),\quad x_1 \sim p_{\text{data}}
\]</span></p>
<div class="callout callout-style-default callout-warning callout-titled">
<div class="callout-header d-flex align-content-center">
<div class="callout-icon-container">
<i class="callout-icon"></i>
</div>
<div class="callout-title-container flex-fill">
<span class="screen-reader-only">Warning</span>Notation flip
</div>
</div>
<div class="callout-body-container callout-body">
<p>In Rectified Flow (<a href="#eq-rf-loss" class="quarto-xref">Equation&nbsp;2</a>), <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> denotes <strong>noise</strong> and <span class="math inline">\(x_1\)</span> denotes the <strong>data point</strong> — the opposite of standard DM convention, where <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> is the clean image. This flip resurfaces later in FLUX’s own sampling notation (<a href="#sec-sampling-pipeline" class="quarto-xref">Section&nbsp;3.2</a>), so keep it in mind when comparing equations across sections.</p>
</div>
</div>
<p>By defining a deterministic transport path from noise to data, RF avoids explicit noise schedules and stochastic sampling, solving instead a simple ODE with a learned velocity field — generally faster and more stable to train and sample from than classic diffusion.</p>
</section>
<section id="transformers" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="transformers" id="transformers">Transformers</h2>
<p>Transformers, originally built for NLP, introduced the self-attention mechanism: a way for every element of a sequence to weigh its relationship to every other element. This has become the substrate for state-of-the-art models across text, image, video, and multimodal domains.</p>
<p>Each attention block projects the input embeddings into query (<span class="math inline">\(Q\)</span>), key (<span class="math inline">\(K\)</span>), and value (<span class="math inline">\(V\)</span>) matrices via learned linear layers, then computes:</p>
<p><span id="eq-attention"><span class="math display">\[
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^{T}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\cdot V
\tag{3}\]</span></span></p>
<p>where <span class="math inline">\(d_k\)</span> is the key dimensionality and the softmax normalizes attention weights to sum to 1 (<a href="#eq-attention" class="quarto-xref">Equation&nbsp;3</a>). In practice, <strong>multi-head attention</strong> runs several <span class="math inline">\(Q\)</span>/<span class="math inline">\(K\)</span>/<span class="math inline">\(V\)</span> projections in parallel so the model can capture different types of relationships simultaneously.</p>
<p>For vision, <strong>Vision Transformers (ViTs)</strong> tokenize an image into fixed-size, non-overlapping patches (e.g., 16×16 pixels), flatten and linearly project each patch, then feed the resulting sequence — with positional encodings added — through a standard transformer encoder. This lets ViTs model long-range dependencies without a convolutional inductive bias.</p>
<p>Two attention regimes matter for what follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Self-Attention:</strong> <span class="math inline">\(Q\)</span>, <span class="math inline">\(K\)</span>, and <span class="math inline">\(V\)</span> are all projected from the same input embedding.</li>
<li><strong>Cross-Attention:</strong> used in text-to-image generation to condition image synthesis on a prompt — <span class="math inline">\(Q\)</span> comes from image tokens, while <span class="math inline">\(K\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(V\)</span> come from text embeddings.</li>
</ul>
<p>FLUX, as we’ll see, uses a <em>joint</em> scheme that blends both ideas: a <strong>Multi-Modal Attention</strong> where text and image tokens are concatenated and attend to each other simultaneously.</p>
</section>
</section>
<section id="flux.1" class="level1">
<h1>FLUX.1</h1>
<section id="introduction" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="introduction" id="introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>FLUX.1 is a rectified-flow transformer trained in the latent space of an image autoencoder, released by Black Forest Labs in August 2024. The FLUX.1 family sets a new state-of-the-art for text-to-image tasks on both output quality and image–text alignment, as measured by ELO-score human-preference comparisons against Midjourney, DALL·E 3, SD3, and others across dimensions like prompt following, size/aspect variability, typography, output diversity, and visual quality. These ELO comparisons are bar-chart evaluations reproduced from the official Black Forest Labs announcement — not architecture diagrams — so they are summarized here in prose rather than redrawn.</p>
<p>While Black Forest Labs states FLUX follows the Rectified Flow training paradigm, the actual training setup — dataset, noise/timestep scheduling, hyperparameters — has never been publicly disclosed. What <em>can</em> be reverse-engineered is the model’s architecture and inference-time behavior, directly from the public inference code (the <code>diffusers</code> implementation). That’s the paper’s core contribution, and this guide follows the same top-down path: first a bird’s-eye view of the architecture, then the sampling pipeline, then a deep dive into the transformer itself.</p>

<section id="sec-sampling-pipeline" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-sampling-pipeline" id="sec-sampling-pipeline">FLUX.1 Sampling Pipeline</h2>
<p>For simplicity, the paper (and this guide) describes sampling conditioned on a single prompt at a time. Like LDM, FLUX operates in a latent space: the final latent is decoded back into an RGB image. Following LDM’s approach, Black Forest Labs trained a convolutional autoencoder from scratch with an adversarial objective, but scaled the latent channel count from 4 (LDM) up to <strong>16</strong>.</p>
<p>The sampling pipeline has three phases:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong>Initiation and Pre-processing</strong></li>
<li><strong>Iterative Refinement</strong></li>
<li><strong>Post-processing</strong></li>
</ol>
<p>The notation follows the official <code>diffusers</code> implementation of the FLUX.1 pipeline.</p>
<section id="initiation-and-pre-processing" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="initiation-and-pre-processing" id="initiation-and-pre-processing">Initiation and Pre-processing</h3>
<p>The pipeline takes four required inputs:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>text</strong> — the textual prompt guiding generation.</li>
<li><strong>guidance_scale</strong> — controls conditioning strength.</li>
<li><strong>num_inference_steps</strong> — number of iterative sampling steps.</li>
<li><strong>resolution</strong> — spatial (height × width) resolution of the output image.</li>
</ul>

<p>Each input is preprocessed as follows (see <a href="#fig-pipeline" class="quarto-xref">Figure&nbsp;2</a>):</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>text</strong> is encoded by two pretrained text encoders:
<ul>
<li><strong>T5</strong> produces dense, per-token embeddings — the <code>encoder_hidden_states</code>.</li>
<li><strong>CLIP</strong> produces a single pooled embedding for the whole prompt (like a CLS token) — the <code>pooled_projection</code>.</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong>num_inference_steps</strong> determines how many timesteps are sampled from the full diffusion range <span class="math inline">\(t \in [0:T]\)</span>, typically <span class="math inline">\(T = 1000\)</span>. These selected timesteps define the sampling trajectory.</li>
<li><strong>resolution</strong> determines the spatial shape of the initial latent noise <span class="math inline">\(z_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\)</span>. Given target pixel resolution <span class="math inline">\((H, W)\)</span>, the latent dimensions are <span class="math inline">\((h = H\,/\!/\,\text{VAE}_{scale},\ w = W\,/\!/\,\text{VAE}_{scale})\)</span> with <span class="math inline">\(\text{VAE}_{scale} = 8\)</span>. The image-token grid is further downsampled to <span class="math inline">\((h\,/\!/\,2,\ w\,/\!/\,2)\)</span>, and each token gets a unique identifier <span class="math inline">\((t, \hat h, \hat w)\)</span> locating it on a 2D grid — these are the <code>img_ids</code>. The <code>text_ids</code> reuse the same structure but with <span class="math inline">\(t = \hat h = \hat w = 0\)</span> for every one of the <span class="math inline">\(n = 512\)</span> (T5’s max token count) text tokens: <code>text_ids = n·(0,0,0)</code>.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="iterative-refinement-and-post-processing" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="iterative-refinement-and-post-processing" id="iterative-refinement-and-post-processing">Iterative Refinement and Post-processing</h3>
<p>Let <span class="math inline">\(\Phi = [\text{encoder\_hidden\_states}, \text{pooled\_projection}, \text{guidance}, \text{img\_ids}, \text{text\_ids}]\)</span> denote the set of preprocessed, constant inputs. As in standard diffusion sampling, <span class="math inline">\([z_t, t, \Phi]\)</span> is fed into the transformer at every sampling iteration:</p>
<p><span id="eq-sampling-recursion"><span class="math display">\[
\forall\, t \in \text{timesteps}:\quad z_{t+\Delta t} = \text{Samp}\big(v_\theta(z_t, t, \Phi)\big)
\tag{4}\]</span></span></p>
<p>where <span class="math inline">\(v_\theta\)</span> is the network estimating the velocity vector (<a href="#sec-rectified-flows" class="quarto-xref">Section&nbsp;2.4</a>), and <span class="math inline">\(\text{Samp}(\cdot)\)</span> is the Flow-Matching Euler Discrete sampler.</p>
<div class="callout callout-style-default callout-important callout-titled">
<div class="callout-header d-flex align-content-center">
<div class="callout-icon-container">
<i class="callout-icon"></i>
</div>
<div class="callout-title-container flex-fill">
<span class="screen-reader-only">Important</span>Notation reminder
</div>
</div>
<div class="callout-body-container callout-body">
<p>In <a href="#eq-sampling-recursion" class="quarto-xref">Equation&nbsp;4</a>, <code>timesteps</code> runs from <span class="math inline">\(0\)</span> to <span class="math inline">\(1\)</span>, with <span class="math inline">\(z_1\)</span> the clean image latent and <span class="math inline">\(z_0\)</span> pure Gaussian noise — the reverse of typical DM convention (compare with <a href="#eq-eps-loss" class="quarto-xref">Equation&nbsp;1</a> where <span class="math inline">\(x_0\)</span> is clean). Don’t conflate FLUX’s sampling-time <span class="math inline">\(z_0\)</span>/<span class="math inline">\(z_1\)</span> with the RF training notation in <a href="#eq-rf-loss" class="quarto-xref">Equation&nbsp;2</a>.</p>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the iterative refinement finishes, the final clean latent <span class="math inline">\(z_1\)</span> is decoded by the pretrained VAE into the final image <span class="math inline">\(x_1\)</span>.</p>
</section>
</section>
<section id="sec-transformer" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-transformer" id="sec-transformer">Transformer</h2>
<p>The core of FLUX.1’s pipeline is the velocity predictor <span class="math inline">\(v_\theta\)</span>, optimized to estimate the velocity vector along the sampling trajectory. Like SD3, FLUX.1 replaces the conventional U-Net with a fully transformer-based design.</p>
<p>On every sampling iteration, inputs are preprocessed, then passed through a sequence of transformer blocks of two types:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong>Double-Stream</strong> blocks — separate weights for text and image tokens.</li>
<li><strong>Single-Stream</strong> blocks — shared weights across both modalities.</li>
</ol>
<p>Both block types use <strong>Multi-Modal Attention</strong>: a joint self-attention over concatenated text and image tokens, enabling bidirectional interaction that captures both within-modality and cross-modality information.</p>

<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="per-iteration-pre-process" id="per-iteration-pre-process">Per-Iteration Pre-process</h3>
<p>At every sampling iteration, the transformer receives an updated <em>timestep</em> (iterated from a precomputed list of values between 1 and 0) and an updated latent <span class="math inline">\(z_t\)</span> (<code>hidden_state</code>, refined iteratively from Gaussian noise toward a clean image). These join the “constant” inputs computed once during pipeline pre-processing.</p>

<p>Formally, three things happen in this phase (<a href="#fig-preprocess" class="quarto-xref">Figure&nbsp;4</a>):</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Shared-dimension projection.</strong> Per-domain linear layers bring the latent embeddings and the dense T5 prompt embeddings into a shared dimensionality of <strong>3072</strong> features per token.</li>
<li><strong><code>temb</code> construction.</strong> Unlike standard diffusion models, which encode only the timestep in this phase, FLUX.1 folds in both the timestep <em>and</em> the pooled CLIP prompt embedding — yet keeps the traditional name <code>temb</code>. Timestep and guidance are each embedded via sinusoidal projection (à la DDPM), then passed through dedicated linear layers to reach the shared 3072-dim space. The three projected embeddings (timestep, guidance, pooled prompt) are summed to form the final <code>temb</code> (the red block in the original Figure 7).</li>
<li><strong>Positional embeddings.</strong> <code>img_ids</code> and <code>text_ids</code> are concatenated, then used to extract per-token positional embeddings. Each of the 3 axes <span class="math inline">\((t, \hat h, \hat w)\)</span> is converted into a continuous embedding via axis-specific sinusoidal frequencies scaled by a constant <span class="math inline">\(\theta\)</span>. Cosine and sine of the resulting frequency–position products are computed and interleaved into real-valued vectors, then concatenated across axes to yield the final <code>pos_embeds</code> — a cos/sin tensor pair ready for <strong>Rotary Positional Encoding (RoPE)</strong> (the purple block in the original Figure 7). This computation depends only on token identity/position, not on <code>timestep</code> or <code>hidden_states</code>, so <code>pos_embeds</code> stays constant across all sampling steps.</li>
</ul>
<p><code>pos_embeds</code> and <code>temb</code> support the attention mechanism throughout the step, while <code>hidden_states</code> and <code>encoder_hidden_states</code> are the tensors actually refined at each step.</p>

<figcaption class="quarto-float-caption-bottom quarto-float-caption quarto-float-fig" id="fig-adaln-caption-0ceaefa1-69ba-4598-a22c-09a6ac19f8ca">
Figure&nbsp;7: AdaLN layer: MSA and MLP modulation parameters are computed from the conditioning signal. In the Single-Stream block, MLP modulation is not computed (reinterpreted from Figure 9).
</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The normalized tensors extract <span class="math inline">\(K\)</span>, <span class="math inline">\(Q\)</span>, <span class="math inline">\(V\)</span> per domain, which are concatenated for mixed attention (<a href="#fig-doublestream-b" class="quarto-xref">Figure&nbsp;6</a>). The concatenated <span class="math inline">\(K\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(Q\)</span> are rotated using precomputed <strong>Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE)</strong> — a technique that injects positional information by rotating query/key vectors in the complex plane according to token position, rather than adding sinusoidal/learned embeddings outright. RoPE preserves relative positional relationships across sequences, generalizes to unseen sequence lengths, and has become popular in vision-language models where spatial relationships (especially when repurposing text-style positional schemes for image patches) matter.</p>
<p>The rotated <span class="math inline">\(K\)</span>/<span class="math inline">\(Q\)</span> pass through the mixed attention operation; outputs are split back into their respective streams and alpha-blended with the residual input via <code>gate_msa</code> (from AdaLN, <a href="#fig-adaln" class="quarto-xref">Figure&nbsp;7</a>). Results are further normalized via LayerNorm, modulated with <code>scale_mlp</code>/<code>shift_mlp</code> (also from AdaLN), passed through the feed-forward layer, and finally alpha-blended with the unnormalized input via <code>gate_mlp</code> (from AdaLN).</p>
</section>
<section id="single-stream-transformer-block" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="single-stream-transformer-block" id="single-stream-transformer-block">Single-Stream Transformer Block</h3>
<p>After the Double-Stream blocks, the processed latent and prompt embeddings are concatenated and fed through a series of <strong>Single-Stream blocks</strong>. Where Double-Stream blocks use different weights for prompt vs.&nbsp;latent tokens, Single-Stream blocks use a <em>single</em> set of weights on the concatenated tensor. They also replace the standard sequential attention-then-MLP pattern with a <strong>parallel</strong> mechanism: attention and MLP are computed simultaneously from the same input.</p>

<p><a href="#tbl-blockcompare" class="quarto-xref">Table&nbsp;1</a> summarizes the two block types. The paper doesn’t document Black Forest Labs’ exact motivation for combining both, but offers a considered analysis:</p>
<p><strong>Weight sharing (shared vs.&nbsp;not shared).</strong> Shared weights (Single-Stream) mean more efficient parameter usage and tighter cross-modal integration, potentially aiding generalization — at the cost of flexibility, since text and latent tokens must be processed identically despite differing statistics. Separate weights (Double-Stream) let the model specialize per modality, which can help when domain-specific distinctions matter, at the cost of extra parameters and compute. Using both suggests a deliberate efficiency/specialization trade-off.</p>
<p><strong>Computation style (sequential vs.&nbsp;parallel).</strong> Double-Stream’s sequential design (normalize → attention → normalize → MLP) lets each layer build on the last, enabling tightly coupled, stage-wise processing. Single-Stream’s parallel design normalizes once and runs attention and MLP simultaneously from the same representation, combining their outputs downstream — more efficient and higher per-block representational capacity, but potentially less inter-layer interaction depth.</p>
<p>In short: Single-Stream blocks favor efficiency and simplicity via parallel computation and shared weights; Double-Stream blocks favor specialization and expressiveness via sequential flow and separate weights. Double-Stream blocks follow the <em>mm-DiT</em> design previously used in SD3, and the addition of Single-Stream blocks likely reflects an intent to expand model capacity in a comparatively lightweight, efficient way.</p>

<section id="models-and-tools" class="level1">
<h1>Models and Tools</h1>
<section id="text-to-image-variants" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="text-to-image-variants" id="text-to-image-variants">Text-to-Image Variants</h2>
<p>All FLUX.1 text-to-image variants share the same <strong>12B-parameter</strong> architecture, differing in training/distillation strategy, licensing, and deployment:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>FLUX.1 [pro]</strong> — the highest-performing variant: best prompt alignment, visual detail, and output diversity. Commercial-only, hosted via licensed API endpoints (Replicate, Fal.ai); weights are not released.</li>
<li><strong>FLUX.1 [dev]</strong> — an open-weight, guidance-distilled alternative to Pro. Licensed strictly for non-commercial use (research/hobbyist). Downloadable and runnable locally via Hugging Face; may need more sampling steps than Pro to match its quality.</li>
<li><strong>FLUX.1 [schnell]</strong> — a speed-optimized variant distilled from Pro via timestep-distillation, able to generate in as few as a single step. Released under Apache 2.0 (unrestricted commercial use), trading some prompt fidelity/detail for latency — ideal for real-time or lightweight deployments.</li>
<li><strong>FLUX1.1 [pro]</strong> — an enhanced Pro variant: faster generation plus improved quality, prompt adherence, and diversity. Adds an <strong>Ultra</strong> mode (4× higher resolution at similar speed) and a <strong>Raw</strong> mode (hyper-realistic, candid-style images), plus LLM-based prompt upsampling. Not covered in architectural depth by this report; commercial licensing required via Replicate/Fal.ai.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="tools" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="tools" id="tools">Tools</h2>
<p>Beyond text-to-image, Black Forest Labs has extended FLUX.1 with additional tools (current as of June 5, 2025; not explored in architectural depth in the source report):</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>FLUX-Fill</strong> — inpainting/outpainting given a text description and a binary mask.</li>
<li><strong>FLUX-Canny</strong> — structural guidance from Canny edges extracted from an input image, combined with a text prompt.</li>
<li><strong>FLUX-Depth</strong> — structural guidance from a depth map extracted from an input image, combined with a text prompt.</li>
<li><strong>FLUX-Redux</strong> — an adaptor aligning dense per-token image embeddings (from a SigLIP image encoder) with T5’s text-embedding space, enabling image-conditioned generation from a pretrained FLUX.1 base. Limited to image variation in the Dev version, but unlocks prompt-driven image restyling when paired with FLUX1.1 [pro] Ultra.</li>
<li><strong>FLUX-Kontext</strong> — extends FLUX into in-context generation and editing: multimodal conditioning on both text and reference images, enabling localized edits, style transfer, and character/scene consistency across images. As of the report’s writing, Kontext [pro]/[max] were not yet publicly released and so weren’t directly evaluated.</li>
</ul>
</section>
</section>
<section id="appendix-a-qualitative-evaluation" class="level1">
<h1>Appendix A: Qualitative Evaluation</h1>
<p>The paper closes with a qualitative comparison of FLUX.1 [dev] (50 sampling steps, guidance scale 2.5) against Stable Diffusion 2.1 (50 sampling steps, guidance scale 7.5), both at 512×512 resolution, focused on <strong>automotive scenes</strong> — relevant to the author’s affiliation with GM R&amp;D. Both models were used without additional fine-tuning, and all prompts requested a dashcam-style front-vehicle viewpoint. These are photographic example-image comparisons rather than architecture diagrams, so they are described here rather than redrawn:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Overall image quality</strong> — FLUX.1 produces high-quality automotive scenes under varying weather/lighting without extra optimization.</li>
<li><strong>Text–image alignment</strong> — FLUX.1 is sensitive to subtle prompt differences (e.g., light vs.&nbsp;heavy snow) and can compose complex attributes like “a sunny day after a snowy period.”</li>
<li><strong>Sparse-prompt alignment</strong> — FLUX.1 correctly interprets minimal/loosely defined prompts such as “adverse weather” or “difficult viewing conditions,” while still responding to direct requests like “blurry.”</li>
<li><strong>Rare-concept synthesis</strong> — FLUX.1’s vocabulary and pretraining let it render rare concepts such as a “boat-trailer” or an “upside-down crashed car.”</li>
<li><strong>Complex-prompt adherence</strong> — T5’s dense per-token embeddings let FLUX.1 satisfy prompts containing multiple simultaneous requests.</li>
<li><strong>Resolution/aspect-ratio flexibility</strong> — unlike U-Net-based diffusion models typically limited to a fixed set of resolutions, FLUX.1 operates across a wide range of resolutions and aspect ratios.</li>
</ul>
<p>Across all these dimensions, FLUX.1 shows a pronounced advantage over SD 2.1, especially for complex, out-of-distribution generative tasks — the author notes potential applications in automotive data augmentation, adverse-condition simulation, rare-concept generation/inpainting, and general data restoration/enhancement.</p>
