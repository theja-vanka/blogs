<section id="high-resolution-image-synthesis-with-latent-diffusion-models" class="level1">

<p><img src="/blogs/posts/generative-ai/latent-diffusion/latent.png" class="img-fluid"></p>
<hr>
<p>This is the paper that introduced <strong>Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs)</strong> — the architecture that underlies Stable Diffusion. Its core trick is deceptively simple: instead of running a diffusion model directly on RGB pixels, first compress the image into a small, perceptually equivalent latent space with an autoencoder, and run the (expensive, iterative) diffusion process there instead. This guide focuses on <em>how and why that works</em>, walking through the method in detail, with a lighter pass over the experimental results.</p>
<p><strong>Paper:</strong> Rombach, Blattmann, Lorenz, Esser, Ommer (2021/2022). <em>High-Resolution Image Synthesis with Latent Diffusion Models.</em> CVPR 2022. <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.10752">arXiv:2112.10752</a></p>
<hr>
<section id="why-this-paper-exists" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="why-this-paper-exists" id="why-this-paper-exists">1. Why this paper exists</h2>
<p>Diffusion models (DMs) had, by late 2021, become the state of the art for image synthesis — better mode coverage than GANs, no adversarial training instability, and strong likelihood-based density estimation. But they had a brutal cost problem:</p>
<ul>
<li>Training a powerful pixel-space DM could take <strong>150–1000 V100 GPU-days</strong>.</li>
<li>Generating 50,000 samples could take <strong>~5 days on a single A100</strong>, because sampling requires many sequential forward passes of a large neural network.</li>
</ul>
<p>The root cause is that DMs are <em>likelihood-based</em> models, and likelihood-based training is “mode-covering”: it spends a disproportionate amount of model capacity and compute on modeling imperceptible, high-frequency pixel details that contribute almost nothing to how an image looks to a human. Every one of those denoising steps still has to run over the full, high-dimensional pixel grid.</p>
<p>The authors’ insight is that image “information content” naturally splits into two regimes:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong>Perceptual compression</strong> — removing high-frequency detail that a human wouldn’t notice is missing. This is what a good autoencoder or JPEG-style codec does.</li>
<li><strong>Semantic compression</strong> — learning the actual conceptual/semantic composition of the data. This is the hard generative modeling problem that diffusion models are good at.</li>
</ol>
<p>Most of a pixel-space DM’s compute is wasted re-deriving perceptual compression that a much cheaper, non-iterative model could have done once, up front. So: separate the two stages. Train an autoencoder once to handle perceptual compression, then train the diffusion model entirely inside that compressed latent space, where it only has to worry about semantics.</p>

<p>This separation is the entire idea of the paper. Everything else — the autoencoder design, the diffusion loss, the UNet, the cross-attention conditioning — is in service of making that separation work well.</p>

</section>
<section id="perceptual-image-compression-stage-1-the-autoencoder" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="perceptual-image-compression-stage-1-the-autoencoder" id="perceptual-image-compression-stage-1-the-autoencoder">2. Perceptual image compression (Stage 1: the autoencoder)</h2>
<p>The first stage is an autoencoder <span class="math inline">\((\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{D})\)</span> trained <em>independently</em> of the diffusion model, once, and then reused for many downstream diffusion models and tasks.</p>
<p>Given an image <span class="math inline">\(x \in \mathbb{R}^{H\times W\times 3}\)</span>:</p>
<ul>
<li>The encoder maps it to a latent <span class="math inline">\(z = \mathcal{E}(x)\)</span>.</li>
<li>The decoder reconstructs it: <span class="math inline">\(\tilde{x} = \mathcal{D}(z) = \mathcal{D}(\mathcal{E}(x))\)</span>.</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(z \in \mathbb{R}^{h \times w \times c}\)</span>, where the encoder downsamples spatially by a factor <span class="math inline">\(f = H/h = W/w\)</span>, with <span class="math inline">\(f = 2^m\)</span> for various <span class="math inline">\(m\)</span>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Two design choices matter a great deal here:</p>
<p><strong>Loss function.</strong> Rather than training with a plain pixel-space <span class="math inline">\(L_1\)</span>/<span class="math inline">\(L_2\)</span> reconstruction loss (which produces blurry reconstructions because it’s satisfied by “safe,” averaged pixel values), the autoencoder uses a combination of a <em>perceptual loss</em> and a <em>patch-based adversarial objective</em>. This keeps reconstructions on the manifold of realistic images and enforces local realism, rather than optimizing a pixel-wise metric that doesn’t correlate well with perceived quality.</p>
<p><strong>Regularizing the latent space.</strong> An unconstrained autoencoder latent space can have arbitrarily high variance, which is a bad target for a diffusion model to learn (it needs <em>some</em> regularity to interpolate/denoise sensibly). Two regularization schemes are tried:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>KL-reg.</strong>: a small KL penalty pulling the learned latent distribution toward a standard normal, à la a VAE.</li>
<li><strong>VQ-reg.</strong>: a vector-quantization layer, absorbed into the decoder — effectively a VQGAN, but where the diffusion model (not an autoregressive transformer) will model the prior over the latent.</li>
</ul>
<p>Crucially, because the downstream diffusion model is a convolutional UNet that can exploit the 2D spatial structure of <span class="math inline">\(z\)</span>, this compression can stay <em>mild</em> — unlike prior two-stage approaches (VQGAN + autoregressive transformer) that needed to compress very aggressively (often flattening the latent into a 1D sequence) to keep the downstream autoregressive model computationally tractable. Keeping the latent 2D and only mildly compressed is what allows LDMs to get near-lossless reconstructions while still slashing computational cost — this is exactly the trade-off visualized in Figure 1 of the paper, where the LDM’s higher downsampling factor <span class="math inline">\(f{=}4\)</span> still reconstructs images far more faithfully (PSNR 27.4, R-FID 0.58) than DALL·E’s discrete VAE at <span class="math inline">\(f{=}8\)</span> (PSNR 22.8, R-FID 32.01) or the original VQGAN at <span class="math inline">\(f{=}16\)</span> (PSNR 19.9, R-FID 4.98).</p>

<section id="latent-diffusion-models-stage-2-the-actual-generative-model" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="latent-diffusion-models-stage-2-the-actual-generative-model" id="latent-diffusion-models-stage-2-the-actual-generative-model">3. Latent Diffusion Models (Stage 2: the actual generative model)</h2>
<section id="a-quick-refresher-on-diffusion-models" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="a-quick-refresher-on-diffusion-models" id="a-quick-refresher-on-diffusion-models">3.1 A quick refresher on diffusion models</h3>
<p>A standard denoising diffusion probabilistic model learns <span class="math inline">\(p(x)\)</span> by learning to reverse a fixed-length (<span class="math inline">\(T\)</span>-step) Markov chain that gradually adds Gaussian noise to data. In practice this reduces to training a sequence of denoising autoencoders <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\)</span>, <span class="math inline">\(t = 1 \ldots T\)</span>, each of which is trained to predict the noise <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon\)</span> that was added to produce the noisy sample <span class="math inline">\(x_t\)</span> from the clean data <span class="math inline">\(x\)</span>. The (simplified, reweighted) training objective is:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
L_{DM} = \mathbb{E}_{x,\,\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1),\,t}\Big[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|_2^2\Big] \tag{1}
\]</span></p>
<p>with <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span> sampled uniformly from <span class="math inline">\(\{1, \ldots, T\}\)</span>. This is normally evaluated and optimized directly on RGB pixels <span class="math inline">\(x\)</span> — which is the expensive part.</p>
</section>
<section id="moving-the-diffusion-process-into-latent-space" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="moving-the-diffusion-process-into-latent-space" id="moving-the-diffusion-process-into-latent-space">3.2 Moving the diffusion process into latent space</h3>
<p>Once the perceptual autoencoder <span class="math inline">\((\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{D})\)</span> from Section 2 is trained and frozen, LDMs simply replace <span class="math inline">\(x\)</span> with <span class="math inline">\(z = \mathcal{E}(x)\)</span> everywhere in the diffusion objective:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
L_{LDM} := \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{E}(x),\,\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1),\,t}\Big[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(z_t, t)\|_2^2\Big] \tag{2}
\]</span></p>
<p>The denoising network <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta(\circ, t)\)</span> is a <strong>time-conditional UNet</strong>, now built primarily from 2D convolutional layers operating on the small <span class="math inline">\(h \times w \times c\)</span> latent grid instead of the full <span class="math inline">\(H \times W \times 3\)</span> pixel grid. Because the forward (noising) process is fixed and closed-form, <span class="math inline">\(z_t\)</span> can be computed cheaply from <span class="math inline">\(\mathcal{E}(x)\)</span> during training — there’s no need to run the decoder at every training step. At sampling time, you run the reverse diffusion process entirely in latent space to get a sample <span class="math inline">\(z \sim p(z)\)</span>, and only decode once at the very end via <span class="math inline">\(\mathcal{D}\)</span>.</p>
<p>This is the entire computational win: the same UNet architecture and the same number of diffusion steps now operate on a grid that might be, say, <span class="math inline">\(32\times32\times4\)</span> instead of <span class="math inline">\(256\times256\times3\)</span> — dramatically cutting both training FLOPs and sampling latency, since sampling requires many sequential evaluations of <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta\)</span>.</p>

<section id="conditioning-mechanisms-turning-ldms-into-controllable-generators" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="conditioning-mechanisms-turning-ldms-into-controllable-generators" id="conditioning-mechanisms-turning-ldms-into-controllable-generators">3.3 Conditioning mechanisms: turning LDMs into controllable generators</h3>
<p>An unconditional diffusion model just samples “some image from the training distribution.” To make it <em>useful</em> — text-to-image, layout-to-image, class-conditional, super-resolution, inpainting — the model needs to condition on side information <span class="math inline">\(y\)</span>. The paper handles this in two ways depending on the task:</p>
<p><strong>1. Spatial concatenation.</strong> For densely-aligned conditioning signals that live in the same spatial grid as the image (a low-resolution image for super-resolution, a semantic segmentation map, a masked image for inpainting), the conditioning signal is simply concatenated channel-wise to the UNet’s input. This is cheap and works well precisely because the signal is already spatially aligned with <span class="math inline">\(z_t\)</span>.</p>
<p><strong>2. Cross-attention, for general/unaligned conditioning.</strong> For anything that <em>isn’t</em> naturally a spatial map — most importantly, free-form text — the paper augments the UNet with <strong>cross-attention layers</strong>. A domain-specific encoder <span class="math inline">\(\tau_\theta\)</span> (e.g.&nbsp;a transformer over BERT-tokenized text) projects the conditioning input <span class="math inline">\(y\)</span> into an intermediate representation <span class="math inline">\(\tau_\theta(y) \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times d_\tau}\)</span>. This is then fed into the UNet’s intermediate layers via standard multi-head cross-attention:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d}}\right)\cdot V
\]</span></p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
Q = W_Q^{(i)} \cdot \varphi_i(z_t), \quad K = W_K^{(i)} \cdot \tau_\theta(y), \quad V = W_V^{(i)} \cdot \tau_\theta(y)
\]</span></p>
<p>where <span class="math inline">\(\varphi_i(z_t) \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_\epsilon^i}\)</span> is a flattened intermediate UNet feature map, and <span class="math inline">\(W_Q^{(i)}, W_K^{(i)}, W_V^{(i)}\)</span> are learned projection matrices at that layer. In plain terms: the image latent’s spatial features (as queries) attend over the tokens of the conditioning representation (as keys/values) — exactly the mechanism transformers use for cross-modal attention, just injected into a convolutional UNet at multiple resolutions.</p>
<p>Both <span class="math inline">\(\tau_\theta\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(\epsilon_\theta\)</span> are trained jointly, end to end, by minimizing the conditional variant of the LDM loss:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
L_{LDM} := \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{E}(x),\,y,\,\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1),\,t}\Big[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(z_t, t, \tau_\theta(y))\|_2^2\Big] \tag{3}
\]</span></p>

<p>The cross-attention route is what lets the <em>same architecture</em> handle text-to-image, class-conditional, and layout-to-image generation just by swapping out <span class="math inline">\(\tau_\theta\)</span> and the training data — no task-specific architecture needed. The concatenation route is what lets the model be applied convolutionally to render images much larger than it was trained on (Section 5 below), since concatenation-based conditioning has no fixed sequence length the way cross-attention over a fixed set of tokens does.</p>

<section id="experiments-condensed" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="experiments-condensed" id="experiments-condensed">4. Experiments (condensed)</h2>
<p>The paper runs a large experimental sweep; here are the headline findings.</p>
<p><strong>Finding the right compression factor.</strong> The authors sweep the downsampling factor <span class="math inline">\(f \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32\}\)</span> (calling the pixel-space case, <span class="math inline">\(f{=}1\)</span>, “LDM-1”). Small <span class="math inline">\(f\)</span> (little compression) makes training slow, because the diffusion model is still mostly doing perceptual compression itself. Large <span class="math inline">\(f\)</span> (aggressive compression) causes fidelity to plateau early, because too much information is lost in Stage 1 before the diffusion model ever sees the data. <strong>LDM-4 and LDM-8 hit the sweet spot</strong> — after 2M training steps, LDM-8 shows a 38-point FID improvement over pixel-based LDM-1, while sampling substantially faster.</p>
<p><strong>Unconditional image generation.</strong> Trained on CelebA-HQ, FFHQ, LSUN-Churches, and LSUN-Bedrooms at 256×256, LDMs set a new state-of-the-art FID of <strong>5.11 on CelebA-HQ</strong>, beating both prior likelihood-based models and GANs, and consistently improve Precision/Recall over GAN baselines — evidence of the better mode coverage that comes from likelihood-based training.</p>
<p><strong>Text-to-image synthesis.</strong> A 1.45B-parameter KL-regularized LDM (BERT tokenizer + transformer as <span class="math inline">\(\tau_\theta\)</span>), trained on LAION-400M, is evaluated on MS-COCO. With classifier-free guidance, it becomes competitive with contemporaneous state-of-the-art autoregressive (Make-A-Scene) and diffusion (GLIDE) text-to-image models, while using substantially fewer parameters than either.</p>
<p><strong>Class-conditional ImageNet.</strong> LDM-4 with classifier-free guidance beats ADM (the prior best diffusion model for this task) while using roughly half the parameters and about 4× less training compute.</p>
<p><strong>Convolutional sampling beyond 256².</strong> Because spatially-aligned conditioning is injected by concatenation rather than a fixed-length attention context, an LDM trained at 256×256 can be applied <em>convolutionally</em> at inference time to synthesize much larger, spatially coherent images (up to ~1024² in some settings) — demonstrated on semantic-layout-to-landscape synthesis at 512×1024.</p>
<p><strong>Super-resolution &amp; inpainting.</strong> LDMs trained for 4× super-resolution (conditioning by concatenating a low-res image) are competitive with the specialized SR3 diffusion model, and LDM-based inpainting sets a new state-of-the-art FID versus the specialized LaMa architecture, while human raters preferred LDM inpainting results in a user study.</p>
<p>Across nearly every task, the consistent story is: <strong>comparable or better sample quality than pixel-space diffusion models or GANs, at a fraction of the training and sampling compute.</strong></p>

<section id="limitations-societal-impact-as-stated-by-the-authors" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="limitations-societal-impact-as-stated-by-the-authors" id="limitations-societal-impact-as-stated-by-the-authors">5. Limitations &amp; societal impact (as stated by the authors)</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sequential sampling is still slower than GANs.</strong> LDMs inherit diffusion’s iterative sampling process; even though it’s much cheaper than pixel-space diffusion, a single forward pass (as in a GAN) is still faster.</li>
<li><strong>The autoencoder is a fidelity bottleneck for high-precision tasks.</strong> Reconstruction loss from the <span class="math inline">\(f{=}4\)</span> autoencoder is small but nonzero, which can matter for applications needing pixel-exact accuracy (the authors flag their super-resolution models as somewhat limited here).</li>
<li><strong>Dual-use concerns.</strong> The same efficiency gains that democratize access to high-quality image synthesis also make it cheaper to generate manipulated imagery / deepfakes and spread misinformation — a concern the authors note disproportionately affects depictions of women.</li>
<li><strong>Data memorization and bias.</strong> Like other large generative models, LDMs can potentially reveal aspects of training data, and can reproduce or amplify biases present in that data (e.g.&nbsp;from web-scraped datasets like LAION).</li>
</ul>

</section>
<section id="why-this-paper-mattered" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="why-this-paper-mattered" id="why-this-paper-mattered">6. Why this paper mattered</h2>
<p>Conceptually, the contribution isn’t a new generative modeling principle — it’s a <em>systems-level</em> re-architecture: decouple “make it look real at the pixel level” (autoencoder, trained once, fast) from “make it semantically coherent/controllable” (diffusion, iterative, now much cheaper because it runs on a small latent grid), and glue them together with a general-purpose cross-attention conditioning mechanism that works for text, class labels, layouts, or anything else you can encode into tokens.</p>
<p>That combination — cheap latent-space diffusion + flexible cross-attention conditioning — is exactly the recipe later scaled up (with a larger UNet, a bigger text encoder, and web-scale text-image data) to become Stable Diffusion, which is why this paper is often cited as <em>the</em> latent diffusion / Stable Diffusion paper even though the original public release came somewhat later.</p>
