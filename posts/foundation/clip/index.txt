<section id="sec-tldr" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-tldr" id="sec-tldr">TL;DR</h2>
<p>CLIP throws out the idea that a computer vision model must be trained to predict a fixed, hand-labeled list of categories (the “1000 ImageNet classes” paradigm). Instead, it trains an image encoder and a text encoder jointly, from scratch, on 400 million (image, text) pairs scraped from the public internet, using a single proxy task: <strong>given a batch of images and a batch of text snippets, figure out which image goes with which text.</strong> This is a contrastive objective, not a generative or predictive one — the model never has to generate or reconstruct the exact caption, only to rank the correct pairing above the incorrect ones.</p>
<p>Because the text encoder learns to map arbitrary natural-language descriptions into the same embedding space as images, at test time you can build a classifier for <em>any</em> set of categories just by describing them in words (e.g., “a photo of a dog”, “a photo of a cat”) — no additional labeled training data, no fine-tuning, no new output head. This is what the paper calls <strong>zero-shot transfer</strong>, and it is CLIP’s central contribution: a single, generically pre-trained model that is benchmarked across more than 30 existing computer vision datasets and is frequently competitive with fully supervised, task-specific baselines. The headline result is that zero-shot CLIP matches the accuracy of the original supervised ResNet-50 on ImageNet without using any of ImageNet’s 1.28 million training labels.</p>
<p>The paper’s contribution is therefore threefold: <strong>(a)</strong> a dataset-construction and training recipe that makes natural-language supervision computationally practical at scale (400M pairs, contrastive objective, large batch size); <strong>(b)</strong> an extensive empirical study of zero-shot transfer across dozens of tasks (OCR, action recognition, geo-localization, fine-grained classification); and <strong>(c)</strong> a careful analysis of what this buys you — better robustness to distribution shift, but still real limitations on abstract/counting tasks, fine-grained tasks, truly out-of-distribution data (e.g., MNIST), and social bias.</p>
</section>
<section id="sec-introduction" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-introduction" id="sec-introduction">Introduction and motivating work</h2>
<section id="the-problem-being-solved" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-problem-being-solved" id="the-problem-being-solved">The problem being solved</h3>
<p>Standard computer vision pipelines (circa 2021, and still common today) train a model to predict a <strong>fixed, predetermined set of object categories</strong> — e.g., 1000 ImageNet classes. This has two structural weaknesses:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Generality:</strong> any new visual concept requires new labeled data and, typically, a new output head and a fine-tuning pass.</li>
<li><strong>Usability:</strong> benchmark accuracy on a closed label set is not the same thing as usable, general-purpose visual understanding.</li>
</ul>
<p>Meanwhile, in NLP, task-agnostic pre-training on raw text (autoregressive and masked language modeling — think GPT-2/GPT-3, BERT, T5) had already demonstrated that web-scale, weakly structured supervision can beat curated, crowd-labeled datasets, and that a single pre-trained model can zero-shot transfer to many downstream tasks via a text-to-text interface. The paper’s motivating question is: <strong>can the same “learn directly from raw, uncurated web supervision” recipe work for vision, using text as the supervisory signal instead of labels?</strong></p>
</section>
<section id="prior-art-the-paper-builds-on" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="prior-art-the-paper-builds-on" id="prior-art-the-paper-builds-on">Prior art the paper builds on</h3>
<p>The idea of learning visual representations from paired text is not new — the paper is explicit about this lineage:</p>
<ul>
<li>Mori et al.&nbsp;(1999): predicting nouns/adjectives in paired documents for content-based image retrieval.</li>
<li>Quattoni et al.&nbsp;(2007): manifold learning in classifier weight space to predict caption words.</li>
<li>Srivastava &amp; Salakhutdinov (2012): multimodal Deep Boltzmann Machines over image + text-tag features.</li>
<li>Joulin et al.&nbsp;(2016): CNNs predicting bag-of-words captions on YFCC100M, competitive with ImageNet pre-training on transfer tasks.</li>
<li>Li et al.&nbsp;(2017), <strong>Visual N-Grams</strong>: the first work to zero-shot transfer a generically-trained model to standard image classification benchmarks — but only reaching 11.5% top-1 on ImageNet, far below the fully supervised state of the art (88.4% at the time) and even below classical computer-vision baselines (~50%).</li>
<li>VirTex (Desai &amp; Johnson, 2020), ICMLM (Bulent Sariyildiz et al., 2020), ConVIRT (Zhang et al., 2020): modern transformer-based approaches (autoregressive, masked-LM, and contrastive objectives respectively) — CLIP is explicitly “a simplified version of ConVIRT trained from scratch.”</li>
</ul>
<p>Why had this line of work stayed a curiosity rather than a mainstream recipe? <strong>Scale.</strong> Weakly-supervised approaches that did scale (Mahajan et al., 2018 on Instagram hashtags; Kolesnikov et al., 2019 on JFT-300M) hard-coded a fixed vocabulary of 1,000–18,291 classes and used static softmax classifiers — sacrificing exactly the flexibility that natural language could offer. Meanwhile natural-language approaches like VirTex/ICMLM/ConVIRT trained on only 100k–200k images, “accelerator days” instead of “accelerator years.” CLIP’s contribution is closing this scale gap: 400 million pairs, trained efficiently enough to be practical.</p>
</section>
<section id="headline-efficiency-finding-figure-2" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="headline-efficiency-finding-figure-2" id="headline-efficiency-finding-figure-2">Headline efficiency finding (Figure 2)</h3>
<p>Before committing to the final recipe, the authors benchmarked three ways to learn from (image, text) pairs, all measured by zero-shot ImageNet accuracy as a function of the number of images processed:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li>A <strong>Transformer Language Model</strong> that autoregressively predicts the exact caption text (like an image-captioning system) — the weakest and slowest of the three, despite being the most expressive.</li>
<li>A simpler <strong>Bag-of-Words prediction</strong> baseline, predicting an unordered bag-of-words encoding of the caption — this alone was <strong>3× more sample-efficient</strong> than the transformer LM.</li>
<li><strong>Bag-of-Words Contrastive (CLIP’s objective)</strong> — swapping the predictive objective for a contrastive one on top of the same bag-of-words encoding bought a further <strong>4× efficiency gain</strong>.</li>
</ol>
<p>That 3×-then-4× compounding is the single biggest architectural decision in the paper: <em>don’t try to predict exact words; just distinguish the right pairing from the wrong ones.</em></p>
</section>
</section>
<section id="sec-approach" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-approach" id="sec-approach">The approach</h2>
<section id="natural-language-supervision" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="natural-language-supervision" id="natural-language-supervision">Natural language supervision</h3>
<p>The core idea: learn visual representations from the supervisory signal already implicit in natural language paired with images, rather than from a fixed, discretized label taxonomy. Note that different prior papers describe near-identical approaches as “unsupervised,” “self-supervised,” “weakly supervised,” and “supervised” — a sign that the underlying idea (natural language as training signal) is more fundamental than the label people attach to it. The claimed advantages over classic supervised or self-supervised learning:</p>
<ul>
<li>Vastly easier to <strong>scale</strong> — no need for the “1-of-N gold label” annotation format; you can passively harvest supervision from text that already exists on the web.</li>
<li>Unlike most unsupervised/self-supervised representation learning, it doesn’t just learn a representation — it <strong>connects that representation to language</strong>, which is what enables flexible zero-shot transfer at the end.</li>
</ul>
</section>

<tr class="odd">
<td>MS-COCO / Visual Genome</td>
<td>~100K photos each</td>
<td>High quality, crowd-labeled, but far too small</td>
</tr>
<tr class="even">
<td>YFCC100M</td>
<td>100M photos</td>
<td>Metadata sparse/noisy; after filtering to only images with natural-language English titles/descriptions, shrinks 6× to ~15M — about ImageNet-sized</td>
</tr>

<p>So the authors constructed a new dataset: <strong>400 million (image, text) pairs</strong> gathered from a variety of public internet sources. Construction details:</p>
<ul>
<li>They searched for pairs whose text includes one of <strong>500,000 queries</strong> (base vocabulary = all words occurring ≥100 times in English Wikipedia, augmented with high-PMI bigrams, popular Wikipedia article names, and WordNet synsets).</li>
<li>Results were approximately class-balanced by including <strong>up to 20,000 (image, text) pairs per query</strong>.</li>
<li>Total word count ends up comparable to the WebText dataset used to train GPT-2.</li>
<li>This dataset is called <strong>WIT</strong> (WebImageText) — note this predates and is unrelated to Google’s later “WIT: Wikipedia-based Image Text” dataset of the same acronym.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="selecting-an-efficient-pre-training-method" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="selecting-an-efficient-pre-training-method" id="selecting-an-efficient-pre-training-method">Selecting an efficient pre-training method</h3>
<p><strong>Compute is the binding constraint.</strong> For context: Mahajan et al.&nbsp;(2018) spent 19 GPU-years; Xie et al.&nbsp;(2020) spent 33 TPUv3-core-years — and those systems only predicted 1,000 ImageNet classes. Learning an <em>open</em> set of visual concepts from raw text looked daunting on cost grounds alone, so training efficiency became the primary criterion for choosing a method (this is the Figure 2 study described above).</p>
<p><strong>The final CLIP objective (contrastive, symmetric, in-batch negatives).</strong> Given a batch of <em>N</em> (image, text) pairs, CLIP is trained to predict which of the <em>N × N</em> possible pairings actually occurred. It learns a joint multi-modal embedding space by training an image encoder and text encoder to:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>maximize</strong> the cosine similarity of the <em>N</em> correct (image, text) pairs, while</li>
<li><strong>minimizing</strong> the cosine similarity of the <em>N² − N</em> incorrect pairings,</li>
</ul>
<p>optimized via a <strong>symmetric cross-entropy loss</strong> over these similarity scores. This batch-construction trick and objective traces back to the multi-class N-pair loss (Sohn, 2016), was popularized as <strong>InfoNCE</strong> (Oord et al., 2018), and was previously adapted to (text, image) contrastive learning in medical imaging by Zhang et al.&nbsp;(2020) — ConVIRT.</p>
<section id="formalizing-the-objective" class="level4">
<h4 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="formalizing-the-objective">Formalizing the objective</h4>
<p>Let <span class="math inline">\(I_f = f_{\text{image}}(I)\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(T_f = f_{\text{text}}(T)\)</span> be the raw encoder outputs for a batch of <span class="math inline">\(N\)</span> images and <span class="math inline">\(N\)</span> texts. Project each into a shared <span class="math inline">\(d_e\)</span>-dimensional embedding space via learned linear maps <span class="math inline">\(W_i\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(W_t\)</span>, then L2-normalize:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
I_e = \frac{I_f W_i}{\lVert I_f W_i \rVert_2}, \qquad
T_e = \frac{T_f W_t}{\lVert T_f W_t \rVert_2}
\]</span></p>
<p>Compute the scaled pairwise cosine-similarity (“logits”) matrix using a learned temperature <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\text{logits}_{jk} = \left(I_e T_e^{\top}\right)_{jk} \cdot e^{t}, \qquad j,k \in \{1, \dots, N\}
\]</span></p>
<p>The loss is the average of two cross-entropy terms — one that, for each image row, treats the matching text as the correct class among all <span class="math inline">\(N\)</span> texts in the batch; and one that does the same for each text column against all <span class="math inline">\(N\)</span> images:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}\Big(
\underbrace{\text{CE}(\text{logits}, \; \mathbf{y} \mid \text{rows})}_{\text{image} \rightarrow \text{text}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\text{CE}(\text{logits}^{\top}, \; \mathbf{y} \mid \text{rows})}_{\text{text} \rightarrow \text{image}}
\Big), \qquad \mathbf{y} = [0, 1, \dots, N-1]
\]</span></p>
<p>i.e.&nbsp;the ground-truth label for row/column <span class="math inline">\(k\)</span> is simply <span class="math inline">\(k\)</span> itself — the diagonal of the <span class="math inline">\(N \times N\)</span> similarity matrix should dominate.</p>
</section>

<section id="design-simplifications-relative-to-convirt" class="level4">
<h4 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="design-simplifications-relative-to-convirt">Design simplifications relative to ConVIRT</h4>
<p>Because the 400M-pair dataset makes over-fitting a non-issue, the authors simplified training considerably versus Zhang et al.&nbsp;(2020):</p>
<ul>
<li>Trained <strong>entirely from scratch</strong> — no ImageNet-pretrained image encoder, no pretrained text encoder.</li>
<li><strong>No non-linear projection head</strong> between encoder representation and the shared embedding space (a technique introduced by Bachman et al., 2019, popularized by Chen et al., 2020b / SimCLR) — CLIP uses only a <strong>linear</strong> projection per modality, with no observed efficiency loss.</li>
<li><strong>No random sentence sampling</strong> transformation — most WIT captions are already a single sentence.</li>
<li><strong>Minimal image augmentation</strong> — just a random square crop from a resized image.</li>
<li>The <strong>temperature <span class="math inline">\(\tau\)</span> is a learned parameter</strong> (log-parameterized multiplicative scalar), not a manually-tuned hyperparameter, initialized equivalent to 0.07 (Wu et al., 2018) and clipped to prevent the logits from being scaled by more than 100 (needed for training stability).</li>
</ul>
</section>
</section>
<section id="model-architecture" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="model-architecture" id="model-architecture">Model architecture</h3>
<p><strong>Two families of image encoder were trained:</strong></p>
<ol type="1">
<li><strong>ResNet-50</strong>, with several modifications: ResNet-D improvements (He et al., 2019), antialiased rect-2 blur pooling (Zhang, 2019), and — notably — the global average pooling layer is <strong>replaced by an attention-pooling mechanism</strong>: a single layer of transformer-style multi-head QKV attention where the query is conditioned on the global-average-pooled image representation.</li>
<li><strong>Vision Transformer (ViT)</strong> (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), followed closely, with a minor tweak: an extra layer norm applied to the combined patch + position embeddings before the transformer, and a slightly different initialization.</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Text encoder:</strong> a Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) using the architectural modifications of Radford et al.&nbsp;(2019) — i.e., GPT-2-style. Base configuration: <strong>63M parameters, 12 layers, 512-wide, 8 attention heads</strong>, operating over a lower-cased byte-pair encoding (BPE) vocabulary of <strong>49,152</strong> tokens (Sennrich et al., 2015), with max sequence length capped at 76 for efficiency. Text is bracketed with <code>[SOS]</code>/<code>[EOS]</code> tokens; the <code>[EOS]</code> activation at the top layer is layer-normalized and linearly projected into the shared embedding space. Masked self-attention is retained (even though not strictly required for a contrastive-only objective) to preserve the option of initializing from, or jointly training with, a language-modeling objective in future work.</p>
<p><strong>Scaling rule:</strong> rather than scaling only width (as in Mahajan et al., 2018) or only depth (as in the original ResNet paper), the image encoder is scaled the way EfficientNet scales (Tan &amp; Le, 2019) — jointly across width, depth, <em>and</em> resolution — though CLIP uses a simple <strong>equal allocation</strong> across the three dimensions rather than EfficientNet’s tuned ratio. The text encoder is scaled <strong>in width only</strong>, proportionally to the ResNet’s width increase, with <strong>no depth scaling</strong> — the paper found CLIP’s performance much less sensitive to text-encoder capacity than to image-encoder capacity.</p>
</section>

<p>Reading the three panels: <strong>(1)</strong> during pre-training, an image encoder and text encoder are jointly trained so that the cosine similarity of matched image/text embeddings (<span class="math inline">\(I_k \cdot T_k\)</span>, the diagonal) is high and mismatched pairs are low. <strong>(2)</strong> at evaluation time, the class names of a brand-new target dataset (e.g.&nbsp;“plane”, “car”, “dog”, “bird”) are wrapped in a prompt template like <em>“A photo of a {object}.”</em> and pushed through the (frozen) text encoder — this produces one embedding vector per class, which functions as the <strong>weight vector of a linear classifier synthesized entirely from language</strong>. <strong>(3)</strong> for a new test image, its embedding is compared against every class’s text embedding, and the class with the highest cosine similarity is the zero-shot prediction. This third step is why the paper describes the text encoder as a <strong>hypernetwork</strong> (Ha et al., 2016): a network that generates the weights of another network (here, a linear classifier) instead of computing the target quantity itself. The idea of zero-shot classifiers of this exact form (compare image embedding to embeddings of natural-language class descriptions) traces back to Lei Ba et al.&nbsp;(2015) and, for language-generated classifiers specifically, Elhoseiny et al.&nbsp;(2013).</p>

<section id="sec-experiments" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-experiments" id="sec-experiments">Experiments</h2>
<section id="zero-shot-transfer" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="zero-shot-transfer" id="zero-shot-transfer">Zero-shot transfer</h3>
<section id="what-zero-shot-means-here" class="level4">
<h4 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="what-zero-shot-means-here">What “zero-shot” means here</h4>
<p>The paper deliberately broadens “zero-shot learning” beyond its usual computer-vision meaning (generalizing to unseen <em>categories</em>) to mean <strong>generalizing to unseen <em>datasets/distributions</em></strong> — motivated by Larochelle et al.&nbsp;(2008)’s zero-data learning framing. The authors argue this is really a proxy for measuring <strong>task-learning capability</strong>, not just representation quality: many popular CV benchmarks (CIFAR-10, for instance) were built as generic-method testbeds rather than as measurements of one specific real-world task, so zero-shot transfer to them is best read as a test of robustness/generalization, which the paper revisits directly in the robustness section below.</p>
</section>
<section id="mechanics-of-zero-shot-classification" class="level4">
<h4 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="mechanics-of-zero-shot-classification">Mechanics of zero-shot classification</h4>
<p>For a target dataset, embed every class name (through the frozen text encoder) once, embed the test image once, compute cosine similarities, scale by the learned temperature <span class="math inline">\(\tau\)</span>, and softmax to get a probability distribution. This is literally a <strong>multinomial logistic regression classifier</strong> with L2-normalized inputs, L2-normalized weights, no bias term, and temperature scaling — except the weights were “written” by the text encoder rather than learned by gradient descent on that specific task. Framed this way, every step of CLIP pre-training is optimizing performance on a randomly-constructed proxy classification problem with 1 example per class and 32,768 total classes (the batch size). Since the zero-shot classifier only needs to be computed once per dataset, its cost is amortized across every prediction made on that dataset.</p>
</section>

<section id="prompt-engineering-and-ensembling-figure-4" class="level4">
<h4 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="prompt-engineering-and-ensembling-figure-4">Prompt engineering and ensembling (Figure 4)</h4>
<p>Two subtle but consequential engineering choices:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Polysemy is a real problem.</strong> Bare class names lack context — e.g., ImageNet’s “crane” could mean the bird or the construction equipment; Oxford-IIIT Pets’ “boxer” could mean the dog breed or the athlete.</li>
<li><strong>The pre-training distribution is mostly full sentences, not bare words.</strong> Using the template <strong>“A photo of a {label}.”</strong> as a default closes this distribution gap and alone improves ImageNet accuracy by <strong>1.3%</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Dataset-specific prompt customization</strong> helps further: “A photo of a {label}, a type of pet.” for Oxford-IIIT Pets; specifying food/aircraft for Food101/FGVCAircraft; quoting the text/number for OCR datasets; “a satellite photo of a {label}.” for satellite imagery.</li>
<li><strong>Ensembling across many prompt templates</strong> (e.g., “a photo of a big {label}”, “a photo of a small {label}”) in <strong>embedding space</strong> (not probability space) — this means a single cached, averaged text embedding per class captures the whole ensemble at no extra inference cost. On ImageNet, ensembling <strong>80 different prompts</strong> contributes an additional <strong>3.5%</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Combined effect:</strong> prompt engineering + ensembling together improve ImageNet accuracy by <strong>almost 5%</strong>, at essentially zero marginal inference cost once amortized — described in the paper as equivalent to roughly a <strong>4× compute gain</strong>, but “free.”</li>
</ul>
</section>

<ul>

<section id="zero-shot-vs.-few-shot-figures-68" class="level4">
<h4 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="zero-shot-vs.-few-shot-figures-68">Zero-shot vs.&nbsp;few-shot (Figures 6–8)</h4>
<ul>
<li>Zero-shot CLIP <strong>matches the average performance of a 4-shot linear classifier</strong> trained on the same CLIP feature space, and <strong>nearly matches a 16-shot classifier’s performance</strong> across the broader evaluation suite (Figure 6). This is notable because normally you’d expect <em>some</em> labeled data to always help — the paper’s explanation is that CLIP’s zero-shot classifier is <strong>directly specified via language</strong>, whereas example-based (few-shot) learning must infer the intended concept indirectly from images, and a single image often contains many entangled visual concepts (background, pose, lighting, etc.), which is especially unreliable in the 1-shot regime.</li>
<li><strong>Effective data efficiency varies enormously by dataset</strong> (Figure 7, below): from under 1 labeled example per class (Flowers102, EuroSAT — where zero-shot already beats a 1-shot linear probe) up to an estimated <strong>184 examples per class</strong> (FER2013). <strong>Median 5.4, mean 20.8</strong> examples/class across the 26 datasets studied.</li>
</ul>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="/blogs/posts/foundation/clip/fig7_data_efficiency.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Recreation of Figure 7: estimated number of labeled examples per class a linear classifier on the same CLIP features would need to match zero-shot CLIP’s accuracy (log scale).</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<ul>
<li>Because zero-shot CLIP is itself effectively a linear classifier, <strong>fully-supervised linear-probe performance roughly upper-bounds what zero-shot transfer can achieve</strong> (Figure 8 in the original paper). Across datasets there is a strong correlation (<span class="math inline">\(r = 0.82\)</span>, <span class="math inline">\(p &lt; 10^{-6}\)</span>) between zero-shot and fully-supervised linear-probe accuracy, but zero-shot typically trails by <strong>10–25 points</strong>; only 5 datasets (STL-10, CIFAR-10, Food101, OxfordPets, Caltech101 — all already &gt;90% for both) come within 3 points of the supervised ceiling.</li>
<li><strong>Scaling behaviour (Figure 9 in the original paper):</strong> average zero-shot error across 39 evaluations on 36 datasets follows a <strong>smooth log-log linear trend</strong> as a function of model compute, across a <strong>44× range of compute</strong> spanning the 5 ResNet-family CLIP models — echoing the compute-scaling laws documented for GPT-family models (Kaplan et al., 2020). Individual per-task curves are much noisier than the aggregate trend.</li>
</ul>
</section>
</section>
<section id="representation-learning-linear-probe-evaluation" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="representation-learning-linear-probe-evaluation" id="representation-learning-linear-probe-evaluation">Representation learning (linear-probe evaluation)</h3>
<p>Rather than end-to-end fine-tuning (which the authors note can <em>mask</em> failures of the pre-training representation itself, since fine-tuning adapts the representation per-dataset), the paper deliberately evaluates via <strong>linear probes</strong>: fit a linear classifier on top of frozen features and measure accuracy. This choice also mirrors the zero-shot evaluation methodology, enabling apples-to-apples comparison, and keeps the study of <strong>66 models × 27 datasets (1,782 evaluations)</strong> computationally tractable and free of confounding hyperparameter-tuning effort.</p>
<p>Findings (Figure 10 in the original paper):</p>
<ul>
<li>On the standardized 12-dataset suite of Kornblith et al.&nbsp;(2019): small CLIP ResNets beat other ImageNet-1K-trained ResNets (including BiT-S) but <em>underperform</em> ImageNet-21K-trained BiT-M and EfficientNet-family models at similar compute.</li>
<li>But CLIP <strong>scales exceptionally well</strong>: the largest ResNet trained (RN50x64) <strong>slightly outperforms</strong> the best existing public model (Noisy Student EfficientNet-L2) on both overall score and compute efficiency, on this 12-dataset suite.</li>
<li><strong>CLIP Vision Transformers are ~3× more compute-efficient than CLIP ResNets</strong> — echoing Dosovitskiy et al.&nbsp;(2020)’s original ViT-vs-CNN efficiency finding, now shown to hold for CLIP’s contrastive training too.</li>
<li>The single best CLIP model (<strong>ViT-L/14@336px</strong>) beats the best existing model by an average of <strong>2.6%</strong> on the 12-dataset suite.</li>
<li>On the broader, 27-dataset suite (chosen partly to include tasks like geo-localization, OCR, facial-emotion recognition, and action recognition that Kornblith et al.’s 12-dataset suite entirely misses — arguably a selection bias toward ImageNet-adjacent tasks), <strong>every CLIP model, at every scale, is more compute-efficient than every other system evaluated</strong>, and the best-model improvement margin widens to <strong>5%</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="/blogs/posts/foundation/clip/fig11_clip_vs_efficientnet.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Recreation of Figure 11: per-dataset delta between a linear probe on CLIP features and a linear probe on Noisy Student EfficientNet-L2 features, across 27 datasets.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p>CLIP’s features beat the best public ImageNet model (Noisy Student EfficientNet-L2) on <strong>21 of 27 datasets</strong> — with the largest gains on OCR-flavored tasks (SST2 +23.6, HatefulMemes +18.8), geo-localization/scene recognition (Country211 +22.7, SUN397 +6.5), and fine-grained car/traffic-sign recognition (StanfordCars +15.9, GTSRB +14.7 — the authors suggest ImageNet’s single “street/traffic sign” label collapses intra-class detail that hurts fine-grained transfer). CLIP loses, unsurprisingly, on <strong>ImageNet itself</strong> (−3.0, the dataset EfficientNet was directly trained on) and on low-resolution CIFAR-10/100 (likely due to CLIP’s lack of scale-based data augmentation).</p>
</section>
<section id="robustness-to-natural-distribution-shift" class="level3">
<h3 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="robustness-to-natural-distribution-shift" id="robustness-to-natural-distribution-shift">Robustness to natural distribution shift</h3>
<p><strong>Background:</strong> Since ~2015, “superhuman” ImageNet accuracy claims have repeatedly failed to hold up once models are tested on <em>natural</em> distribution shifts (as opposed to synthetic corruptions like ImageNet-C or adversarial perturbations). Taori et al.&nbsp;(2020) formalized this with 7 natural-shift test sets — ImageNetV2, ImageNet Sketch, Youtube-BB, ImageNet-Vid, ObjectNet, ImageNet-A, ImageNet-R — and the concept of <strong>effective robustness</strong> (accuracy gain under shift <em>beyond</em> what’s predicted by in-distribution accuracy alone) versus mere <strong>relative robustness</strong> (any accuracy gain at all under shift).</p>
<p><strong>Key finding:</strong> a ResNet-101 matched to CLIP’s zero-shot ImageNet accuracy (76.2%) makes <strong>far more mistakes</strong> under natural distribution shift than zero-shot CLIP does. Zero-shot CLIP shrinks the “effective robustness gap” (the gap between ImageNet accuracy and shifted-distribution accuracy) by <strong>up to 75%</strong>.</p>
<div class="quarto-figure quarto-figure-center">
<figure class="figure">
<p><img src="/blogs/posts/foundation/clip/fig13_robustness_gains.png" class="img-fluid figure-img"></p>
<figcaption>Recreation of Figure 13 (right panel): per-dataset accuracy change of zero-shot CLIP (ViT-L/14@336px) vs.&nbsp;an accuracy-matched ResNet-101, on the “banana” class shared across 5 distribution-shift test sets.</figcaption>
</figure>
</div>
<p><strong>But adapting CLIP to ImageNet erodes this benefit.</strong> Fitting an L2-regularized logistic-regression classifier on CLIP features using the ImageNet training set raises ImageNet accuracy by <strong>+9.2%</strong> (to 85.4%, tying 2018’s SOTA) — yet <strong>average accuracy under distribution shift barely moves</strong>, and actually <strong>drops</strong> on ImageNet-R (−4.7), ObjectNet (−3.8), ImageNet Sketch (−2.8), and ImageNet-A (−1.9). Only ImageNetV2 improves substantially (+5.8), which the authors attribute to ImageNetV2 having been collected via a process almost identical to the original ImageNet’s. In other words: a 9.2-point, “3 years of SOTA progress”-sized jump in ImageNet accuracy buys <strong>essentially nothing</strong> in real-world robustness — a striking illustration of Goodhart’s-law-style benchmark overfitting.</p>
<p>A second robustness intervention — generating a <strong>custom zero-shot classifier per evaluation dataset</strong> (rather than reusing/pooling a fixed 1000-way ImageNet classifier, which Taori et al.&nbsp;had to do via class-hierarchy max-pooling for datasets like Youtube-BB and ImageNet-Vid whose classes are ImageNet superclasses) — improves average effective robustness by another <strong>5%</strong>, concentrated in large gains on the few datasets where class granularity actually differed from ImageNet’s.</p>
<p>The paper also traces the zero-shot → few-shot → fully-supervised continuum (Figure 15 in the original): higher effective robustness correlates with <strong>less distribution-specific training data used</strong>, and this benefit erodes steadily as more labeled ImageNet examples are used to adapt the classifier, disappearing almost entirely by full supervision.</p>
</section>
</section>

<p>The striking pattern: humans jump from <strong>54% → 76%</strong> accuracy after just <strong>one</strong> example per class, with almost all of that gain concentrated in images the human was previously uncertain about — i.e., humans “know what they don’t know” and can resolve that uncertainty from a single example. CLIP’s few-shot methods show <strong>no such jump</strong> (in fact, the paper notes few-shot linear probes on CLIP features can <em>underperform</em> zero-shot CLIP, a counter-intuitive result revisited in the Limitations section). This exposes a real gap between human sample-efficient learning (which appears to integrate strong priors) and current few-shot machine learning methods, which mostly do not make effective use of prior knowledge. The hardest classes for CLIP also tend to be the hardest for humans, suggesting shared sources of difficulty — likely label noise and genuinely out-of-distribution or ambiguous images.</p>

<section id="sec-overlap" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-overlap" id="sec-overlap">Data overlap analysis</h2>
<p>A natural worry with a 400M-image internet-scraped pre-training set: some evaluation-set images might have leaked into training, inflating reported zero-shot accuracy. Rather than trying to scrub all possible overlaps up front (which would require knowing every future eval dataset in advance, and would make adding new evals expensive), the authors instead <strong>measured and quantified</strong> the effect:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li>Run a duplicate detector (detailed in the paper’s Appendix C) per eval dataset; manually tune a per-dataset similarity threshold for high precision.</li>
<li>Split each eval dataset into <strong>Overlap</strong> (near-duplicate found in training data) vs.&nbsp;<strong>Clean</strong> (no duplicate found) vs.&nbsp;<strong>All</strong> (unmodified).</li>
<li>Compute zero-shot RN50x64 accuracy on each split; report <strong>All − Clean</strong> as the estimated accuracy inflation from contamination, with binomial significance testing.</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Result:</strong> across 35 datasets, <strong>9 have zero detected overlap</strong> (synthetic/specialized datasets like MNIST, GTSRB, CLEVR, or ones guaranteed clean by publication date, like ObjectNet and Hateful Memes) — evidence the detector’s false-positive rate is low. <strong>Median overlap 2.2%, mean 3.2%.</strong> Overall accuracy is rarely shifted by more than <strong>0.1%</strong>; only 7 of 35 datasets exceed that, and only 2 remain statistically significant after Bonferroni correction. <strong>Largest single effect: +0.6% on Birdsnap</strong> (12.1% overlap). <strong>Largest overlap by volume: Country211 at 21.5%</strong> (because Country211 is built from YFCC100M, a subset of which is in WIT) — yet this only inflates accuracy by 0.2%, because the <em>accompanying text</em> of overlapping images rarely mentions the geo-localization-relevant information the eval actually measures. The authors note some confounds (e.g., Kinetics-700’s apparent −20% “overlap” effect turns out to be duplicate all-black transition frames, not genuine leakage) and conclude the effect of train/test overlap on CLIP’s headline numbers is <strong>small and mostly not statistically significant</strong>, consistent with similar analyses in Mahajan et al.&nbsp;(2018) and Kolesnikov et al.&nbsp;(2019).</p>
</section>
<section id="sec-limitations" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-limitations" id="sec-limitations">Limitations</h2>
<p>The paper is unusually candid about where CLIP falls short. Consolidated list:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Zero-shot CLIP is not close to overall SOTA.</strong> On datasets with training splits, it’s roughly competitive with a plain supervised linear classifier on ResNet-50 features — a baseline that, in 2021, was already well below task-specific state of the art. The authors estimate <strong>roughly a 1000× compute increase</strong> would be needed for zero-shot CLIP to reach overall SOTA — “infeasible to train with current hardware.”</li>
<li><strong>Weak on abstract/systematic tasks and fine-grained classification.</strong> Poor on counting (CLEVRCounts), distance estimation (KITTI Distance), and fine-grained categories like car models, aircraft variants, flower species — near-random on some novel tasks unlikely to be represented in the pre-training data.</li>
<li><strong>Poor generalization to truly out-of-distribution data</strong>, even when the <em>task</em> itself is one CLIP is otherwise good at. The canonical example: CLIP learns a strong OCR-like representation for digitally-rendered text (it does well on “Rendered SST2,” synthetic sentences turned into images), but only achieves <strong>88% on handwritten MNIST digits</strong> — a simple logistic regression on raw pixels beats it. Nearest-neighbor search confirms almost nothing resembling MNIST digits exists in CLIP’s training data. This is read as evidence CLIP does not solve deep learning’s brittle out-of-distribution generalization problem — it just tries to make more of the input space “in-distribution” by using a huge, varied dataset, which is a strategy that can still be violated (as MNIST shows).</li>
<li><strong>Limited to a closed set of concepts at inference time</strong>, unlike a genuinely generative approach (e.g., image captioning) that could produce novel outputs — but the authors found the generative/captioning baseline they tried far less compute-efficient (see the Approach and Experiments sections above), so this is a deliberate trade-off, not an oversight. Suggested future directions: jointly training a contrastive + generative objective, or searching over natural-language explanations at inference time (cf.&nbsp;Andreas et al., 2017).</li>
<li><strong>Doesn’t fix data inefficiency, just outruns it with data volume.</strong> If every one of the ~12.8 billion image-views seen across 32 training epochs were shown at one per second, that’s <strong>405 years</strong>. Combining CLIP with self-supervision/self-training methods is suggested as a route to genuine data-efficiency gains.</li>
<li><strong>Methodological caveats:</strong> despite the “zero-shot” framing, the authors repeatedly consulted full validation sets during development — an advantage a genuinely zero-shot deployment wouldn’t have. Also, the primary 27-dataset evaluation suite was assembled somewhat organically alongside CLIP’s own development, raising co-adaptation/selection-bias concerns; a purpose-built zero-shot benchmark is suggested as future work.</li>
<li><strong>Social bias</strong>, inherited from unfiltered, uncurated internet image–text pairs (see Broader Impacts, next section).</li>
<li><strong>Prompt sensitivity.</strong> Zero-shot accuracy can be sensitive to exact wording/phrasing of the class-description prompt, sometimes requiring “prompt engineering” trial and error to get right.</li>
<li><strong>Counter-intuitive zero-shot vs.&nbsp;few-shot drop.</strong> Because CLIP does not directly optimize for few-shot performance — the paper falls back to fitting linear probes on top of frozen CLIP features for few-shot evaluation — performance can <em>decrease</em> when moving from zero-shot to few-shot (adding a small number of labeled examples), the opposite of the human pattern documented above.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="sec-impacts" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-impacts" id="sec-impacts">Broader impacts</h2>
<p><em>(This section’s specifics are drawn primarily from OpenAI’s companion blog post for this paper, since the raw PDF/HTML extraction available in this environment was truncated before reaching the paper’s own Section 7 / Appendix F. The direction and substance are consistent with how this section of the paper is widely cited elsewhere.)</em></p>
<p>Because CLIP lets anyone define an arbitrary classifier just by naming categories in text, <strong>the choice of category labels itself becomes a source of model behavior and potential harm</strong> — there’s no dataset-curation step to catch it. Two studies illustrate this:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Bias probing with FairFace.</strong> When CLIP is given a label set that mixes FairFace demographic categories with a handful of pejorative terms (e.g., “criminal,” “animal”), it classified images of people aged <strong>0–20 into an egregious/derogatory category at a rate of ~32.3%</strong>. Simply <strong>adding “child” as an available class</strong> dropped that misclassification rate to <strong>~8.7%</strong> — a small change in the label vocabulary sharply changes model behavior, which the paper treats as both a demonstration of risk and a (partial, imperfect) mitigation.</li>
<li><strong>Surveillance-relevant capability testing.</strong> Since CLIP needs no task-specific training data, it can be pointed at sensitive tasks with unusually little friction. The authors tested this directly on <strong>celebrity identification</strong>: CLIP reaches <strong>59.2% top-1 accuracy</strong> picking the right identity out of 100 candidates, and <strong>43.3%</strong> out of 1,000 candidates. Framed against dedicated, production-grade facial recognition systems, this is not competitive — but the fact that a general-purpose, task-agnostic model gets non-trivial accuracy on an unrequested, ethically fraught task at all is presented as exactly the kind of risk that needs characterizing before deployment, not after.</li>
</ul>
<p>The paper’s own framing (echoed in the blog) is that flexibility is a double-edged sword: the same lack of a fixed, curated label taxonomy that makes CLIP broadly useful also means bias and misuse-potential show up <strong>wherever a user chooses to point the text encoder</strong>, rather than being contained by a dataset-curation process the model creators control. The authors present this as motivation for follow-on research into characterizing capabilities, shortcomings, and biases of this model class rather than as a solved problem.</p>
</section>
<section id="sec-related" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-related" id="sec-related">Related work</h2>
<p>CLIP sits at the intersection of three research threads the paper explicitly connects itself to:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Zero-data / zero-shot learning</strong>, dating back over a decade in computer vision as a way to generalize to unseen object categories (Larochelle et al., 2008; Lampert et al., 2009), and more recently reframed (as this paper does) as a way to measure task-learning rather than just category generalization — mirroring the shift GPT-2/GPT-3 made in NLP.</li>
<li><strong>Natural language as a flexible prediction space.</strong> Early proofs of concept: Socher et al.&nbsp;(2013, Stanford) trained a CIFAR-10 model to predict into a word-vector embedding space and showed it could name two never-seen classes; DeVISE (Frome et al., 2013) scaled a similar idea to ImageNet-scale fine-tuning. <strong>Visual N-Grams</strong> (Li et al., 2017) is singled out as the most direct ancestor — the first to attempt zero-shot transfer to <em>existing</em> image classification benchmarks using a generically pre-trained model, at 11.5% ImageNet accuracy.</li>
<li><strong>Modern architectures for text-image representation learning</strong>: VirTex (autoregressive captioning), ICMLM (masked language modeling), and ConVIRT (contrastive learning, in the medical-imaging domain) — all recent, small-scale (100–200K image) proofs of concept that CLIP scales up by roughly 2,000–4,000×.</li>
<li>More broadly, CLIP’s efficiency choices connect to the <strong>contrastive representation learning</strong> literature (SimCLR/Chen et al.&nbsp;2020a,b, MoCo/Chen et al.&nbsp;2020d, BYOL/Grill et al.&nbsp;2020) and to <strong>compute-scaling-law</strong> work (Hestness et al., 2017; Kaplan et al., 2020) that CLIP’s own Figure 9 scaling result echoes.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="sec-conclusion" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-conclusion" id="sec-conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>The paper’s own framing: task-agnostic, internet-scale pre-training — the recipe that drove NLP’s recent breakthroughs (GPT-family models) — <strong>transfers to computer vision</strong> when natural language is used as the supervisory signal instead of hand-curated labels. Like GPT models, CLIP picks up a surprisingly wide variety of tasks “for free” during pre-training (OCR, geo-localization, action recognition, and more), demonstrable purely via zero-shot transfer, with <strong>no task-specific fine-tuning</strong>. The finding that zero-shot evaluation appears to be a <strong>more representative</strong> measure of real-world model capability than in-distribution ImageNet benchmarking (because a zero-shot model cannot overfit to a benchmark it was never trained or tuned against) is presented as evidence that the field should weight zero-shot/broad-distribution evaluation more heavily going forward — not just for vision, but potentially, the authors suggest, for other modalities as well.</p>
</section>

<section id="sec-glossary" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="sec-glossary" id="sec-glossary">Glossary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Zero-shot transfer</strong> — applying a model to a task/dataset it was never trained or fine-tuned on, using only a natural-language description of the target categories.</li>
<li><strong>Contrastive objective</strong> — a training signal that rewards correctly <em>ranking</em> a matching pair above non-matching pairs, rather than reconstructing/predicting exact content (contrast with a generative/captioning objective).</li>
<li><strong>In-batch negatives</strong> — for a batch of <span class="math inline">\(N\)</span> pairs, every non-matching combination of image <span class="math inline">\(i\)</span> and text <span class="math inline">\(j \neq i\)</span> is treated as a negative example “for free,” without needing separately mined negatives.</li>
<li><strong>InfoNCE / N-pair loss</strong> — the family of contrastive losses (Sohn, 2016; Oord et al., 2018) that CLIP’s symmetric cross-entropy loss belongs to.</li>
<li><strong>Linear probe</strong> — freezing a pre-trained encoder and training only a linear (logistic regression) classifier on top of its output features, used here as the paper’s primary representation-learning evaluation method.</li>
<li><strong>Effective robustness vs.&nbsp;relative robustness</strong> (Taori et al., 2020) — effective robustness is accuracy gain under distribution shift <em>beyond</em> what’s predicted by the in-distribution/out-of-distribution accuracy relationship observed across many models; relative robustness is any accuracy gain at all.</li>
<li><strong>Hypernetwork</strong> (Ha et al., 2016) — a network that generates the <em>weights</em> of another network; CLIP’s text encoder functions as a hypernetwork generating a zero-shot linear classifier’s weight vector from a text description.</li>
<li><strong>Prompt engineering / ensembling</strong> — customizing (and averaging over multiple variants of) the natural-language template used to describe classes, to close the gap between the pre-training text distribution (full sentences) and the bare category names typically provided by classification datasets.</li>
</ul>
</section>
