Vision-Language-Action Models for Robotics: A Review Towards Real-World Applications

A Comprehensive Survey — IEEE Access 2025

Krishnatheja Vanka·June 28, 2026·22 min read

Amid growing efforts to leverage advances in large language models (LLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs) for robotics, Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have recently gained significant attention. By unifying vision, language, and action data at scale — modalities that have traditionally been studied separately — VLA models aim to learn policies that generalise across diverse tasks, objects, embodiments, and environments. Unlike previous surveys that focus narrowly on action representations or high-level model architectures, this work offers a comprehensive, full-stack review integrating both software and hardware components of VLA systems. The review covers architectural design strategies, modality-specific processing techniques, learning paradigms, data collection strategies, publicly available datasets, data augmentation methods, and evaluation benchmarks. Throughout, the paper aims to offer practical guidance for the robotics community in applying VLAs to real-world robotic systems.

Source paper: Kawaharazuka et al. (2025). Vision-Language-Action Models for Robotics: A Review Towards Real-World Applications. IEEE Access, 13, 162467–162504. DOI | arXiv:2510.07077 | Project website

Introduction

The last several years have witnessed a remarkable transformation in artificial intelligence driven by the emergence of large-scale pre-trained models. Large language models (LLMs) such as LLaMA 2 and GPT-4 have demonstrated an extraordinary capacity for language understanding, reasoning, and generation; while large vision-language models (VLMs) such as BLIP-2, PaLM-E, PaLI-X, and PaliGemma have extended these capabilities to the joint understanding of visual and linguistic information. These advances have catalysed a parallel revolution in robotics, where practitioners are increasingly asking: can we build generalist robots that understand natural language, perceive their environment richly, and produce flexible, reliable actions — all within a single unified model?

Early attempts to bring LLMs and VLMs into robotics decoupled reasoning from action. In these modular pipelines, a VLM would interpret an instruction and scene, then select from a library of hand-coded motion primitives or pre-trained skill policies. While effective for a narrow repertoire of predefined tasks, such systems were inherently limited: generalisation depended entirely on the breadth of the skill library, and any task outside that library required significant manual engineering.

To overcome these limitations, a growing body of research focuses on Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models — systems that jointly learn visual perception, language grounding, and robot action generation within a single end-to-end model. By training on large, diverse datasets spanning multiple tasks, environments, and robot embodiments, VLAs aim to produce generalist robot policies capable of handling novel instructions and scenes with minimal or no additional fine-tuning.

TipDefinition: Vision-Language-Action (VLA) Model

A VLA model is a system that takes visual observations and natural language instructions as required inputs — and may incorporate additional sensory modalities such as proprioception, depth, tactile signals, or audio — and produces robot actions by directly generating control commands. Systems in which a high-level policy merely selects an index from a pre-defined set of skills or primitives are excluded from this definition. The emphasis on direct control generation distinguishes VLAs from prior modular robot-learning architectures.

This survey provides a comprehensive, full-stack review of VLAs, integrating both software and hardware perspectives, and covering:

  • Strategy and architectural transition — from early CNN-based models through transformer-based and diffusion-based policies to modern hierarchical control frameworks.
  • Architectures and building blocks — core model classes (sensorimotor, world model, affordance-based) and modality-specific design choices.
  • Training paradigms — supervised, self-supervised, and reinforcement learning approaches, including pre-training and fine-tuning strategies.
  • Data ecosystems — robot platforms, data collection methods, publicly available datasets, augmentation methods, and evaluation benchmarks.
  • Practical guidance — recommendations for researchers seeking to deploy VLAs on real robotic systems.

Challenges

The construction of robust, deployable VLA models is constrained by several fundamental and interrelated challenges, spanning data availability, embodiment diversity, and computational cost.

Data Requirements and Scarcity

Training VLA models requires large-scale, diverse, and well-annotated datasets that simultaneously align visual observations, natural language instructions, and robot actions. In practice, however, datasets satisfying all three modalities are extremely limited in scale and diversity.

Vision-language datasets such as COCO Captions or web-scale corpora offer broad linguistic grounding but lack the action grounding that robotics requires. Conversely, robot demonstration datasets collected through teleoperation contain rich motor information but are typically confined to narrow task distributions with limited linguistic variability.

This mismatch creates two distinct bottlenecks. First, models pre-trained on vision-language internet data may not transfer effectively to robotic manipulation tasks due to the lack of motor grounding and the substantial domain gap between internet imagery and close-up, manipulator-centric robot views. Second, collecting high-quality robot demonstrations via teleoperation is expensive, physically demanding, and difficult to scale — challenges compounded when additional modalities (tactile, acoustic, 3D) are required.

WarningData Bottleneck

The scarcest and most expensive resource in VLA training is aligned multi-modal demonstration data: triplets of (visual observation, natural language instruction, robot action). Strategies including collaborative data collection (Open X-Embodiment), synthetic data generation, and latent action learning from unlabelled video are active areas of research to alleviate this bottleneck.

Embodiment Transfer

Robots exhibit enormous diversity in embodiment — some platforms consist solely of robotic arms; others incorporate wheels, legs, or complex bimanual configurations. Even among arm-based robots, joint configurations, link structures, sensor placements, and degrees of freedom vary significantly.

Transferring policies across embodiments remains a major open challenge. Each robot operates in a distinct action space and proprioceptive observation space reflecting its unique kinematics. A policy trained on a 7-DoF single-arm robot may be of limited value to a bimanual system or a legged robot without significant adaptation.

A related challenge lies in leveraging human motion data for robot training. Human video demonstrations offer an attractive alternative to expensive robot data collection, but they lack explicit action labels, and even inferred actions differ substantially from robot actions in both form and semantics.

Computational and Training Cost

Training VLA models entails considerable computational demands due to the high-dimensional, multi-modal nature of their inputs across long temporal horizons. Transformer-based architectures — which dominate current VLA design — scale poorly with respect to sequence length, amplifying memory and compute requirements.

At inference time, running large VLA models on resource-constrained robotic platforms poses additional challenges related to latency and memory. Many real-time robot controllers require inference at 10–50 Hz or higher, placing strict upper bounds on model complexity. These constraints motivate ongoing research into model compression, efficient architectures, distillation methods, and dedicated hardware.

VLA Design Strategy and Transition

The history of VLA model development reflects a steady architectural evolution driven by the twin goals of scalability and generalisation.

Early CNN-Based End-to-End Architectures

CLIPort (2022) is one of the earliest models to integrate CLIP with the Transporter Network for pick-and-place manipulation. It demonstrated the feasibility of leveraging a pre-trained VLM for robotic task grounding, but CNN/MLP-based architectures face fundamental limitations in unifying diverse modalities and scaling to large heterogeneous datasets.

Transformer-Based Sequence Models

Gato (DeepMind, 2022) is a generalist agent performing text chatting, VQA, image captioning, Atari gameplay, and robot control using a single shared decoder-only transformer — tokenizing language via SentencePiece and images via ViT. While demonstrating impressive breadth, Gato’s robotic capabilities were narrow (primarily block stacking).

VIMA (2022) proposed an encoder-decoder transformer for multi-modal task specifications combining text and goal images, with a frozen T5 encoder and a transformer decoder autoregressively generating discrete action tokens. Evaluation was confined to simulation.

Unified Real-World Policies with Pre-Trained VLMs

RT-1 (Google, 2022) marked a turning point — a real-time, general-purpose VLA trained on 130,000 episodes across 700 real-world tasks. It processes images through EfficientNet, conditions on language via FiLM with USE embeddings, compresses via TokenLearner, and non-autoregressively predicts all action dimensions in parallel.

RT-2 (2023) replaced the custom backbone with a large pre-trained VLM (PaLM-E 562B or PaLI-X 55B) and jointly fine-tuned on internet-scale vision-language tasks and robot demonstrations — demonstrating strong generalisation to novel objects and environments. This VLM-as-backbone design became the de facto standard.

RT-X (Open X-Embodiment, 2023) demonstrated that training on datasets from 20+ robot platforms yields a more general-purpose VLA than single-embodiment training.

RT-H introduced hierarchical policy structure — a high-level controller predicting symbolic “language motion” plans followed by a low-level controller generating robot actions — improving performance on long-horizon tasks.

OpenVLA (2024) emerged as the widely-adopted open-source counterpart, employing Prismatic VLM (LLaMA 2 7B) with DINOv2 and SigLIP visual encoders, fine-tuned on the full Open X-Embodiment dataset.

Diffusion Policy Integration

Octo (2024) introduced diffusion-based action generation into the VLA framework, appending a Diffusion Policy action head to a transformer encoder. Notable for its fully open-source implementation and flexible goal specification (language or goal image).

Diffusion Transformer Architectures

RDT-1B (2024) integrated the diffusion process directly into the transformer backbone — a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) — rather than treating it as an external action head. An Alternating Condition Injection strategy alternates between image and text tokens as cross-attention queries at successive layers.

Flow Matching Policy Architectures

π₀ (Physical Intelligence, 2024) built on PaliGemma (3B VLM) with a dedicated action expert using flow matching to generate continuous action chunks at up to 50 Hz. Generating entire action chunks in parallel (non-autoregressively) enables smooth, consistent real-time control for dexterous tasks including bimanual manipulation, cloth folding, and assembly.

Latent Action Learning from Video

LAPA (2024) trains a VQ-VAE to produce discrete latent action tokens from paired observation frames, enabling VLAs to learn from unlabelled human video data at internet scale. The latent action head is subsequently replaced and fine-tuned to produce actual robot control commands.

Hierarchical Policy Architectures

π₀.₅ (2025) combines a FAST (Frequency-based Action Sequence Tokenisation) high-level action token generator — applying Discrete Cosine Transform along the temporal axis to compress action sequences — with a flow-matching low-level controller.

GR00T N1 (NVIDIA, 2025) integrates LAPA-style latent action pre-training, RDT-1B-style diffusion generation, and π₀-style flow-matching control into a multi-stage hierarchical policy generalising across robot platforms.

NoteArchitectural Trend

The field has moved from flat sensorimotor models toward hierarchical “System 1 + System 2” designs: a slow, reasoning-capable VLM high-level policy coupled to a fast, continuous low-level motor controller. This mirrors the dual-process theory of cognition and is increasingly the dominant design pattern.

Architectures and Building Blocks

Sensorimotor Models

Sensorimotor models are the most prevalent VLA class, directly mapping perception and instruction to control commands. Seven distinct architectural variants are documented:

TipArchitecture Selection Heuristic

For most single-robot manipulation deployments, architectures 5 or 6 (VLM + diffusion/flow-matching head) offer the best balance of semantic understanding, continuous action quality, and real-time capability. For multi-robot or long-horizon deployments, architecture 7 (hierarchical VLM + Diffusion Transformer) is increasingly preferred.

World Models

World models predict future observations or latent representations, enabling planning and the exploitation of unlabelled video data. Three design patterns appear in the literature:

Pattern 1 — Action Generation via World Models. Predict future images/video (UniPi, DreamGen, SuSIE), optical flow (AVDC), or feature point trajectories (ATM, Track2Act), then use an inverse dynamics model (IDM) to extract robot actions. Embodiment-agnostic intermediate representations (optical flow, feature points) are particularly valuable for cross-embodiment transfer.

Pattern 2 — Latent Action Generation. Train a VQ-VAE to produce discrete latent action tokens from paired observation frames without explicit action labels, enabling training on internet-scale human video. LAPA, Moto, UniVLA, and UniSkill demonstrate this approach. Used for pre-training in GR00T N1 and DreamGen.

Pattern 3 — Implicit World Model. Train VLAs with auxiliary objectives requiring joint prediction of actions and future observations. GR-1, GR-2, GR-3, 3D-VLA, and WorldVLA adopt this pattern, showing that future frame prediction substantially improves task success rates.

Affordance-Based Models

Affordances denote the actionable properties of objects or scenes relative to the robot’s embodiment. Three design patterns:

  • VLM-predicted affordances: VoxPoser (affordance + constraint maps via GPT-4, OWL-ViT, SAM), LERF-TOGO (CLIP/DINO NeRF for language-grounded 3D affordances), KAGI (GPT-4o keypoint sequences for RL guidance).
  • Affordances from human datasets: VRB (contact points + hand trajectories from EPIC-KITCHENS), HRP (Ego4D affordance labels), VidBot (3D affordances for zero-shot robot deployment).
  • Integrated affordance + action prediction: CLIPort, RoboPoint, RoboGround, Chain-of-Affordance (autoregressive affordance chain: object position → grasp point → placement location → action).

Data Modalities

Vision

Visual token compression: Perceiver Resampler (Flamingo), Q-Former (BLIP-2), QT-Former (temporal Q-Former), TokenLearner (learnable spatial summarisation).

Language

Language tokenization is inherited from the LLM backbone (T5, LLaMA, Gemma, Qwen2, Phi-2, SmolLM2). Fusion with visual features is accomplished via FiLM conditioning (affine visual feature transformation conditioned on language) or unified token-sequence processing through a shared transformer.

Action

Four primary action representation strategies:

  1. Discretized tokens via binning — 256 bins per action dimension, cross-entropy loss; autoregressive (RT-2, OpenVLA) or non-autoregressive (RT-1). FAST compresses token length via Discrete Cosine Transform + BPE for faster inference.
  2. MLP decoding of continuous actions — L1/L2 regression, with optional GMM or LSTM heads; multi-step action chunking (OpenVLA-OFT) improves trajectory smoothness.
  3. Diffusion / flow matching — Non-autoregressive generation of continuous action chunks; flow matching (π₀) requires fewer denoising steps than diffusion, enabling ≥50 Hz control.
  4. Latent action representations — Discrete tokens learned from unlabelled video (LAPA, UniVLA); action head replaced and fine-tuned for robot control post-training.
NoteCross-Embodiment Action Representation

The Open X-Embodiment project standardised a 7-DoF action format (xyz + Euler angles + gripper) across 20+ robot platforms. CrossFormer uses embodiment-specific action heads atop a shared transformer. UniAct defines a Universal Action Space (UAS) as a shared discrete codebook with embodiment-specific continuous decoders.

Miscellaneous Modalities

  • Audio: Mel-spectrogram → ResNet / AST / Whisper encoder (Unified-IO 2, FuSe, SOLAMI). Simple alternative: ASR-based text conversion (RoboNurse-VLA).
  • Tactile sensing: Image-based tactile sensors (DIGIT, GelStereo) encoded via ViT or TVL models (FuSe, TLA, Tactile-VLA). 6-axis force-torque via MoE fusion (ForceVLA).
  • 3D information: Depth images (SpatialVLA’s Ego3D encoding via ZoeDepth + sinusoidal PE); multi-view RGB-D (GO-1, Evo-0 via VGGT); voxel representations (3D-LADA, VoxAct-B); point clouds (3D-VLA, PointVLA, DexVLA via PointNet++).

Emerging Techniques

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning: ECoT and CoVLA generate intermediate reasoning traces before actions; CoT-VLA uses visual subgoal images as intermediate representations. Both approaches improve multi-step task performance.

Mixture of Experts (MoE): ForceVLA and ChatVLA dynamically route to specialised action sub-networks based on task phase (contact vs. free-space), providing task-adaptive control without linear compute scaling.

Continual learning: iManip incrementally adds learnable action prompts to a frozen VLA backbone, enabling new skill acquisition without catastrophic forgetting.

Training Strategy and Implementation

Supervised Learning (Imitation Learning)

Behaviour cloning (BC) on expert demonstrations is the dominant VLA training paradigm. Loss functions depend on the action representation: cross-entropy for discrete tokens; L1/L2 for continuous MLP-decoded actions; denoising score matching for diffusion/flow-matching heads.

Self-Supervised Learning

Self-supervised objectives exploit unannotated data — particularly human video — for VLA pre-training. Key approaches include:

  • World model pre-training: Predict future observations from current observation and action/latent action (GR-1 on Ego4D, LAPA).
  • Contrastive pre-training: CLIP/SigLIP alignment of visual encoders to language semantics.
  • Masked prediction: MAE-style pre-training of visual encoders for spatially rich representations.

Reinforcement Learning

Online RL can push VLA performance beyond the demonstration-quality ceiling but faces high sample complexity and requires safe exploration. VLM-as-reward (KAGI uses GPT-4o for reward-shaping keypoints), sim-to-real transfer, and preference-based RLHF are active directions.

Training Stages

Pre-training stages:

  1. VLM backbone pre-training on internet-scale data (PaLM-E, PaliGemma, Prismatic).
  2. Cross-embodiment robot pre-training on Open X-Embodiment (700+ tasks, 20+ robots).
  3. Human video pre-training (Ego4D, Something-Something) for world model / latent action learning.

Fine-tuning strategies:

Inference Optimisation

  • Action chunking: Predict multiple future steps simultaneously; reduces inference call frequency and smooths trajectories.
  • Temporal ensembling: Average overlapping action chunk predictions for further smoothing.
  • FAST tokenisation: DCT + BPE compression of action sequences; dramatically reduces token length and improves control frequency.
  • Model quantisation / distillation: Enable on-robot inference at embedded hardware resource budgets.
TipInference Frequency Guidelines

Flow-matching heads (π₀): up to 50 Hz. Diffusion heads (Octo): ~10–25 Hz. Discrete token autoregressive (RT-2): typically 1–3 Hz, depending on model size. FAST tokenisation can bring autoregressive models to competitive frequencies.

Datasets

Data Collection Methods

Major Public Datasets

Open X-Embodiment (OXE): The largest multi-robot dataset, aggregating data from 20+ robot platforms across 100+ tasks. Standardised around 7-DoF actions and single-camera RGB observations (224×224). Primary pre-training corpus for OpenVLA, Octo, and RT-X.

DROID: Large-scale Franka Panda manipulation dataset across 86 distinct environments and 564 scenes, emphasising visual diversity. Used in OpenVLA and π₀ training.

Bridge Data (v1/v2): 60,000+ WidowX arm demonstrations of kitchen/tabletop tasks with language instruction labels; designed for embodiment-focused diversity studies.

Something-Something / Ego4D: Large-scale egocentric human video datasets used for world model pre-training (GR-1, LAPA) to bootstrap temporal dynamics representations without action labels.

LEROBOT Dataset: Community-curated Hugging Face dataset supporting standardised loading, augmentation, and evaluation for robot learning.

Data Augmentation

NoteKey Augmentation Strategies
  • Photometric augmentations (random crop, color jitter, blur) — universal baseline.
  • Domain randomisation (background textures, lighting, distractor objects) — improves sim-to-real and lab-to-lab transfer.
  • Robot-agnostic augmentation — erasing the robot arm from demonstrations to reduce robot-appearance dependence.
  • Synthetic data generation — ROSIE (InstructPix2Pix background augmentation), GenAug (diffusion-model scene augmentation), RoboDreamer (text-to-video synthetic demonstrations).
  • Language augmentation — LLM-based instruction paraphrasing to increase linguistic diversity.

Review of Real-World Robot Applications

Robot Platforms for VLA

Single-arm manipulators: Franka Panda (most widely used, torque-sensing), WidowX 250, UR5/UR10, Everyday Robots.

Bimanual systems: ALOHA (ViperX + WidowX leader-follower teleoperation) — the dominant platform for dexterous bimanual research.

Mobile manipulators: HelloRobot Stretch, Spot with arm attachment.

Legged robots: ANYmal, Spot, Unitree, Agility Robotics Digit.

Humanoid robots: Figure 01, Fourier Intelligence GR-1, 1X NEO, Unitree H1. GR00T N1 is designed explicitly for humanoid generalisation.

Evaluation Benchmarks

WarningEvaluation Standardisation Gap

The field currently lacks a universal, reproducible VLA benchmark comparable to ImageNet for visual recognition. Simulation and real-world evaluations use incompatible metrics, task definitions, and success criteria, making cross-paper comparisons unreliable. SimplerEnv is a notable recent effort toward standardisation.

Simulation:

Real-world evaluation: Task success rate (typically 20–50 trials per condition) across instruction phrasings, novel objects, and visual distractors. Standardisation remains limited; per-lab task definitions dominate.

Real-World Application Domains

Household manipulation: Pick-and-place, rearrangement, drawer manipulation, dish washing, laundry folding, kitchen tasks. π₀ and GR00T N1 represent the current capability frontier for dexterous household tasks.

Industrial assembly and manufacturing: Precise insertion, screwing, and part alignment tasks, incorporating force-torque sensing (ForceVLA) and tactile feedback (Tactile-VLA, TLA).

Healthcare and assistive robotics: Medication delivery (RoboNurse-VLA), physical rehabilitation, social interaction in clinical settings.

Field robotics and logistics: Warehouse navigation, agricultural manipulation, disaster response — requiring long-horizon spatial reasoning over large-scale, unstructured environments.

Humanoid whole-body control: GR00T N1 and related systems target full humanoid control integrating locomotion and manipulation across dozens of joints.

Recommendations for Practitioners

TipArchitecture Selection

For most single-robot manipulation tasks, a VLM + Flow Matching Action Head (architecture 6, e.g., π₀-style) offers the best balance of semantic understanding, smooth control, and real-time capability. For long-horizon or multi-task deployments, consider a hierarchical VLM + Diffusion Transformer (architecture 7, e.g., GR00T N1-style). For resource-constrained on-robot inference, use smaller VLM backbones (SmolVLA, TinyVLA) with FAST tokenisation.

TipData Strategy
  • Start from a publicly available VLA checkpoint pre-trained on OXE (OpenVLA, Octo, π₀) to benefit from cross-embodiment transfer.
  • Collect task-specific demonstrations via teleoperation (ALOHA, GELLO) for post-training fine-tuning.
  • Apply visual augmentation aggressively and paraphrase instructions via LLMs.
  • Aim for at least 100–500 demonstrations per task for reliable fine-tuning.
TipTraining Pipeline
  • Use LoRA for memory-efficient adaptation of large VLM backbones.
  • Apply action chunking with temporal ensembling during inference.
  • Validate in simulation (SimplerEnv, LIBERO) before hardware deployment.
  • Profile inference latency on target hardware early in the development cycle.
WarningDeployment Safety
  • Implement hard safety monitors (joint limit checks, Cartesian workspace bounds, force thresholds) independent of the VLA.
  • Deploy with a human-in-the-loop for initial trials; collect failure trajectories for targeted fine-tuning.
  • For near-human deployments (healthcare, collaborative manufacturing), require formal safety certification independent of VLA behaviour.

Future Research Directions

Data Modality

Integration of additional sensing modalities (tactile, acoustic, 3D) remains an active frontier. Collecting and annotating multi-modal datasets at scale is an open challenge; synthetic generation from physically realistic simulators is a promising path for tactile and force data.

Reasoning

Current VLAs excel at reactive visuomotor control but struggle with extended multi-step reasoning, spatial planning, and causal inference. Integrating chain-of-thought reasoning (ECoT, CoT-VLA) and external symbolic planners with VLA action generation is an important research direction.

Continual Learning

Real-world robots must acquire new skills over time without forgetting prior capabilities. Continual learning methods tailored to VLAs (iManip-style incremental prompts, parameter-efficient expansion) are nascent but critical for long-term deployability.

Reinforcement Learning

Online RL can push performance beyond demonstration-quality ceilings. Key research challenges include: computational cost of online RL with large models; safe real-world exploration; VLM-as-reward for open-ended task evaluation; and efficient sim-to-real transfer.

Safety

VLAs in human environments introduce safety requirements that current models do not adequately address. Formal safety constraints (control barrier functions, safety filters), uncertainty quantification, and out-of-distribution detection are critical for responsible deployment.

Failure Detection and Recovery

Real tasks frequently encounter unexpected failures (object slippage, plan infeasibility, environmental change). Research into failure detection via world models, graceful degradation, and recovery policy learning is urgently needed for reliable real-world deployment.

Evaluation

A community-endorsed evaluation framework — covering diverse tasks, embodiments, and environmental conditions with standardised success criteria — is a critical infrastructure need. SimplerEnv is a step in this direction but covers only a subset of the full deployment space.

Applications

VLAs are beginning to enter domains beyond tabletop manipulation: humanoid whole-body control, outdoor field robotics, healthcare, and construction — each introducing unique requirements that current architectures do not fully address.

Conclusion

Vision-Language-Action models represent a paradigm shift in robot learning — from narrow, task-specific controllers to generalist policies that unify perception, language grounding, and motor control within a single scalable model. The field has progressed rapidly from early CNN-based proofs of concept (CLIPort) through transformer-based unified policies (RT-1, RT-2, OpenVLA) to diffusion and flow-matching architectures (Octo, RDT-1B, π₀) and sophisticated hierarchical control systems (π₀.₅, GR00T N1).

This survey has provided a comprehensive, full-stack review of VLAs, covering a formal definition and taxonomy of VLA model classes; the principal architectural patterns and their evolutionary trajectory; modality-specific processing choices for vision, language, action, and auxiliary inputs; training paradigms from imitation learning and self-supervised world model pre-training to online reinforcement learning; the ecosystem of robot platforms, data collection methods, publicly available datasets, and evaluation benchmarks; practical guidance for deploying VLAs on real robotic systems; and open challenges and future research directions spanning data, reasoning, safety, and evaluation.

NoteOpen Challenges Summary

Despite substantial progress, VLAs remain far from a truly generalist robot policy capable of reliably performing arbitrary tasks in unstructured human environments. The most pressing open challenges are: (1) scalable, diverse, aligned multi-modal data; (2) reliable cross-embodiment transfer; (3) safety guarantees and failure recovery; (4) standardised evaluation; and (5) efficient on-robot inference. The trajectory of the field is unmistakably upward, and VLAs are demonstrating capabilities in household, industrial, and humanoid domains that would have seemed unreachable only a few years ago.
